CHAPTER 4
The Blood That Binds

The pounding on the door was relentless, each strike of knuckles against wood like a hammer to Nesta’s skull. She groaned, rolling onto her side, the thin mattress of the Illyrian hut doing little to cushion the ache in her bones. The sound came again—harder, more insistent.

“Open the door, Archeron.”

Devlon.

Nesta’s eyes snapped open. The hut was dark, the embers in the hearth long since burned to ash. She sat up, the rough wool of her blanket scraping against her skin. The pounding continued, each beat a demand.

“Now.”

She swung her legs over the side of the bed, her bare feet touching the cold dirt floor. The gray gown she’d worn to sleep was wrinkled, the hem damp from the night’s chill. She didn’t bother smoothing it as she strode to the door and yanked it open.

Devlon stood on the threshold, his broad frame nearly filling the doorway. His dark eyes were unreadable, his mouth a thin, grim line. Behind him, the training grounds were still shrouded in predawn gloom, the peaks of the mountains lost in the mist.

“What?” Nesta demanded, her voice rough with sleep.

Devlon didn’t waste words. “Bellius has accused you of attacking him unprovoked. You’re to appear before the warrior council at dawn.”

The words hit her like a physical blow. Her stomach twisted, but she kept her face blank, her chin lifted. “And if I refuse?”

Devlon’s lip curled. “Then you’ll be dragged before them in chains. Your choice.”

Nesta bared her teeth. “I didn’t touch him.”

“That’s not what he says.” Devlon’s gaze flicked over her, assessing. “You’ve made enemies here, Archeron. Bellius is well-connected. His word carries weight.”

Nesta’s fingers curled into fists at her sides. She could feel the weight of the accusation pressing down on her, the threat of exile—or worse—hanging in the air. But she refused to let him see her fear.

“Fine,” she said, her voice steady. “I’ll be there.”

Devlon studied her for a long moment, as if waiting for her to break. When she didn’t, he gave a sharp nod. “Don’t be late.” He turned on his heel and strode away, his boots kicking up dust in his wake.

Nesta shut the door, leaning against it as the silence of the hut settled around her. Her pulse pounded in her ears, her breath coming too fast. She pressed a hand to her stomach, as if that could still the nausea coiling inside her.

The warrior council. She’d heard stories of what happened to those who stood before them—exile, punishment, sometimes worse. And Bellius’s word against hers? She had no allies here. No one who would speak for her.

She pushed away from the door, her legs unsteady as she crossed the hut to the small table where a basin of water sat. She splashed her face, the cold shock doing little to clear her mind. The reflection that stared back at her was hollow-eyed, her cheekbones sharper than they’d been weeks ago. She looked like a ghost of herself.

Outside, the first light of dawn crept over the peaks, painting the sky in shades of gray and gold. But the day felt heavier than the night, the weight of what was to come pressing down on her like a shroud. She dressed quickly, her movements mechanical, her mind racing.

By the time she stepped out of the hut, the training grounds were stirring to life. Warriors moved between the huts, their voices low, their eyes flicking toward her with curiosity and suspicion. She ignored them, her spine straight, her chin high.

The warrior council awaited. And Nesta would face them alone.

---

The fire pit hissed and spat as Nesta stepped into the circle of warriors, their faces half-lit by the flickering flames. The scent of burning pine and damp earth filled her nose, the cold mountain air biting at her cheeks. She kept her hands loose at her sides, though every instinct screamed to clench them into fists.

Cassian stood at the edge of the circle, arms crossed over his chest, his wings half-furled. His Siphons glowed faintly in the dim light, crimson against the dark leather of his gloves. His expression was unreadable, but his jaw was tight, the muscle there twitching.

Devlon sat on a low stone bench, his weathered face set in a scowl. "Explain yourself, Nesta Archeron."

She didn’t flinch at the use of her full name. Instead, she lifted her chin higher, meeting his gaze head-on. "Bellius attacked me during training. I defended myself."

Murmurs rippled through the gathered warriors. Bellius, his thigh wrapped in a bloodied bandage, smirked from where he leaned against a post. "She’s lying. I barely touched her, and she came at me with a dagger."

Nesta’s fingers twitched. "You grabbed me. Threw me to the ground. If I hadn’t fought back, you would’ve done worse."

Cassian’s wings shifted, a subtle movement, but his voice cut through the noise like a blade. "She’s telling the truth." His gaze flicked to Bellius, cold and unyielding. "You provoked her. And she acted in self-defense."

Devlon’s eyes narrowed. "You weren’t there, Cassian. You don’t know what happened."

"I know Bellius," Cassian shot back, his voice low and dangerous. "And I know Nesta."

The warriors fell silent, their attention shifting between the two males. Nesta’s chest tightened at the weight of Cassian’s defense, but she forced herself to keep her expression neutral. She didn’t need his protection. Didn’t want it.

Devlon exhaled sharply. "Even if that’s true, she used a weapon. Against one of our own."

"She’s not one of your own," Bellius sneered. "She’s a High Lady’s sister. A weakling who doesn’t belong here."

Nesta’s teeth bared. "I’m not weak."

Cassian’s Siphons flared brighter, but he didn’t look at her. "She’s here because she was ordered to be. And if she’s going to survive this, she needs to learn how to fight."

A few warriors muttered in agreement, but others shook their heads, their faces hardening. Devlon rubbed his temples. "This isn’t a debate. The rules are clear—no weapons in training. She broke them."

Kars, a broad-shouldered warrior seated at the back, cleared his throat. "She was defending herself. If it had been any of us, we would’ve done the same."

Devlon’s glare could’ve frozen fire. "Stay out of this, Kars."

Kars didn’t back down. "She’s not the first to break the rules, and she won’t be the last. But she’s not the one who started this fight."

The circle erupted into argument, voices clashing like steel on steel. Nesta stood in the center of it, her pulse roaring in her ears. She didn’t care about their verdict. She only cared about the dagger at Cassian’s belt—the one she’d stolen from the armory. The one that had saved her.

Devlon raised a hand, silencing the crowd. "Enough." His gaze locked onto Nesta. "You will not be exiled. But you are forbidden from carrying a weapon during training. And Bellius"—his voice hardened—"you will stay away from her. Understood?"

Bellius’s smirk didn’t waver. "Of course."

Devlon dismissed the gathering with a wave of his hand, and the warriors dispersed, their voices fading into the night. Nesta didn’t move. Neither did Cassian.

For a heartbeat, they just stood there, the firelight casting long shadows between them. Then his eyes flicked to the dagger at his belt. He knew.

She didn’t look away. Neither did he.

---

The training grounds were a cacophony of clanging steel and grunts of exertion, the crisp mountain air biting at Nesta’s cheeks as she stepped onto the packed earth. The warriors paused mid-swing, their gazes snapping to her like hounds scenting prey. She ignored them, her boots kicking up dust as she strode toward the center of the field.

Cassian fell into step beside her, his wings rustling with irritation. “You’re not carrying a weapon.”

Nesta didn’t glance at him. “I don’t need one.”

A low chuckle rippled through the watching Illyrians. Cassian’s jaw tightened, his Siphons flaring crimson. “This isn’t a game, Nesta.”

“Wasn’t it you who said I had to train?” She stopped, turning to face him. His dark eyes were unreadable, but the muscle ticking in his cheek betrayed his frustration. “Or was that just another lie to get me out of Velaris?”

Cassian exhaled sharply through his nose. “You think I wanted this? To drag you up here and watch you get torn apart?”

“Then why do it?” The words came out colder than she intended, but she didn’t take them back.

“Because I’m not in the habit of letting my warriors get killed.” His voice was a whip-crack, low enough that only she could hear. “Even the ones who don’t want to be here.”

Nesta bared her teeth. “I never asked for your protection.”

“No,” he agreed, stepping closer. “You never ask for anything. That’s the problem.”

Before she could retort, Devlon’s voice cut through the tension. “Enough chatter!” The grizzled warrior strode toward them, his scarred face split in a grin that didn’t reach his eyes. “Today’s training is a mock battle. And since our newest recruit seems so eager to prove herself…” He gestured to Nesta with a mocking flourish. “She’ll be on the losing side.”

The warriors erupted into laughter, their earlier wariness replaced by smug anticipation. Nesta didn’t react, her pulse steady despite the heat creeping up her neck.

Devlon clapped his hands. “Positions!”

The warriors scrambled to form ranks, the stronger fighters taking the front lines while the less experienced fell back. Nesta was left standing alone, the opposing force a wall of muscle and steel.

Bellius stepped forward, his grin wide and vicious. “No weapons, little High Lady?” He flexed his fingers, claws glinting in the sunlight. “How generous.”

Nesta cracked her knuckles, her voice a whisper. “Let’s see how well you fight without a weapon.”

---

The training grounds were a churning sea of dust and muscle, the air thick with the scent of sweat and iron. Nesta stood at the center, her boots planted in the dirt, her breath steady despite the weight pressing down on her chest. Around her, the Illyrian warriors shifted, their wings rustling like dry leaves in the wind. Their laughter had faded to silence, replaced by the kind of focus that came before a storm.

Cassian watched from the sidelines, his arms crossed over his chest, his crimson Siphons glinting in the morning light. His face was unreadable, but his eyes—those dark, knowing eyes—were fixed on her. She refused to look at him.

Devlon stood opposite her, his smirk sharp enough to cut. "Ready to lose, girl?"

Nesta didn’t answer. She didn’t need to.

The signal came—a sharp whistle—and the world exploded into motion.

The first warrior lunged, his sword a blur of steel. Nesta twisted, her body moving before her mind could catch up. The blade grazed her side, a hot line of pain, but she was already spinning, her foot lashing out to catch his knee. He grunted, stumbling, and she didn’t hesitate. A fist to his ribs, a shove to send him sprawling. The crowd murmured, but she didn’t stop.

Another came at her, then another. She dodged, blocked, struck. The terrain was hers now—dips in the earth, rocks jutting from the ground, the uneven footing that had once tripped her up. She used it all. A warrior’s boot caught on a loose stone, and she was there, driving her elbow into his throat before he could recover. He gasped, doubling over, and she was already moving.

Bellius snarled, his face twisted with frustration. "You’re just lucky," he spat, circling her.

Nesta bared her teeth. "Luck has nothing to do with it."

He lunged.

She sidestepped, her body coiled like a spring. His momentum carried him past her, and she drove her fist into his side. Bone cracked beneath her knuckles, and he went down hard, wheezing. The crowd fell silent.

For a heartbeat, there was nothing but the sound of her own breathing, the pounding of her heart. Then—

A low whistle cut through the stillness.

Nesta turned, her chest heaving, to see Kars standing at the edge of the field. His eyes were wide, his mouth slightly open. "That was… impressive."

She didn’t look at him. "It was survival."

The warriors around her shifted, their expressions no longer mocking. Some looked away, others studied her with newfound wariness. Devlon’s smirk had vanished, replaced by something colder, more calculating.

Cassian’s voice cut through the tension, rough with something she didn’t dare name. "That’s enough for today."

Nesta didn’t wait for the others to disperse. She turned on her heel and walked away, her body aching with every step. The stares of the warriors burned into her back, but she didn’t turn around.

She reached the edge of the training grounds, where the earth gave way to the steep drop of the mountainside. The wind whipped at her braid, tugging strands loose. She flexed her hands, the bruises on her knuckles darkening with every passing second.

For the first time since arriving, she felt something other than numbness.

A spark.

Hot. Bright.

Defiance.

---

The hut smelled of damp wood and sweat. Nesta’s sweat. The kind that came from pushing a body beyond its limits, from forcing it to move when every instinct screamed to stop. She had done it—again and again—until her muscles trembled like overstretched wire, until her lungs burned with each ragged breath.

She collapsed onto the pallet, her body too heavy to hold itself upright any longer. The rough wool of the blanket scratched against her skin, but she didn’t care. She didn’t care about anything except the way the world seemed to tilt beneath her, the way her vision blurred at the edges.

The door creaked open.

She didn’t need to look to know it was him. The scent of leather and wind and something darker, something like iron and fire, filled the small space. Cassian. Of course it was Cassian.

A soft *thud* as something landed on the table. She didn’t turn her head. Didn’t acknowledge him.

“You’re bleeding.”

His voice was low, rough. Not the usual bark of commands, not the teasing lilt he sometimes used when he thought she wasn’t listening. This was different. Quieter.

She still didn’t look at him. “I don’t need your pity.”

Silence. Then the sound of his boots against the floor, slow and deliberate. The pallet dipped beneath his weight as he sat on the edge of it, his wings brushing against her leg. The touch was light, barely there, but it sent a jolt through her.

“It’s not pity.” His voice was closer now. “It’s strategy. You won’t last long if you’re too injured to fight.”

She turned her head then, meeting his gaze. His dark eyes were unreadable, his jaw tight. She bared her teeth. “Why do you care?”

For a moment, he didn’t answer. His throat worked, his fingers flexing against his thighs. Then, quietly, “Because I see myself in you.”

She scoffed, but the sound lacked its usual bite. “You? The great Illyrian warrior? The general who commands armies?”

His mouth twisted. “You think I was born with wings and a sword in hand?” He leaned forward, his voice dropping. “I was the bastard son of an Illyrian whore. The warriors spat at my feet before they ever called me brother. I fought for every scrap of respect, every inch of ground. I know what it’s like to be hated. To be told you don’t belong.”

Nesta’s breath caught. She had never heard him speak like this. Never seen him like this—raw, unguarded. The words hung between them, heavy and unfamiliar.

Then, just as quickly as it had come, the moment shattered. Cassian stood abruptly, his wings flaring slightly as if shaking off the weight of his own confession. His voice returned to its usual gruffness. “Get some rest. Tomorrow, we start again.”

He didn’t wait for her reply. The door clicked shut behind him, leaving her alone in the silence.

The pouch of herbs sat untouched on the table. She stared at it, her fingers curling into the blanket beneath her.

Outside, the wind howled through the mountains, a mournful sound that echoed the emptiness inside her. But for the first time, the silence didn’t feel so vast.

She closed her eyes, Cassian’s words echoing in her mind.

*I see myself in you.*

And for the first time, she wondered if she wasn’t as alone as she thought.
