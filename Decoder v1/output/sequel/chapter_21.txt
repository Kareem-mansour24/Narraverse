CHAPTER 21
The Storm Before the Silence

The first crack of thunder rolled through the mountains like a warning.

Nesta didn’t need the storm to tell her something was wrong. She’d woken to the same unease that had been clinging to the camp for days—whispers that died when she passed, eyes that slid away from hers. The warriors had never been friendly, but now they looked at her like she was already a corpse.

She sat up in the narrow cot, the thin wool blanket pooling at her waist. The tent was cold, the damp seeping through the canvas. Outside, the wind howled, carrying the scent of rain and pine and something sharper—fear.

Cassian was gone.

Devlon had sent him on some reconnaissance mission at dawn, scouting the cliffs for signs of the assassin. As if she needed protecting. As if she hadn’t spent the last two months learning exactly how to kill a man in a dozen different ways.

Nesta dressed quickly, pulling on the gray gown that had become her uniform here. It was immaculate, as always—she refused to let the grime of this place touch her. The braided coronet she wove into her hair was tight enough to make her scalp ache, but she didn’t loosen it. She wouldn’t give them the satisfaction.

The training yard was already crowded when she stepped outside, the warriors moving through their drills with forced precision. No one met her eyes. No one spoke to her.

She picked up a pair of wooden practice swords and began her own routine, the familiar weight of the blades grounding her. The movements were sharp, precise—each strike a release for the hollow ache inside her chest. She hadn’t felt anything in months, not really. Not since the war. Not since she’d watched her sister die and come back different. Not since she’d realized that no amount of screaming or breaking things would fill the void.

But the anger was still there. It simmered beneath her skin, a constant companion.

A shadow fell over her.

Nesta didn’t pause. She pivoted, bringing the sword up in a swift arc that stopped just shy of Kars’s throat.

The warrior didn’t flinch. His dark eyes were grim, his mouth a thin line. “Bellius is rallying his men.”

Her fingers tightened around the hilt. “When?”

Kars shook his head. “Soon. Before Cassian returns.”

The words settled over her like the storm clouds above, heavy and suffocating. She’d known this was coming. Bellius and his faction had been pushing for weeks, testing her, challenging her. And now they were done waiting.

Nesta lowered the sword. “How many?”

“Enough.”

She bared her teeth. “That’s not an answer.”

Kars didn’t back down. “Enough to make sure it’s over quickly.”

Her breath came faster, the hollow space inside her filling with something dark and jagged. She’d spent her life being told what to do, where to go, how to behave. She’d let them push her, let them break her, let them turn her into this—this nothing.

No more.

Nesta exhaled sharply. “Tell them I’m waiting.”

Kars studied her for a long moment before nodding once. Then he turned and melted into the crowd of warriors, leaving her standing alone in the center of the yard.

The first drops of rain began to fall.

Nesta’s hands clenched into fists at her sides.

---

The rain fell in fat, heavy drops, slicking Nesta’s braided coronet to her scalp. She didn’t bother wiping the water from her face as she strode toward the command tent, her gray gown clinging to her legs with every step. The fabric was immaculate—she had made sure of it—but the mud of the training yard splattered her boots regardless. Let it. Let them see her walk through their filth and not care.

The flap of the tent was tied back, revealing Devlon inside, bent over a map spread across a wooden table. He didn’t look up as she entered, though the shift in the air must have told him someone had come. The scent of damp wool and iron filled the space, the latter clinging to him like a second skin.

“Where is he?” Nesta demanded, her voice cutting through the patter of rain on canvas.

Devlon’s fingers stilled on the map. Slowly, deliberately, he lifted his gaze. His dark eyes were flat, assessing. “Who?”

She bared her teeth. “Don’t play games with me. Cassian.”

A muscle feathered in Devlon’s jaw. “The General has duties elsewhere.”

“Bullshit.” The word snapped out of her, sharp as a whip. “He was supposed to be here. With me.”

Devlon straightened, his broad frame blocking the light from the lantern behind him. “You’re not one of us, girl. You don’t get to demand answers.”

Nesta’s fingers curled into invisible claws at her sides. “I’m not asking for your permission. I’m telling you to tell me where he is.”

Devlon’s lips curled into something that wasn’t quite a smile. “You’re in no position to make demands. Not here. Not ever.”

The rain outside intensified, drumming against the tent like a warning. Nesta’s pulse pounded in her throat, but she didn’t flinch. “I’m not leaving until you tell me.”

“Then you’ll be waiting a long time.” Devlon’s voice was a blade wrapped in velvet. “The General’s orders were clear. You’re to remain here, under my supervision. And you’ll do as you’re told.”

Nesta’s breath came faster, her chest rising and falling with the effort of holding back the scream building inside her. She had spent months feeling nothing—nothing but the hollow ache in her bones, the emptiness where her soul should have been. But this? This was a spark in the dark. This was something.

“You’re complicit,” she said, her voice low, dangerous. “In whatever’s happening here. In whatever they’re planning.”

Devlon’s smirk didn’t waver. “Careful, girl. Accusations like that can get you killed.”

She laughed, a sharp, brittle sound. “I’m already dead. What’s one more threat?”

For a moment, Devlon’s mask slipped, something like surprise flickering in his eyes. Then it was gone, replaced by cold amusement. “You have your orders. Follow them, or face the consequences.”

Nesta turned on her heel, the wet hem of her gown slapping against her legs. She didn’t bother with a parting shot. She didn’t need one.

The rain had turned the training yard into a swamp, the mud sucking at her boots as she strode away from the command tent. The warriors had scattered, seeking shelter from the storm, but she didn’t care. Let them watch. Let them whisper.

A shadow shifted near the edge of the yard, just beyond the reach of the flickering torches. Nesta slowed, her gaze locking onto the figure standing there.

Bellius.

His lips were curled into a smirk, his eyes gleaming in the dim light. The air between them crackled with something dark, something hungry.

Nesta didn’t break stride. She stopped just short of him, close enough to see the malice in his gaze, close enough to feel the heat of his breath.

“I’m not going anywhere,” she said, her voice a low growl.

Bellius’s smirk widened, his teeth flashing white in the darkness. “Good.”

---

The hut smelled of damp wool and old smoke. Nesta stood just inside the door, her chest rising and falling in shallow, controlled breaths. The storm outside had not yet broken, but the air was thick with the promise of it—heavy, suffocating. She flexed her fingers, the memory of Bellius’s smirk still burning against her skin.

Her gaze flicked to the small wooden table beside the bed. The dagger Cassian had given her lay there, its hilt worn smooth from use. She had left it behind when she stormed out earlier, but now she reached for it, her fingers brushing the cool metal. The weight of it was familiar, grounding. She traced the engravings along the blade—Illyrian runes, ones she didn’t understand but had memorized the shape of all the same.

The door creaked.

Nesta’s body locked, her grip tightening around the dagger as she whirled toward the sound. But it wasn’t Bellius standing in the doorway. It was Kars, his broad frame silhouetted against the dim light of the storm-choked sky. His wings were tucked tight against his back, his dark hair plastered to his forehead with rain that hadn’t yet fallen in earnest.

“You shouldn’t be here,” she said, her voice sharp.

Kars didn’t flinch. “Neither should you. Not like this.” His eyes flicked to the dagger in her hand, then back to her face. “They’re coming for you at dusk.”

Nesta’s pulse spiked. “Who?”

“Bellius’s faction. They’re using the storm as cover. The camp will be distracted, the wind will swallow the sound of their wings.” Kars stepped inside, shutting the door behind him. The hut suddenly felt too small. “They mean to make an example of you.”

Nesta bared her teeth. “Let them try.”

Kars exhaled through his nose, a sound that might have been a laugh if it weren’t so bitter. “You don’t understand. This isn’t just about humiliating you. They want you broken. Or dead.”

The words settled over her like a shroud. She had known, of course. Had felt the weight of their hatred pressing down on her with every sneer, every whispered insult. But to hear it spoken aloud—

“Why tell me?” she demanded. “Why not let them have their fun?”

Kars met her gaze, his dark eyes unreadable. “Because I believe in what you could be.”

Nesta stilled. The dagger suddenly felt too heavy in her hand. “What?”

“You’re not like them,” he said, jerking his chin toward the door, toward the camp beyond. “You’re not like anyone I’ve ever met. And I’ve seen what happens when people like you are crushed before they have the chance to rise.” His voice was low, steady. “I won’t be part of that.”

Nesta’s throat tightened. She wanted to laugh, to sneer, to tell him he was a fool for thinking she was anything but a hollow shell. But the words stuck in her chest, lodged beneath the weight of something she refused to name.

Kars didn’t wait for a response. He turned toward the door, his hand resting on the latch. “Be ready,” he said over his shoulder. “And don’t trust anyone.”

Then he was gone, slipping out into the gathering dark like a shadow.

Nesta stood alone in the hut, the silence pressing in around her. The first drops of rain began to fall, cold and relentless, drumming against the thatched roof like the countdown to something inevitable. She looked down at the dagger in her hand, her fingers trembling.

For the first time in months, she felt something.

And it terrified her.

---

The rain fell in sheets, a relentless drumming against the earth that matched the pounding in Nesta’s skull. It soaked through her gray gown within moments, the fabric clinging to her like a second skin. She didn’t bother to shield herself from it. Let it drown her. Let it wash away the hollow ache that had taken root in her chest, the one that had festered since Feyre’s words had sliced through her like a blade.

*You are not welcome here.*

The wind howled through the Illyrian peaks, whipping her braided coronet loose, strands of hair whipping across her face. She didn’t push them away. She welcomed the sting, the bite of the cold. It was better than the nothingness.

The training grounds loomed ahead, the firelight flickering through the downpour like a beacon. The warriors were gathered around it, their laughter loud, forced. They were pretending. Pretending they weren’t watching her. Pretending they didn’t know what was coming.

Nesta’s fingers curled into fists at her sides.

Bellius stood at the center of the circle, his broad frame blocking the worst of the wind. His voice boomed over the storm, thick with false cheer. *"Tonight, we celebrate the storm!"* The warriors cheered, their voices rough, their eyes darting toward her. Pity. Anticipation. She could taste it in the air, sour and cloying.

She didn’t slow her pace.

Bellius turned his head, his smirk cutting through the rain. *"Join us, Nesta."* His voice was a taunt, a challenge. *"Or are you too afraid?"*

The warriors fell silent, the firelight casting long shadows across their faces. Waiting.

Nesta stopped just beyond the circle, the mud sucking at her boots. The rain dripped from her lashes, her jaw set. She didn’t look at them. Didn’t acknowledge the way their gazes burned into her. She kept her eyes on Bellius.

His smirk didn’t waver.

She had spent months feeling nothing. Months drowning in the hollow, in the emptiness that had swallowed her whole after the war. After the Cauldron. After everything.

But now—

Now, something flickered in her chest. A spark. A ember of rage, hot and bright.

It terrified her.

She let it burn.

Nesta’s voice cut through the noise, sharp as a blade. *"I’m not afraid of you."*

The warriors stilled. The fire crackled, the wind howling like a warning.

Bellius’s smirk faltered for the barest second before he bared his teeth. *"We’ll see."*

---

The storm crashed over the Illyrian Mountains like a living thing, thunder shaking the earth beneath Nesta’s boots. Lightning split the sky, illuminating the jagged peaks in stark white light, and the warriors scattered like leaves in the wind, their wings snapping open as they bolted for shelter. The training grounds emptied in seconds, the clang of steel and grunts of sparring replaced by the howl of the gale.

Nesta didn’t move.

Her dagger gleamed in her grip, the blade steady despite the rain lashing her face. The gray gown she wore—immaculate, as always—was already plastered to her body, the fabric clinging to the sharp angles of her frame. She didn’t shiver. Didn’t flinch. The hollow ache inside her had long since numbed her to the cold.

Bellius remained, his wings flared wide, the red Siphons on his knuckles glowing like embers in the dark. His smirk was a blade of its own, twisting as he took a step toward her. "You should have run," he snarled over the wind.

Nesta said nothing. Her fingers tightened around the hilt of her dagger, her breath steady, controlled. She had spent weeks learning to move like this—like the wind itself, like the storm. And now, with the world screaming around her, she was ready.

Bellius lunged.

She twisted, her body coiling like a spring before unraveling in a fluid arc. The dagger slashed, but he was already moving, his wings beating once to propel him back. His laughter was a dark, mocking thing. "Is that all?" he taunted, circling her. "After all this time, is that *all* you’ve got?"

Nesta bared her teeth. The rain dripped from her braided coronet, her cheekbones sharp enough to cut. She didn’t answer. Words were useless. She had spent too long drowning in them—hers, theirs, the ones that had carved her hollow.

She attacked again.

This time, she didn’t hold back. Her dagger flashed, a silver streak in the storm, and Bellius barely dodged, his wings snapping out to block. The impact reverberated up her arm, but she didn’t falter. She pressed forward, her movements fueled by something darker than rage—something colder. Something that had been festering since the moment she had been dragged into this world, since the moment she had been told she was broken and left to rot.

Bellius grunted as her blade grazed his ribs, a thin line of red blooming against his skin. His eyes flashed, the Siphons flaring brighter. "Bitch," he hissed.

Nesta didn’t care. She didn’t *feel*. Not the rain, not the wind, not the exhaustion creeping into her muscles. There was only the next strike, the next breath, the next moment where she could prove—*to herself*—that she wasn’t nothing.

Bellius recovered faster than she expected. His wings beat once, twice, and then he was on her, his dagger a blur. She blocked, but the force of his blow sent her stumbling back. Her boot caught on a slick patch of mud, and she went down hard, her knees slamming into the wet earth.

The warriors watched from the shadows, their faces unreadable. She didn’t look at them. Didn’t *care*.

Bellius loomed over her, his wings casting her in shadow. His dagger pressed against her throat, the edge biting just enough to draw a thin line of blood. "This is the end," he whispered, his breath hot against her face.

Nesta didn’t look away. Didn’t blink.

She smiled.

"No," she said, her voice steady despite the storm raging around them. "It’s just the beginning."

---

The wind howled through the Illyrian peaks, a living thing that clawed at Nesta’s braided coronet, tugging strands free to whip across her face. The storm had turned the training camp into a battleground of shadows and flickering torchlight, the air thick with the scent of damp earth and male aggression. She stood her ground, fingers curled into fists at her sides, the gray gown she’d worn clinging to her legs like a second skin. Immaculate, just as she’d intended. A mockery of the filth around her.

Bellius loomed before her, his wings half-spread, the feathers rustling with barely contained violence. His warriors fanned out behind him, their faces a mix of hunger and hesitation. They had come for blood. Hers.

And she had let them.

A roar split the night—deep, primal, shaking the very stones beneath her boots. Nesta didn’t turn. She knew that sound. Knew the weight of it in her bones.

Cassian landed between her and Bellius with the force of a falling star, his wings snapping wide, the crimson glow of his Siphons casting eerie light across the muddied ground. His flying leathers were splattered with rain, his gloves dark with it, but his stance was unyielding. A wall of muscle and fury.

“Step away from her.”

Bellius didn’t move. His gaze flicked between Cassian and Nesta, calculating. The warriors murmured, their loyalty wavering like reeds in the wind. Cassian didn’t so much as glance at them. His focus was solely on the male before him, his voice dropping into a register that promised pain.

“This ends now.”

Bellius smirked, but there was a flicker of something in his eyes—uncertainty, perhaps. Or fear. “You’re too late, General.”

Nesta stepped forward, her boots sinking into the mud. The cold seeped through the thin soles, but she didn’t flinch. She met Cassian’s gaze for the first time since he’d landed, and something in her chest tightened at the sight of him. The bruises on his knuckles. The way his jaw clenched, as if he were barely holding himself back.

She lifted her chin. “No,” she said, her voice steady despite the storm raging around them. “He’s right on time.”

The words hung between them, heavy with meaning. Cassian’s wings shifted, the crimson light of his Siphons pulsing like a heartbeat. The warriors fell silent, their breath held in collective anticipation.

Bellius’s smirk faltered. “This doesn’t concern you, General. She—”

“She is mine to protect.” Cassian’s voice was a blade, sharp and final. “And you will answer for this.”

Nesta’s pulse spiked. *Mine.* The word echoed in her skull, unwanted and yet… not entirely unwelcome. She pushed the thought aside. She didn’t need protecting. Not from Bellius. Not from anyone.

But she didn’t contradict him.

Bellius’s wings twitched, his fingers flexing at his sides. “You’ve always had a soft spot for broken things, haven’t you?” His gaze flicked to Nesta, lingering on the hollows of her cheeks, the sharp angles of her face. “Though I can’t imagine why. She’s not worth the trouble.”

Cassian moved before Nesta could react, his hand shooting out to wrap around Bellius’s throat. The male choked, his wings flaring as he was lifted onto his toes. “You will not speak of her,” Cassian growled. “You will not even *look* at her.”

The warriors tensed, some reaching for their weapons. Nesta’s breath hitched, but she didn’t intervene. Let them see. Let them all see what happened when you crossed Cassian.

Bellius’s face darkened, his fingers clawing at Cassian’s wrist. “You’re making a mistake,” he rasped.

Cassian bared his teeth. “I’ve made worse.”

A beat of silence. Then, from the darkness beyond the torchlight, the sound of wings—many wings. Nesta’s stomach twisted. Reinforcements. For whom, she couldn’t be sure.

Cassian’s grip didn’t waver. His gaze finally locked onto hers, his expression unreadable. The storm raged on, the wind carrying the scent of rain and iron, the distant rumble of thunder a counterpoint to the tension coiling between them.

“Are you hurt?” His voice was low, rough.

Nesta shook her head, her throat tight. “Not yet.”

The words were barely audible over the howling wind, but Cassian heard them. His jaw clenched, his fingers flexing around Bellius’s throat before he released him with a shove. The male staggered back, coughing, his warriors catching him before he could fall.

The sound of wings grew louder. Closer.

Cassian didn’t take his eyes off Nesta. “This isn’t over.”

She knew he wasn’t talking about Bellius.

The wind howled in response, carrying the promise of more violence, more bloodshed. The night was far from over.
