CHAPTER 10
The Edge of the Knife

The first light of dawn had not yet crept over the jagged peaks of the Illyrian Mountains when Nesta’s eyes snapped open. She lay still for a moment, listening to the wind howl through the cracks in the stone walls of her quarters, the cold seeping into her bones. Then she moved—slowly, deliberately—testing the limits of her body. The bruises from Bellius’s latest attack throbbed beneath her skin, a dull, persistent ache that flared with every breath. She gritted her teeth and sat up, her ribs protesting, her muscles stiff from the night’s restless sleep.

She dressed in silence, her fingers working mechanically to braid her hair into its usual coronet. The gray gown she wore was immaculate, a stark contrast to the rough stone and dirt of the training grounds. She had not bothered to wash it in the icy stream that served as their only source of water, but it didn’t matter. Nothing mattered here. Not the cold, not the pain, not the way the warriors watched her with thinly veiled contempt.

The training grounds were quiet when she stepped outside, the warriors still asleep in their barracks. She walked to the edge of the cliff, her breath misting in the frigid air, and stared out at the endless expanse of mountains. The wind tugged at her braid, pulling strands loose, but she didn’t bother to fix it. Let them see her unraveling. Let them think it meant something.

She was still standing there when Devlon’s voice cut through the silence.

“Archeron.”

She turned, her expression carefully blank. Devlon stood with his arms crossed, his scarred face unreadable. Behind him, the warriors were beginning to stir, their murmurs rising like the distant hum of insects.

“Today’s test is knife-throwing,” Devlon said, his voice carrying across the training grounds. “You’ll be paired against Bellius.”

Nesta said nothing. She didn’t need to. The warriors’ reactions were enough—some snickered, others exchanged knowing glances. Bellius himself stepped forward, his lips curling into a smirk as he twirled a knife between his fingers.

Cassian was there, too, leaning against the stone wall of the barracks, his arms crossed over his chest. His dark wings were folded tightly against his back, his crimson Siphons glinting in the dim light. His face was unreadable, but his eyes—his eyes were fixed on her, sharp and assessing.

She ignored him. She had ignored him for weeks, ever since she had told him to stop following her, to stop pretending he cared. He had listened, in the end. Or at least, he had stopped trying to talk to her.

Devlon gestured to the throwing line, where a row of knives had been laid out on a wooden table. “First throw is yours, Archeron.”

Nesta walked forward, her boots crunching on the frost-covered ground. The weight of the knife in her hand was unfamiliar, the hilt rough against her palm. She had never thrown one before. She had never needed to.

Bellius’s smirk widened. “Afraid, Archeron?”

She didn’t answer. She didn’t look at him. Instead, she focused on the target—a wooden post with a painted bullseye, swaying slightly in the wind. She lifted the knife, testing its balance, then drew her arm back.

The first throw was hers.

---

The knife left her fingers before she was ready.

It wobbled through the air, the blade catching the pale morning light as it spun end over end, landing with a dull *thunk* in the dirt a good three feet from the target. The warriors gathered around the training grounds snickered, their laughter sharp as the wind that whipped through the Illyrian peaks. Nesta didn’t look at them. She kept her gaze fixed on the target, on the knife jutting from the earth like an accusation.

Bellius stepped forward, his movements fluid, deliberate. He plucked a knife from the bandolier across his chest, testing its weight with a flick of his wrist. His smile was slow, cruel. "Perhaps you should stick to needlework, Lady Nesta."

The first knife struck the target with a *thwack*, the blade quivering in the outer ring. The second followed, closer to the center. The third landed between her feet.

Nesta didn’t flinch. She had spent months learning how to stand perfectly still, how to let the world slide off her like rain. But her fingers twitched at her sides, her nails biting into her palms hard enough to leave crescent moons in her skin.

She reached for another knife.

The weight was wrong. Too light, too balanced. She had handled knives before—kitchen knives, the kind used to slice bread or carve meat. These were different. These were made for throwing, for killing. The hilt was wrapped in worn leather, the blade honed to a razor’s edge. She could feel the eyes of the warriors on her, could hear the whispers rustling through them like dry leaves.

*Pathetic. Useless. What is she even doing here?*

Cassian stood apart from the rest, his arms crossed over his chest, his wings flared slightly as if he were holding himself back. His Siphons glinted crimson in the sunlight, but his face was unreadable. She refused to look at him. Refused to give him the satisfaction of seeing her fail.

Her second throw was better. The knife flew true for half its arc before veering wide, embedding itself in the wooden frame of the target. The warriors’ laughter died down, replaced by a low murmur. Bellius tilted his head, studying her like she was a puzzle he was determined to solve.

"Still not good enough," he said, his voice carrying across the training grounds. "But then, I didn’t expect it would be."

Nesta’s jaw ached from clenching it. She could feel the heat rising in her cheeks, the simmering rage that had been her constant companion for months now. It was a live thing inside her, coiled and ready to strike.

*Don’t let him provoke you.*

Cassian’s voice echoed in her mind, but it sounded hollow now, distant. What did he know of provocation? What did he know of standing in front of a crowd of sneering warriors, of having every failure dissected and ridiculed?

She reached for another knife.

The third throw was hers.

The blade struck the outer ring of the target, the impact sending a shudder through the wood. The warriors fell silent for a heartbeat, their eyes flicking between her and Bellius. Kars, the grizzled old warrior who had been watching from the sidelines, gave a single, slow nod. Devlon’s expression remained impassive, but there was something in his eyes—something like approval.

Bellius didn’t smile this time. He stepped forward, selecting a knife with deliberate care. "Let’s see how you handle this, then."

The knife flew straight and true, the blade grazing the sleeve of her gown before embedding itself in the target behind her. The fabric tore with a soft *rip*, the cold mountain air kissing her skin where the blade had split the seam.

The warriors erupted into cheers, their voices rising into the sky like a flock of crows taking flight. Bellius bowed, sweeping his arm in a mocking gesture of deference. "Better luck next time, Lady Nesta."

Nesta didn’t look at him. She didn’t look at the warriors. She turned her head just enough to catch Cassian’s gaze.

His expression was dark, his wings flared wide as if he were a breath away from launching himself into the air. His hands were clenched at his sides, the muscles in his arms corded with tension. For a moment, she thought he might step forward, might put an end to this farce.

She turned away before he could speak.

---

The training grounds fell silent as Devlon’s voice cut through the crisp mountain air. "Bellius wins."

Nesta didn’t move. Didn’t blink. The words settled over her like a shroud, heavy and suffocating. Around her, the other warriors shifted, their boots scraping against the packed earth. Some murmured in approval, others in disbelief. But none of it mattered. None of it was real.

Because she had lost.

Again.

Her fingers twitched at her sides, the memory of the knife’s handle still burning in her palm. She could still see Bellius’s smug grin, the way his eyes had gleamed when he’d disarmed her. The way he’d leaned in, his breath hot against her ear, and whispered, *"You fight like a human."*

The words had been a blade of their own, twisting deep.

She turned on her heel, her braided coronet unraveling slightly with the movement, strands of hair whipping across her face. The wind howled down from the peaks, biting at her skin, but she barely felt it. All she felt was the fire in her veins, the humiliation clawing at her throat.

Footsteps followed her. Of course they did.

Cassian’s voice was low, measured. *"You did better than I expected."*

Nesta didn’t slow. Didn’t stop. She whirled on him, her lip curling. *"Don’t patronize me."*

His jaw tightened, the crimson Siphons on his knuckles flaring faintly in the afternoon light. *"I’m not. But you’re letting him get in your head."*

Her laugh was sharp, brittle. *"And what if I am? What does it matter?"* She spread her arms wide, the wind tugging at the sleeves of her tunic. *"I’m here, aren’t I? Doing exactly what I was told. Exactly what you all wanted. So what if I lose? What if I’m never good enough?"*

Cassian stepped closer, his wings rustling with the movement. *"It matters because you’re better than this."*

The words struck her like a physical blow. She sucked in a breath, her chest tightening. Better. As if that word meant anything. As if she even knew what it meant anymore.

She turned away before he could see the way her hands trembled. The cliffs loomed ahead, the drop sheer and endless. The wind screamed in her ears as she walked, her boots kicking up dust and pebbles that skittered over the edge.

Behind her, Cassian didn’t follow. But she could feel his gaze on her back, heavy and unrelenting, like the weight of all the things they weren’t saying.

She stopped at the edge, the wind whipping her hair into a frenzy. Below, the valley stretched out, a patchwork of green and gold, the world continuing on as if nothing had changed. As if she hadn’t just been humiliated in front of everyone. As if she wasn’t standing here, hollow and shaking.

Her fingers flexed at her sides, the sting of the knife’s handle still fresh in her palm. She curled them into fists, nails biting into skin.

And for the first time in months, she felt something.

Rage.

---

The hut smelled of pine and sweat and the faint metallic tang of blood. Nesta’s blood, mostly—seeping through the torn fabric of her tunic where the wooden practice sword had found its mark. Again.

She didn’t wince as she ducked inside, though her ribs screamed in protest. The door groaned shut behind her, sealing out the wind that howled through the Illyrian peaks. Kars stood by the small wooden table, his broad back turned to her, fingers working a whetstone along the edge of a knife with slow, deliberate strokes.

Nesta exhaled through her nose. “If you’re going to lecture me, save it.”

The scraping didn’t pause. “Sit.”

She should have refused. Should have turned on her heel and walked back out into the cold, where the wind could punish her instead of his silence. But her legs ached, her shoulders burned, and the thought of climbing back up those cliffs to the training grounds made her want to scream.

So she sat.

The stool creaked beneath her weight. Kars didn’t look up as he set the whetstone aside and reached for a roll of bandages. The knife lay between them, its edge catching the dim light filtering through the shutters.

“You’re bleeding through your tunic,” he said.

Nesta bared her teeth. “I’m aware.”

A muscle feathered in his jaw. He was older than Cassian by a decade, his face lined with scars that told stories she didn’t care to hear. But his hands were steady as he unwound the bandages, the callouses rough against her skin when he finally turned to her.

She hissed as he pressed the cloth to the worst of the gashes. “You could at least pretend to be gentle.”

“Why?” His dark eyes flicked to hers. “You’re not.”

Nesta’s fingers curled into invisible claws. “I didn’t ask for your help.”

“No.” He wrapped the bandage tight, his touch impersonal. “You asked for a knife.”

The words hung between them, heavy as the silence that followed. Outside, the wind keened, rattling the shutters. Nesta stared at the blade on the table. Its edge was flawless, honed to a deadly shine.

Kars picked it up, turning it in his hands. “You’re not weak.”

Her laugh was sharp enough to cut. “That’s not what your warriors say.”

“They’re fools.” He set the knife down with a quiet *click*. “Strength isn’t just about how hard you swing a sword. It’s about knowing when to stop swinging.”

Nesta’s lip curled. “Spare me the Illyrian wisdom.”

Kars leaned back, studying her. “You’re fighting to prove you don’t care,” he said at last. “But the real fight is learning to care again.”

The words struck like a physical blow. Nesta’s breath hitched, her chest tightening as if the bandages had been wrapped around her ribs instead of her arm. She wanted to snarl, to lash out, to tell him he knew nothing of what she’d lost, what she’d survived—

But the knife gleamed between them, its edge reflecting the dim light.

And for the first time in months, the hollow space inside her didn’t feel quite so empty.

What if he was right?

---

The night was a living thing, breathing cold and sharp against Nesta’s skin as she stepped from the crude stone hut. The wind howled through the Illyrian peaks, carrying the scent of pine and frost, and she pulled her thin shawl tighter around her shoulders. Sleep had been a fool’s errand—her mind too restless, her body too aware of the emptiness inside her, the space that had once been filled with something like purpose, or at least the pretense of it.

She didn’t know where she was going until her feet carried her toward the cliffs. The training grounds were silent now, the warriors long since retired to their huts or the tavern in the village below. Only the wind remained, whispering secrets against the rocks.

And then she saw him.

Cassian stood at the edge of the cliff, his wings folded tightly against his back, the crimson Siphons on his knuckles glowing faintly in the dark. He didn’t turn as she approached, but she knew he was aware of her. He was always aware of her.

The wind tugged at her braid, loosening strands that whipped across her face. She stopped a few paces behind him, her breath shallow.

“You’re not the only one who’s lost something.”

His voice cut through the silence, rough and low. Nesta froze, her fingers curling into the fabric of her shawl. She didn’t ask what he meant. She didn’t want to know.

Cassian exhaled, a sound like a blade being drawn. “I’ve spent centuries fighting for people who don’t want to be saved. Who look at me like I’m the enemy just for trying.” His wings shifted, the feathers rustling like dry leaves. “But you…” His voice dropped, raw and unguarded. “You’re fighting to save yourself, even if you don’t realize it.”

Nesta’s hands trembled. The words struck too close, too true. She swallowed. “I don’t need saving.”

Cassian finally turned to her, his dark eyes burning in the moonlight. The wind played with the strands of his hair, tugging them across his forehead. “No,” he said, quieter now. “But you need to stop pretending you don’t need *anything*.”

The words hung between them, heavy and unyielding. Nesta’s chest tightened. She wanted to argue, to spit back some sharp retort, to make him bleed the way he was making her bleed now. But the wind howled around them, and the cliff’s edge loomed, and for the first time, she wondered if the real battle wasn’t against the warriors who sneered at her, or even against Cassian himself.

Maybe it was against the hollow space inside her that had been empty for so long.
