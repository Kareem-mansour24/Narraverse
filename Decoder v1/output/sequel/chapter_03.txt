CHAPTER 3
The Language of Bruises

The cold bit into Nesta’s skin before she even opened her eyes.

She lay still for a moment, letting the ache settle into her bones. The bruises along her ribs pulsed in time with her heartbeat, a dull, insistent throb. Her knuckles, split from yesterday’s sparring, stung as she flexed her fingers. She didn’t wince. Pain was just another sensation, no different from the weight of the blanket or the chill of the air. It meant nothing.

She sat up, the thin mattress groaning beneath her, and reached for the training leathers draped over the wooden chair beside her cot. The hut was small, barely more than a box of rough-hewn planks, but it was hers. For now.

The laces of her tunic dug into her palms as she pulled them tight, her movements precise, controlled. She didn’t look at the bruises blooming like dark flowers across her ribs. Didn’t acknowledge the way her muscles screamed when she bent to fasten the buckles of her boots.

Outside, the world was still half-lost in shadow, the mountains looming like silent sentinels. The air smelled of snow and sweat, of damp earth and the sharp tang of metal. The training grounds were already alive with movement—warriors stretching, sparring, their laughter rough and unkind.

Nesta stepped into the pale dawn light, her breath curling in front of her. The warriors didn’t stop their conversations, but their voices lowered just enough to make it clear they were talking about her. She ignored them. Let them stare. Let them whisper.

She had learned long ago that the best way to survive was to pretend she didn’t notice the knives in their words.

A group of males near the sparring rings glanced her way, their expressions ranging from amusement to outright disdain. One of them, a broad-shouldered warrior with a scar cutting through his eyebrow, smirked as he said something to his companions. They laughed, the sound grating against her nerves.

Nesta didn’t react. She walked past them, her boots crunching on the frost-kissed grass, and stopped at the edge of the training grounds. The mountain rose before her, its slopes jagged and unforgiving. Somewhere up there, the wind howled, sharp enough to flay skin from bone.

She exhaled, watching the mist of her breath dissipate into the cold air.

Then Devlon’s voice cut through the morning, deep and commanding.

“Footrace. Up the eastern slope. First one to the ridge gets an extra ration of meat tonight.”

The warriors whooped, already stripping off their tunics, their bodies coiled with anticipation. Nesta didn’t move. She knew what was coming.

Devlon’s gaze landed on her, his lips curling into something that wasn’t quite a smile. “You too, Archeron. Unless you’re too sore to keep up.”

The warriors nearest her snickered.

Nesta didn’t look at them. Didn’t give them the satisfaction of seeing her react.

She turned toward the mountain, her body already bracing for the climb.

The race hadn’t even started, and she could already feel the burn in her legs.

---

The morning air was thin and sharp in her lungs, the kind that made each breath feel like swallowing knives. Nesta stood at the starting line, the dirt cool beneath her boots, her fingers flexing at her sides. Around her, the warriors lounged, their laughter rough and mocking.

*"Look at the pretty lady, all dressed up for a stroll,"* one of them sneered, elbowing his companion.

*"Think she’ll make it past the first bend before she faints?"* another called, grinning.

Nesta didn’t look at them. She kept her gaze fixed on the path ahead, the steep incline of the mountain cutting into the sky. Her muscles ached from yesterday’s training, from the endless drills Cassian had put her through before he’d vanished this morning without a word. Not that she cared. Not that she’d asked where he’d gone.

Devlon stepped forward, his arms crossed over his broad chest. His smirk was as sharp as the wind. *"Rules are simple, Archeron. Last one to the summit cleans the barracks for a week. No magic, no wings. Just your own two legs."*

Nesta’s jaw tightened. She could feel the weight of their stares, the smug certainty that she’d fail. That she’d be the one scrubbing floors while they laughed.

The horn blew.

She launched forward before the sound had fully faded, her boots kicking up dust as she sprinted. The path was steep, the rocks uneven, but she didn’t slow. Her legs burned, her lungs screamed, but she pushed harder, her pride a living thing inside her chest. She wouldn’t be last. She *wouldn’t*.

The warriors surged ahead, their longer strides eating up the ground, but Nesta didn’t let them widen the gap. She wove between them, her breath coming in ragged gasps, her vision narrowing to the path in front of her. One by one, she passed them—first the slower ones, then the mid-pack, their grunts of surprise lost beneath the pounding of her heart.

The summit loomed closer, the finish line a blur of bodies ahead. She gritted her teeth and dug deeper, her thighs trembling with the effort. The world narrowed to the rhythm of her steps, the burn in her lungs, the stubborn refusal to let them see her break.

She crossed the line mid-pack, her chest heaving, her legs threatening to give out. She didn’t stop, didn’t let herself collapse—not yet. She staggered forward a few more steps before bending at the waist, her hands braced on her knees, her breath sawing in and out of her throat.

Silence.

Then, murmurs. Not the mocking laughter from before, but something quieter. Speculative.

Nesta straightened slowly, her lungs still screaming, and turned to face Devlon. His smirk had faded, his dark eyes unreadable as he tossed her a waterskin. She caught it, her fingers numb.

*"Not bad for a city rat,"* he muttered, his voice low. *"But running won’t save you in the ring."*

---

The training grounds were a blur of dust and sweat, the morning sun already brutal against Nesta’s skin. She had barely slept, her body still aching from yesterday’s drills, but the warriors had no mercy for weakness. Especially not hers.

Bellius stood across from her, his grin wide and vicious as he circled. His knuckles were wrapped in leather, the same as hers, but his fists had already left their mark—on her ribs, her jaw, the tender flesh beneath her eye. She could taste blood, metallic and thick, but she refused to spit it out. Refused to give him the satisfaction.

The warriors around them jeered, their voices a distant roar in her ears. She blocked out the sound, focusing on the way Bellius’s boots dragged through the dirt, the way his muscles coiled before he struck. She had learned that much, at least.

But not enough.

His fist connected with her side, and she stumbled, her breath leaving her in a sharp gasp. The pain was white-hot, searing through her ribs, but she didn’t fall. She couldn’t. Not again.

She swung blindly, her own strike weak and clumsy, but it landed—just enough to make him snarl. His eyes gleamed with triumph as he advanced, his next blow aimed for her jaw.

She braced for it.

Then—

“Enough.”

The word cut through the air like a blade.

Bellius froze mid-swing, his fist still raised. The warriors fell silent, their laughter dying in their throats.

Cassian stood at the edge of the ring, his crimson Siphons flaring like embers in the sunlight. His wings were half-spread, his jaw set in a line so sharp it could have drawn blood.

“Step back,” he ordered, his voice low and lethal.

Bellius hesitated, his grin faltering. But one look at Cassian’s face had him obeying, retreating with a muttered curse.

Nesta’s knees buckled.

She hit the ground before she could stop herself, her vision swimming. Strong hands caught her before she could collapse completely, pulling her upright.

Cassian’s grip was firm but careful, his fingers warm against her skin. She could feel the calluses on his palms, the strength in his arms as he steadied her. For a moment, she let herself lean into him, just for a second, before the humiliation burned through her.

She wrenched herself free, her breath coming in ragged gasps. The warriors were watching. *Laughing.* She could hear it in the way their voices murmured, the way their eyes flicked between her and Cassian.

She didn’t need his help. She didn’t need *anyone’s* help.

Cassian’s jaw tightened as he looked down at her, her face streaked with blood and dirt. “You’re done for the day,” he said, his voice low.

Nesta bared her teeth, her fingers curling into invisible claws. “I didn’t ask for your help.”

---

The hut smelled of damp earth and old smoke. Nesta’s legs trembled with each step, her boots sinking into the mud as she stumbled toward the low wooden door. The training yard had been a blur of pain and humiliation, her body screaming in protest with every strike, every fall. She had refused to yield. Refused to let them see her break.

But now, alone in the dim light of her hut, the weight of it all pressed down on her. She collapsed onto the thin pallet, her breath coming in ragged gasps. The bruises along her ribs throbbed, her knuckles split and raw. She didn’t bother to wipe the blood from her lip.

The door creaked open.

Nesta didn’t lift her head. She knew the scent of him—pine and iron, the faint tang of ointment. Kars. One of the few warriors who hadn’t spat at her feet or called her a whore under his breath.

He hesitated in the doorway, his broad frame blocking the last of the evening light. His dark eyes flicked to her face, to the bruise blooming along her jaw. “You don’t have to do this,” he said quietly.

Nesta glared at him, her voice a rasp. “Yes, I do.”

Kars exhaled through his nose, then stepped inside, shutting the door behind him. He carried a bundle of herbs and a fresh roll of bandages, the linen stark white against his calloused hands. He knelt beside her, his movements careful, as if she were a wounded animal that might bolt at any moment.

She didn’t flinch as he pressed a poultice to her split lip. The herbs stung, but she welcomed the pain. It was better than the hollow ache inside her chest.

“They’re saying you’re either brave or stupid,” Kars murmured, his fingers working deftly as he unwound the bandages. “No one’s sure which.”

Nesta let out a bitter laugh. “What do you think?”

He didn’t answer right away. His hands paused over her ribs, his gaze meeting hers. “I think you’re the first person here who’s ever made Bellius bleed.”

Something flickered in her chest—something hot and bright, like a spark in the dark. She swallowed it down. “He deserved it.”

Kars nodded, as if that were answer enough. He finished wrapping her ribs, his touch gentle despite the roughness of his hands. “You’re lucky he didn’t break them.”

“He tried.”

Kars’s mouth tightened. He stood, wiping his hands on his trousers. “They’re planning something,” he said, his voice low. “For tonight.”

Nesta’s eyes narrowed. “What?”

“I don’t know.” His jaw clenched. “But it’s not good.”

He slipped out the door before she could press him further, leaving her alone in the flickering candlelight. The shadows stretched long across the walls, and Nesta stared at them, her mind racing.

She had come here to prove something—to herself, to Cassian, to all of them. But the mountains didn’t care about her pride. The mountains only cared about survival.

And right now, she wasn’t sure she’d survive the night.

---

The cold gnawed at her bones, a relentless, gnashing thing that sank its teeth into every inch of exposed skin. Nesta sat on the edge of her cot, the thin wool blanket doing little to shield her from the draft seeping through the cracks in the hut’s walls. Outside, the warriors’ laughter rose in raucous waves, punctuated by the clink of mugs and the occasional thud of a fist against wood. She told herself she didn’t care. That their noise was nothing to her. That she was above it.

But the laughter grated, like nails dragged down glass.

She stood abruptly, her muscles protesting the movement. Every part of her ached—her arms, her legs, her back—from the day’s training. Cassian had pushed her harder than ever, as if he could force her to break just by sheer will. She hadn’t. She wouldn’t. But the bruises blooming beneath her skin were a language she couldn’t ignore.

The night air hit her like a slap as she stepped outside. Her breath fogged in front of her, a fleeting ghost before it dissipated into the dark. The snow crunched beneath her boots, the sound too loud in the quiet between the bursts of revelry from the barracks. She told herself she was just stretching her legs. That she wasn’t seeking out the noise, wasn’t drawn to it like a moth to a flame.

The training grounds were empty, the wooden posts and dummies standing sentinel in the moonlight. She passed them without a glance, her steps carrying her toward the barracks. The noise grew louder, the laughter sharper, until she could make out individual voices. She slowed, her pulse quickening.

A crack in the shutters offered a sliver of a view inside. The warmth of the firelight spilled out, painting the snow in gold. Bellius sat at the head of a long table, his face flushed with drink, his grin wide and predatory. His friends were gathered around him, their expressions mirroring his. One of them held up a length of rope, his fingers toying with the frayed end.

“She’ll never see it coming,” he said, his voice slurring just enough to make the words slick with malice.

Nesta’s blood turned to ice.

She knew that rope. Had seen it coiled in the armory, used it herself during training. But the way he held it now—twisted between his fingers, his grip tight—it wasn’t for practice.

Her breath hitched. She should have walked away. Should have turned her back on them and retreated to her hut, locked the door, pretended she hadn’t seen. But her feet stayed rooted to the spot, her eyes fixed on the scene unfolding inside.

Bellius leaned forward, his elbows braced on the table. “Tomorrow,” he said, his voice low but carrying. “When she’s alone. No one will hear her scream.”

The others laughed, the sound sharp and jagged. Nesta’s hands clenched into fists, her nails biting into her palms hard enough to draw blood. She wanted to storm in there. Wanted to wrap that rope around Bellius’s throat and squeeze until his laughter choked into silence.

But she didn’t.

She turned away, her steps quickening as she put distance between herself and the barracks. The cold air burned her lungs, but she welcomed it. Welcomed the sting, the clarity it brought. She wasn’t afraid. Not of them. Not of what they planned.

If they were coming for her, she’d be ready.

Her hut loomed ahead, but she didn’t slow. Instead, she veered toward the armory, her mind made up. The door creaked as she pushed it open, the scent of oil and metal wrapping around her like an old friend. She moved without hesitation, her fingers brushing over the weapons lining the walls. Swords, daggers, bows—none of them felt right. Not for this.

Her hand closed around a small, wickedly curved blade. It fit perfectly in her palm, the weight familiar, comforting. She tucked it into the sheath at her belt, then grabbed a second, smaller one for her boot. Just in case.

The armory door clicked shut behind her. The night was still, the laughter from the barracks now a distant hum. She exhaled, her breath steady, her pulse even.

Let them come.

---

The armory was a cavern of shadows, the scent of oil and steel thick in the air. Nesta moved like a ghost between the racks of weapons, her fingers trailing over hilts and blades as if reacquainting herself with old lovers. The weight of each one was familiar—too familiar. She had spent months pretending she didn’t know how to wield them, pretending she didn’t remember the way a dagger fit into her palm like an extension of her own body.

Her heart pounded, but her hands were steady as she selected a dagger from the rack. The blade was simple, unadorned, its edge honed to a lethal sharpness. She turned it over in her fingers, testing its balance, before slipping it into her boot. The leather hugged the steel like a secret.

She was halfway to the door when she heard it—the faint scuff of boots against stone. Nesta froze, her breath catching. The armory had no windows, no other exits. She ducked behind a stack of crates just as the door creaked open.

Cassian.

His broad frame filled the doorway, his wings tucked tight against his back. The crimson glow of his Siphons cast eerie light across his face, sharpening the angles of his jaw, the furrow between his brows. He looked tired. Or maybe it was something else—something like resignation.

Nesta pressed herself deeper into the shadows, her pulse roaring in her ears. *Don’t see me. Don’t see me.*

Cassian stepped inside, his gaze sweeping over the racks of weapons with the precision of a man who knew every inch of this place. Then he stilled. His eyes locked onto the empty space where the dagger had been.

Nesta’s fingers curled into the rough wood of the crate. *He knows.*

But Cassian didn’t call out. Didn’t so much as twitch in her direction. Instead, he reached for a sword, his movements deliberate, almost slow. He tested its weight, ran a gloved thumb along the edge, then resheathed it with a soft *click*.

For a heartbeat, she thought he might turn toward her hiding place. Thought he might demand to know what she was doing here, in the dark, with a blade hidden in her boot.

But he didn’t.

He just exhaled, long and low, before turning on his heel and leaving the armory as silently as he’d entered.

The door shut behind him. Nesta didn’t move. Didn’t breathe.

*Why?*

Why hadn’t he stopped her? Why hadn’t he said anything?

The questions coiled in her chest, tight and suffocating. She waited until the silence settled like dust before slipping out from behind the crates. The armory was empty now, the air still thick with the scent of him—leather and wind and something darker, something that made her throat tighten.

She didn’t look back as she stepped into the night.

The training grounds were quiet, the laughter from the barracks now a distant murmur. Nesta’s steps were light, but her mind was a storm. The dagger in her boot was a weight she both craved and resented.

*Let them come,* she had thought earlier.

Now, she wondered if she’d been wrong.

Because Cassian had let her go.

And that was far more dangerous than any blade.

---

The hut was too quiet.

Nesta lay on her pallet, fully dressed, her boots still laced, her fingers resting on the hilt of the dagger tucked into her right one. The fire had burned low, casting long, wavering shadows across the rough-hewn walls. Outside, the wind howled through the peaks, but inside, the silence was thick enough to choke on.

She had not slept. Not really. Only waited.

The barracks had gone quiet hours ago, the drunken laughter and clinking of tankards fading into the night. But she knew they would come. Knew it the way she knew the sun would rise, the way she knew the hollow ache in her chest would never quite fade.

A crunch of snow. A low murmur of voices.

Nesta’s breath steadied. Her fingers tightened around the dagger.

The door burst open.

Bellius stumbled in first, his broad frame filling the doorway, his two companions crowding behind him. Their breath reeked of ale and something fouler—spilled wine, unwashed bodies, the stink of arrogance. Their grins were wide, sloppy, their eyes glassy with drink.

"Thought you could hide, little mouse?" Bellius slurred, his words running together. He took a step forward, his boots thudding against the packed earth. "Thought you could just sit here, pretty and quiet, while the rest of us—"

Nesta didn’t move. Didn’t blink.

Bellius’s grin faltered. "What, no smart mouth? No—"

"Leave." Her voice was calm. Deadly.

The warriors laughed, but there was a edge to it now, something sharp and uncertain. Bellius took another step, his hand reaching for her. "Come on, now. Don’t be like that. We just want to—"

Nesta moved.

Faster than she ever had before, faster than she knew she could. The dagger was in her hand, flashing in the dim light, and then it was buried in Bellius’s thigh. He howled, the sound raw and animalistic, his hands flying to the wound as dark blood welled between his fingers.

The other warriors froze, their eyes wide, their faces pale.

Nesta stood slowly, the dagger dripping onto the floor. She didn’t look at the blood. Didn’t look at anything but them.

"I said leave."

For a heartbeat, no one moved. Then, with a snarl, Bellius yanked the dagger from his leg and hurled it at the wall. It embedded itself in the wood with a thunk. "You bitch," he spat, his voice shaking with pain and rage. "You fucking—"

"Get out." Nesta’s voice was ice. "Before I decide to aim higher."

The warriors backed away, their hands raised, their faces twisted in shock. Bellius clutched his bleeding thigh, his eyes burning with hatred. "This isn’t over," he snarled.

Nesta didn’t flinch. "No," she agreed. "It’s not."

The door slammed shut behind them, the sound echoing through the hut like a gunshot.

Silence.

Nesta exhaled, her breath shuddering. She bent and retrieved the dagger from the wall, wiping the blood clean on her leathers. Her hands were steady. For the first time since arriving in the mountains, she felt something other than numbness.

She felt alive.
