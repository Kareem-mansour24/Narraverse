CHAPTER 26
The Weight of Wings

The first peal of thunder rolled across the mountains like a war drum, distant but insistent. Nesta’s eyes snapped open before the sound had fully faded, her body already moving, already knowing. The air in the tent was thick with the scent of rain and iron, the promise of violence clinging to every breath.

She dressed quickly, her fingers working the laces of her leathers with practiced precision. The gray gown she’d worn upon arrival had long since been discarded—too fine, too fragile for this place. Now, she wore the clothes of a warrior, though the title still felt foreign on her tongue. The weight of the blades at her hips was familiar, at least. A comfort, in its own way.

Outside, the wind howled through the jagged peaks, carrying with it the damp bite of an approaching storm. The training grounds were quiet, the usual clatter of sparring weapons absent. Only a handful of figures stood at the edge of the cliffs, their wings tucked tight against the coming rain. Kars was among them, his broad shoulders tense as he scanned the horizon.

He turned as she approached, his dark eyes flicking over her before settling on her face. “They’re coming,” he said, voice low. “Bellius’s faction. More than we thought.”

Nesta’s stomach tightened, but she kept her expression smooth. “How many?”

“Enough.” His jaw clenched. “Enough to make this messy.”

She exhaled through her nose, forcing her fingers to uncurl from where they’d instinctively flexed at her sides. “Then we move now. Before they get any closer.”

Kars nodded, gesturing to the small group of warriors gathered behind him—those who had chosen to stand with her, despite the risks. Their loyalty was a fragile thing, she knew. But it was something.

“We’ll take the high ground,” she said, her voice steady. “Force them to come to us. If we can bottleneck them at the pass—”

“They’ll still outnumber us two to one,” one of the warriors muttered.

Nesta turned her head just enough to pin the male with a look. “Then we make every strike count.”

Silence. Then, a slow nod. Kars bared his teeth in something that wasn’t quite a smile. “And if they don’t take the bait?”

“Then we make them.” She didn’t flinch from the weight of the words. Didn’t let herself think about what that would mean.

The first drops of rain began to fall, cold and heavy, slicing through the tension like a blade. Nesta reached for the hilt of her sword, her fingers tightening around the worn leather.

The horizon darkened, the storm swallowing the last traces of dawn. And somewhere beyond it, Bellius’s army marched.

---

The wind howled across the northern cliffs, biting through the thin wool of Nesta’s gray gown. She had left the furs behind—too cumbersome, too soft. The cold was a welcome distraction, a sharpness to match the hollow ache inside her chest. Below, the training grounds sprawled like a scar upon the mountain, the jagged rocks and sparse grass offering little cover. Perfect for an ambush.

Kars moved beside her, his broad shoulders tense beneath his leathers. “They’ll be here within the hour,” he muttered, eyes scanning the horizon. The storm had passed, but the sky remained a bruised gray, heavy with the promise of more rain.

Nesta said nothing. She didn’t need to. The warriors knew the plan. They had drilled it until their muscles burned, until their blades felt like extensions of their arms. But drilling was one thing. Trust was another.

She could feel their hesitation like a physical weight. The way their eyes flickered toward her, then away. The way their grips tightened on their weapons, not from readiness, but from uncertainty. She was not one of them. Not really. She was a High Lady’s sister, a woman who had never swung a sword in earnest until a few months ago. A woman who had spent her life in silk and silver, not steel and sweat.

Torin was the youngest of them, barely more than a boy, his dark hair still damp from the last downpour. He stood apart from the others, his jaw set, but his fingers twitching at his sides. Nesta approached him slowly, her boots crunching over the frost-kissed rocks.

“You don’t have to fight for me,” she said, her voice low.

Torin’s head snapped up, his eyes widening slightly. She didn’t blame him for the surprise. She had never spoken to him directly before. She had barely spoken to any of them beyond the necessary commands.

“You don’t have to follow me,” she continued, “or swear fealty, or pretend I’m anything more than what I am.” She gestured vaguely toward the camp below. “But if you stay, you fight for this camp. For the people in it. For the ones who can’t fight for themselves.”

Torin’s throat bobbed. He was young, but he wasn’t stupid. He knew what she was offering—what she wasn’t. No grand speeches, no empty promises. Just the truth.

For a long moment, he said nothing. Then, slowly, he nodded. His hand drifted toward the dagger at his belt, fingers curling around the hilt. The blade glinted dully in the dim light, catching the faintest trace of the hidden sun.

Nesta didn’t smile. She didn’t reach out to clap him on the shoulder or offer any of the false warmth she had seen other leaders use to coax loyalty. She simply turned and walked back to her position, leaving Torin to make his choice.

Behind her, she heard the soft scuff of his boots against the rock as he fell into step beside her. She didn’t look at him. But she felt the shift in the air, the quiet solidifying of resolve.

The wind howled again, colder now. Somewhere beyond the cliffs, an army marched. And here, on this barren stretch of rock, they waited.

---

The storm clawed at the cliffs, the wind a living thing that screamed its fury into the night. Nesta stood at the edge of the training grounds, her braided coronet whipping against her sharp cheekbones, the gray gown clinging to her frame like a second skin. She had lost weight—too much of it—but the hollows beneath her eyes were not from hunger. They were from something far worse.

Something that had taken root the moment Feyre had told her she was being sent away.

The Illyrian warriors around her shifted, their wings rustling like dry leaves in the gale. They had been training for weeks, but none of them were ready. Not for this. Not for the army that marched toward them, hungry for blood and fire.

And then the wind stilled.

Not because the storm had relented, but because *he* had arrived.

Cassian landed before her, his wings spread wide, the crimson Siphons on his knuckles glowing like embers in the dark. His leathers were damp from the rain, his dark hair plastered to his forehead, but his eyes—his eyes burned with something far hotter than the storm.

"You’re leading them into a slaughter," he said, his voice cutting through the wind like a blade. "This isn’t the way."

Nesta didn’t flinch. She had spent months feeling nothing, and now, when she finally *did* feel something, it was the white-hot burn of defiance. "Then what is?" she snapped. "Letting Bellius burn this camp to the ground? Letting him take what’s left of Illyria after Hybern already carved it up?"

Cassian’s jaw clenched. "There are other ways. Smarter ways."

"Like what?" She stepped closer, her fingers curling into invisible claws. "Running? Hiding? Letting them pick us off one by one?" The words tasted like ash in her mouth. "I won’t do it. Not again."

Cassian’s wings twitched, the feathers ruffling with tension. "You’re not a soldier, Nesta."

"And you’re not their general anymore," she shot back. "You left. You *chose* to leave."

The words hung between them, sharp and jagged. The warriors around them stilled, their breaths shallow.

Then Kars stepped forward, his voice quiet but firm. "She’s right." The old warrior’s gaze didn’t waver from Cassian’s. "We don’t have a choice. Bellius won’t stop. Not until every last one of us is dead or kneeling."

Cassian’s nostrils flared. "You’re all going to die."

Nesta bared her teeth. "Then we’ll die on our feet."

The wind howled again, as if in answer. Cassian’s wings rustled, the sound like a growl building in his chest. His eyes locked onto hers, and for a heartbeat, she thought she saw something raw and unguarded in them—something that made her chest tighten.

Then it was gone.

"If you die," he said, his voice low and rough, "I’ll never forgive you."

---

The storm howled over the northern cliffs, a living thing of wind and rain that clawed at Nesta’s skin. She stood atop the slick, jagged rocks, her braided coronet dark with moisture, the weight of her sword familiar in her grip. Below, the first wave of Bellius’s warriors crested the ridge, their Siphons flaring like embers in the gloom—red, gold, violet. A warning. A promise.

Her breath came steady, despite the hollow ache in her chest. She had felt nothing for months, and yet now, as the enemy surged forward, something sharp and bright flickered in her veins. Not fear. Not rage. A cold, precise clarity.

She raised her hand.

The ropes snapped.

Boulders, balanced for days on the edge of the cliff, tumbled down the slope in a thunderous avalanche. The warriors scattered, their shouts swallowed by the storm. Some were crushed beneath the weight; others barely dodged, their boots slipping on the rain-slicked stone. Nesta didn’t wait to see the aftermath. She was already moving, leaping from the rocks to the muddy training grounds below, her warriors fanning out behind her.

Kars and Torin were at her flanks, their blades flashing. They had followed her here. Had trusted her.

The first warrior lunged, his sword aimed for her throat. She sidestepped, her own blade meeting his in a shower of sparks. Metal sang, and then he was disarmed, his weapon clattering to the ground. She didn’t kill him. Not yet.

Another came at her, this one faster, his Siphon glowing as he summoned a gust of wind to knock her off balance. She braced herself, digging her heels into the mud, and swung low. Her blade bit into his thigh, and he went down with a snarl.

Chaos reigned. The storm turned the battlefield into a nightmare of shadows and rain, the ground treacherous beneath their feet. Nesta moved through it like a ghost, her body remembering the drills Cassian had forced upon her. The strikes, the parries, the way to twist and turn to avoid a killing blow. She had hated him for it then. Now, she was grateful.

A warrior with a broken nose came at her from the side, his dagger glinting. She pivoted, her sword arcing up to meet his wrist. The dagger fell, and so did his hand. Blood sprayed, black in the dim light, and he screamed.

She didn’t flinch.

Where was Bellius?

She scanned the battlefield, her pulse steady despite the carnage. Her warriors were holding their own, but the enemy kept coming, wave after wave cresting the ridge. They were outnumbered. They had always been outnumbered.

A shout from her left. Torin was down, clutching his side, blood seeping between his fingers. Nesta sprinted toward him, her boots splashing through puddles of rain and gore. She reached him just as a warrior raised his sword for the killing blow.

She didn’t think. She lunged, her blade finding the warrior’s gut. He gasped, his eyes wide with shock, and collapsed.

Torin groaned, his face pale. “Bellius—”

“I know,” she said, her voice sharp. “Stay down.”

She turned, her breath ragged, her sword slick with rain and blood. The storm roared in her ears, but beneath it, she heard the crunch of boots on wet earth.

Too close.

A shadow moved behind her. She spun, her blade coming up—

But not fast enough.

A fist connected with her jaw, sending her sprawling into the mud. Pain exploded through her skull, bright and hot. She tasted blood. Her sword slipped from her grip, lost in the muck.

She looked up.

Bellius stood over her, his Siphon glowing a sickly green, his lips curled into a smirk. “Did you really think you could win?” he sneered.

Nesta bared her teeth, her fingers curling into the mud as she pushed herself up. The world tilted, but she forced herself to stand.

Bellius laughed, the sound lost in the storm. “You’re nothing,” he said. “Just a human playing at war.”

She didn’t answer. She didn’t need to.

Because behind him, the ridge was empty.

No more warriors.

No more reinforcements.

Just the storm. Just the blood. Just the weight of her wings—imaginary, impossible—pressing down on her back.

And then she charged.

---

The wind howled through the Illyrian peaks, a shrieking chorus that swallowed the sound of her breath. Nesta’s boots skidded on the rain-slicked stone as she twisted away from the shadow that lunged from the dark.

Too slow.

Bellius’s blade grazed her arm, a white-hot line of fire that bloomed crimson against her gray sleeve. She hissed, more in fury than pain, and staggered back. The world tilted for a heartbeat—just long enough for him to smirk, his teeth bared like an animal’s.

*"You were always going to lose."*

The words slithered into her ears, but she didn’t answer. She didn’t need to.

Her sword was already moving.

Steel met steel in a shower of sparks, the impact vibrating up her arms. Bellius was bigger, stronger, his strikes meant to crush bone. But Nesta had spent months learning how to take a hit. How to give one back.

She feinted left, then drove her knee into his gut. He grunted, doubling over just enough for her to bring her blade down in a vicious arc. He barely parried in time, his own sword trembling under the force of the blow.

Rain dripped from her braid, stinging her eyes. She blinked it away, her breath coming in sharp, controlled bursts. The wound on her arm throbbed, but she ignored it. Pain was just another sensation. Another thing to push through.

Bellius lunged again, his movements sloppy with rage. She sidestepped, her blade flashing up to catch his ribs. He snarled as the edge bit into flesh, but she didn’t let up. She pressed forward, her body moving on instinct now, years of training—of *surviving*—funneling into every strike.

He was faster than she remembered. Or maybe she was just slower, her muscles heavy with exhaustion. But she didn’t stop. She *couldn’t*.

Not when his next swing came for her throat.

Nesta dropped low, feeling the wind of the blade pass over her head. She rolled, springing up behind him, and drove her elbow into his spine. He staggered, and she used the momentum to slam her boot into the back of his knee. He went down hard, his sword clattering against the rocks.

She was on him in an instant, her blade pressed to the pulse at his throat.

His chest heaved, his dark eyes burning with hatred. But beneath it—fear.

Nesta leaned in, her voice a whisper against the storm.

*"Yield."*

---

The wind howled across the cliffs, tearing at Nesta’s braid as she stood her ground, the weight of the dagger in her hand a familiar, grounding thing. The storm above churned, lightning flickering like the embers of a dying fire, casting Bellius’ face in stark, jagged relief.

His smirk faltered.

Behind him, the warriors who had once snarled alongside him now lowered their weapons, their grips slackening. The clatter of steel against stone was swallowed by the wind, but the silence that followed was louder. Their eyes darted between Nesta and the male who had just landed beside her, his crimson Siphons blazing like brands against the gloom.

Cassian’s wings snapped wide, a shield between her and the remaining threat. His voice cut through the storm, a command that brooked no argument. *"It’s over."*

Bellius’ gaze flicked between them, his lips curling back from his teeth. The fear she had glimpsed moments before was gone, replaced by something uglier. Something that twisted his features into a mask of pure, unadulterated hatred. *"This isn’t over."*

Nesta didn’t lower her blade. The steel in her hand was steady, her breath even. She had faced worse than this. Had stared into the abyss and not flinched. *"For you,"* she said, her voice low, *"it is."*

The words hung between them, a final judgment. Bellius’ wings twitched, the muscles in his shoulders coiling like a viper ready to strike. But the warriors behind him had already stepped back, their expressions grim. Kars, his second, gave her a slow, assessing nod before turning his back on his former leader.

Bellius snarled, the sound raw and guttural. *"You haven’t won anything."*

And then his wings snapped open, the force of them sending dust and pebbles skittering across the cliff’s edge. He launched himself into the storm, the wind swallowing him whole before he could even become a shadow against the clouds.

Nesta watched him go, her fingers tightening around the dagger’s hilt. The storm raged on, indifferent.

---

The rain had slowed to a drizzle, the storm’s fury spent at last. Around her, the warriors stood in stunned silence, their breaths ragged, their blades still raised as if the battle might resume at any moment. But the field was quiet. The only sounds were the distant rumble of thunder and the soft patter of rain against stone.

Nesta’s chest heaved, her grip on the dagger loosening just enough to let the blood drip from the blade onto the muddy ground. She didn’t look at it. Didn’t look at any of them.

Then Kars stepped forward, his broad face split into a grin. “By the gods,” he breathed, clapping a hand on her shoulder. “I’ve never seen anything like that.”

She shrugged him off, her jaw tightening. The others murmured their agreement, their voices thick with awe. She ignored them, too. Let them stare. Let them whisper. She didn’t care.

Cassian’s boots appeared in her peripheral vision, his presence a weight she couldn’t shake. She didn’t turn. Didn’t acknowledge him.

“You did it,” he said, his voice low, rough.

Nesta exhaled through her nose. “We did.”

A beat of silence. The rain dripped from his dark hair, from the edges of his wings, pooling at his feet. She could feel his gaze on her, heavy and unrelenting.

“You’re hurt.” His fingers brushed the shallow cut on her arm, where Bellius’s blade had grazed her.

She jerked away before he could touch her again. “It’s nothing.”

His hand hovered in the air between them, then dropped to his side. “It’s not nothing.”

She finally turned to face him, her eyes flashing. “What do you want from me, Cassian?”

The question hung between them, sharp as a blade. His throat bobbed, but he didn’t answer.

Around them, the warriors shifted uncomfortably, sensing the storm that had nothing to do with the weather. Kars cleared his throat. “We should get back. The storm’s passing, but the winds are still rough.”

Nesta didn’t look at him. Didn’t look at any of them. Her gaze was fixed on Cassian, on the way his jaw clenched, on the way his wings twitched as if he wanted to reach for her again.

But he didn’t.

She turned away first, her braid whipping behind her as she strode toward the edge of the cliffs. The wind tugged at her hair, at the hem of her ruined gown, but she didn’t slow. Didn’t stop.

Behind her, Cassian’s hand fell to his side, his fingers curling into a fist.

The storm may have passed, but the war between them was far from over.
