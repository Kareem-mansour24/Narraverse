CHAPTER 19
The Knife in the Dark

The cold bit into Nesta’s bones before she even opened her eyes.

She had slept in her clothes, the gray wool gown clinging to her frame, the braided coronet of her hair still tight against her skull. The hut was little more than a drafty box of stone and wood, but it had been hers for the past three weeks. Or at least, it had been the closest thing to hers in this gods-forsaken place.

She exhaled, her breath curling in the frigid air, and sat up. The pallet beneath her was thin, the blanket threadbare. No fire had been lit in the hearth since yesterday, and the embers had long since died. She flexed her fingers, wincing at the stiffness in her joints. The climb up the mountain had been brutal, the warriors pushing her harder than she’d expected. Or perhaps she had simply forgotten what it was to be truly exhausted.

Outside, the camp was too quiet.

Nesta stood, her muscles protesting, and stepped to the door. The wood groaned as she pushed it open, the hinges rusted from disuse. The first hint of dawn painted the sky in shades of bruised violet, the peaks of the mountains still shrouded in darkness. But the usual clatter of the camp—the clank of weapons, the low murmur of voices, the scrape of boots on stone—was absent.

Instead, the warriors stood in small clusters, their wings tucked tight against their backs. Their eyes flicked toward her as she stepped outside, then darted away just as quickly.

Nesta’s jaw tightened. She had learned, in these past weeks, that silence from the Illyrians was never a good sign.

She wrapped her arms around herself, the cold seeping through the thin fabric of her gown. The wind howled through the peaks, carrying with it the scent of snow and pine. She had thought, once, that the mountains would be a kind of freedom. That the distance from Velaris, from Feyre, from the weight of everything she had lost, would make it easier to breathe.

She had been wrong.

A shadow detached itself from the nearest group of warriors. Kars, his dark wings half-furled, his face unreadable. He had been one of the few who had not openly sneered at her, though his courtesy was more indifference than kindness.

“You’re awake early,” he said, his voice low.

Nesta didn’t bother with pleasantries. “What’s happening?”

Kars’s gaze flicked over her shoulder, toward the cliffs beyond the camp. “Bellius is not done with you.”

The name sent a jolt through her. Bellius, the warrior who had nearly broken her arm during training two days ago. The one who had spat at her feet when Cassian had left her here, as if her presence were an insult to them all.

“He’s made that clear,” Nesta said, her voice flat.

Kars stepped closer, his wings rustling. “He’s not acting alone. There are others. They think you’re a distraction. A weakness.” His dark eyes met hers. “They think Cassian’s favor has made you untouchable.”

Nesta bared her teeth. “I don’t need Cassian’s favor.”

Kars didn’t smile. “No. But they don’t see it that way.” He glanced toward the eastern ridge, where the first pale light was creeping over the peaks. “Be careful today. The cliffs are treacherous, even for those who know them.”

Nesta followed his gaze. The wind tugged at her braid, pulling strands loose to whip against her face. “Is that a warning?”

Kars’s expression didn’t change. “It’s a fact.” He stepped back, his wings flaring slightly as he prepared to take flight. “The camp is divided. And divisions make for mistakes.”

Before she could respond, he launched himself into the air, his powerful wings beating once, twice, before he vanished into the shadows of the mountains.

Nesta stood alone in the cold, her breath visible in the air, the first true light of dawn creeping over the peaks like a slow, inevitable tide.

---

The first true light of dawn had only just begun to bleed across the peaks when Devlon’s voice cut through the crisp mountain air.

“Today,” he announced, his words carrying over the gathered warriors like a blade unsheathed, “we test the mettle of our newest recruit.”

Nesta didn’t turn to look at Cassian. She didn’t need to. She could feel the weight of his silence, the tension radiating from him like heat from a forge. He stood apart from the others, his wings slightly flared, his gloved hands flexing at his sides. But he said nothing.

Devlon’s grin was all teeth. “A mock battle in the canyon. Three against one.”

The warriors murmured, a low, eager sound. Nesta’s pulse quickened, not with fear, but with the sharp, familiar edge of fury. She had spent weeks enduring their sneers, their jabs, their thinly veiled contempt. This was not a test. This was a trap.

“Rules?” she asked, her voice steady despite the fire in her veins.

Devlon’s smile widened. “No killing. But anything else goes.”

The warriors fanned out, their wings casting long shadows over the rocky ground. Nesta stepped forward, her boots crunching on frost-hardened earth. Her body still ached from yesterday’s training—every muscle, every bone protesting—but she ignored it. She had endured worse. She had survived worse.

Bellius cracked his knuckles, his smirk twisting into something predatory. “Try not to cry, little mortal.”

Nesta bared her teeth. “I’ll try not to break your nose. Again.”

The warriors laughed, but there was no humor in it. Only hunger.

Cassian’s jaw clenched, his Siphons flaring crimson for the briefest moment before dimming. He didn’t move. Didn’t speak. He was a warrior, bound by the same codes that had shaped these men, that had shaped *him*. And yet, she knew—she *knew*—that if she fell, he would catch her.

But not before they tried to break her first.

Bellius rolled his shoulders, loosening up like a hound before the hunt. The others spread out, their wings rustling as they took their positions. Nesta exhaled, the cold air sharp in her lungs.

Let them come.

---

The first strike came like a whip-crack, a blade flashing in the dawn light.

Nesta barely twisted aside in time, the wind of its passage kissing her cheek. She didn’t have time to think before the next one came—low, aimed for her legs. She leapt, boots skidding on the frost-slicked rock, and the third blade sang past her ear.

Three of them. Bellius and his two shadows, moving like a single beast with six arms.

She had known it would be bad. Had known, from the way the warriors had watched her these past weeks, from the way their wings had rustled in anticipation when Cassian had left her here alone. But knowing didn’t make it easier when Bellius’s fist connected with her ribs, didn’t stop the air from exploding from her lungs in a pained gasp.

Nesta staggered, vision swimming, and a boot caught her in the back of the knee. She went down hard, palms scraping against the rock, the impact jarring up her arms. Before she could roll, a hand fisted in her braid and yanked her head back.

Bellius’s face loomed over hers, his breath hot and sour. “This is what happens to outsiders who don’t know their place.” His voice was a low, gloating growl, the words meant for her alone. The other warriors had gone still, their wings half-spread in anticipation.

Nesta bared her teeth. The movement split her lip further, blood welling hot and metallic against her tongue. She didn’t care. She twisted in his grip, ignoring the burn of her scalp, and drove her elbow into his ribs.

Bellius grunted, more in surprise than pain, but his hold loosened just enough. Nesta wrenched free, rolling to her feet in one fluid motion. The world tilted, then righted itself.

The warriors watching murmured, a ripple of sound that might have been surprise. Or approval.

Bellius laughed, low and dark. “Fight all you want, little mortal. It won’t change what you are.” His eyes burned, not with the cold calculation of a warrior, but with something uglier. Something that had been festering long before she’d arrived.

Nesta didn’t answer. She didn’t have to.

She lunged.

Her fist connected with his ribs again, right where she’d struck before. Bellius hissed, but he didn’t retreat. He came at her in a blur of motion, his blade flashing. Nesta dodged, but not fast enough—the edge grazed her side, a line of fire across her skin.

She ignored it. Ignored the way her lungs burned, the way her legs trembled with the effort of staying upright. She had spent months feeling nothing. Now, she felt everything—the cold air in her lungs, the sting of her split lip, the fire of her muscles as she moved.

Bellius feinted left, then drove his knee toward her stomach. Nesta twisted, taking the blow on her hip instead. Pain flared, bright and sharp, but she used the momentum to pivot, her own boot lashing out. It caught him in the thigh, hard enough to make him stagger.

The warriors watching had gone silent.

Nesta didn’t look at them. Didn’t look at anything but Bellius, at the way his lips curled back from his teeth, at the way his wings flared in warning.

She wiped the blood from her mouth with the back of her hand, her gaze never leaving his.

For the first time, she didn’t look away.

---

The wind howled across the training grounds, biting at Nesta’s sweat-slicked skin. Every breath was a knife in her ribs, every movement a battle against the weight of her own body. The world had narrowed to the dust beneath her boots, the taste of iron in her mouth, the relentless figure circling her.

Bellius lunged again, his sword a silver blur. She barely got her own blade up in time, the impact shuddering through her arms, rattling her teeth. Her muscles screamed. Her lungs burned. But she didn’t fall.

Not yet.

The warriors gathered around them had gone quiet. Even the wind seemed to hold its breath as Bellius feinted left, then drove his elbow into her side. Pain exploded through her ribs, white-hot and blinding. She staggered, her knees hitting the ground hard enough to send shockwaves up her spine. The impact knocked the air from her lungs, and she gasped, choking on the dust kicked up by the struggle.

Bellius loomed over her, his breath ragged but his eyes gleaming with triumph. “Pathetic,” he sneered. “You don’t belong here. You never did.”

Nesta’s fingers curled into the dirt, her nails biting into her palms. The pain was a living thing, coiling around her bones, whispering that he was right. That she was weak. That she should have stayed in the gutter where she belonged.

But then—

A flicker of movement. Cassian’s wings snapped taut, his Siphons flaring crimson as he took a step forward. His jaw was clenched so hard she thought it might shatter, his eyes locked on Bellius with a fury that could have burned the world.

Devlon’s hand shot out, blocking his path. “No interference,” the commander said, his voice as cold and unyielding as the mountain itself.

Cassian didn’t look at him. His gaze stayed on Nesta, his chest rising and falling too fast, as if he were the one who’d taken the blow. She could see the war in his eyes—the need to intervene, the knowledge that he couldn’t. Not without proving Bellius right.

Not without proving *her* weak.

She swallowed the blood in her mouth, the metallic tang coating her tongue. The warriors around them shifted, their earlier bloodlust replaced by something quieter. Unease. Pity. Even a few of them wouldn’t meet her eyes.

Bellius crouched in front of her, his breath hot against her face. “You should have stayed in the gutter,” he whispered, low enough that only she could hear. “You don’t belong among warriors. You don’t belong *anywhere*.”

The words slithered into her ears, wrapping around her throat, squeezing. She had heard them before—in the dark of night, in the hollow spaces between her ribs. But she had never let them win.

Not then. Not now.

She lifted her head, her vision swimming, and met his gaze. Her lips curled back from her teeth, her voice a rasp, raw and broken but unyielding. “I’m not going anywhere.”

---

The night was a living thing, slithering through the cracks of the wooden hut, whispering secrets Nesta refused to hear. The Illyrian Mountains loomed beyond the thin walls, their jagged peaks clawing at the sky like the fingers of some ancient, slumbering beast. The camp had fallen into an uneasy silence, the kind that settled over a battlefield after the last scream had faded. Only the wind dared to speak, howling through the rocks and stirring the embers of the dying cookfires into a ghostly dance.

Nesta lay on her cot, her body a map of aches and bruises. Every muscle screamed, every breath a reminder of the day’s relentless training. Cassian had pushed her harder than ever, as if he could carve the hollowness from her bones with sheer force. She had let him. She had let him because it was easier than feeling anything else.

Her fingers twitched against the rough wool blanket, the fabric scratching against her skin. Sleep should have come. Exhaustion tugged at her limbs, dragged at her eyelids. But her mind was a storm, a whirl of memories and regrets and the sharp, unyielding truth of who she had become. *Who she had been forced to become.*

She turned her head toward the door, the wood groaning beneath her weight. The hut was small, barely more than a shed, but it was hers. A fragile barrier between her and the world. Between her and *them.*

A sound.

Not the wind. Not the distant murmur of the camp’s remaining warriors settling in for the night. A footstep. Soft. Deliberate.

Nesta’s breath hitched. Her fingers stilled against the blanket, then slid beneath the thin pillow where Cassian’s dagger lay. The hilt was cool against her palm, familiar. She had refused his gift at first, had thrown the words back in his face like stones. *I don’t need your pity.* But he had left it anyway, tucked beneath her pillow like a silent challenge. *Prove me wrong.*

She had kept it.

The footsteps paused outside her door. A heartbeat of silence. Then the creak of the latch, the whisper of wood against wood as the door inched open.

Nesta moved before she could think.

She lunged from the cot, the dagger flashing in the dim light of the dying hearth. The figure in the doorway was little more than a shadow, a shifting mass of darkness, but she didn’t hesitate. She drove the blade forward with all the force of her rage, her grief, her *hate.*

The dagger found flesh.

A grunt. A hiss of breath. But the figure didn’t fall.

A gloved hand closed around her wrist, twisting it painfully. The dagger clattered to the floor, skittering across the wooden planks. Nesta snarled, baring her teeth, and drove her knee upward. She connected with something solid—thigh, hip, she didn’t care. The figure staggered, but the grip on her wrist didn’t loosen.

A voice, low and venomous, slithered into her ear.

*“You should have stayed down.”*

---

The words coiled around her throat like a noose.

Nesta didn’t think. She *moved*.

Her knee snapped up, driving into the assassin’s groin with every ounce of force she possessed. A choked grunt rewarded her, the grip on her wrist faltering just enough. She twisted free, her other hand lashing out—fingers hooked into claws, aiming for the eyes.

The figure recoiled, but not fast enough. Her nails raked across their cheek, drawing blood. A snarl tore from their throat, and then they were on her again, faster than before. A fist connected with her ribs, knocking the breath from her lungs. She staggered, but the pain only sharpened her focus. She had survived worse than this. She had survived *him*.

The thought sent a jolt of fury through her veins. She wasn’t that girl anymore. She wasn’t helpless.

Nesta lunged, her shoulder slamming into the assassin’s chest. They crashed into the wooden table, sending bowls and spoons clattering to the floor. The dagger skittered across the planks, glinting in the dim light. She didn’t hesitate. She dove for it.

Her fingers closed around the hilt just as a boot stomped down on her wrist. Pain exploded up her arm, but she gritted her teeth and twisted, driving her elbow into their shin. The assassin hissed, their weight shifting—just enough. She wrenched her arm free and rolled, springing to her feet with the dagger clenched in her fist.

They circled each other, breathing hard. The assassin’s hood had fallen back, revealing a face twisted with hatred. A woman. Nesta didn’t recognize her, but the malice in her eyes was familiar enough.

“You don’t even know why you’re here, do you?” the woman spat. “Just another pawn in someone else’s game.”

Nesta bared her teeth. “I don’t care.”

The woman lunged.

Nesta sidestepped, her blade flashing. It sliced across the assassin’s forearm, drawing a line of crimson. The woman snarled, but Nesta was already moving, her body remembering the drills Cassian had forced her through—block, strike, *feint*. She feinted left, then drove her knee into the woman’s thigh. The assassin stumbled, and Nesta didn’t give her time to recover. She kicked out, her heel connecting with the woman’s knee.

A sickening *pop* echoed through the hut. The assassin screamed, collapsing to the floor.

Nesta didn’t wait. She raised the dagger—

A roar split the night.

The door burst open, wood splintering under the force. Cassian filled the doorway, his Siphons blazing crimson, his wings flared wide. His gaze found hers first, wild and desperate. “Are you hurt?”

Nesta shook her head, her breath coming in ragged gasps. Her hands were shaking, but the dagger in her grip was steady.

Cassian’s eyes dropped to the blood on her knuckles, then to the woman writhing on the floor. His expression darkened, his jaw tightening. “Who was it?”

Nesta didn’t look away from the assassin. “I don’t know.” A pause. “But they weren’t here to talk.”

The woman spat at her feet, her face contorted with pain and fury. “You think this is over? You’re already dead, bitch. You just don’t know it yet.”

Cassian’s wings flared, his voice a growl. “This ends now.”

Outside, the camp stirred. Shouts rose in the distance, warriors rousing from their sleep, their voices sharp with alarm. The night air carried the scent of smoke and blood.

The camp was no longer safe.

For any of them.

---

The firelight flickered against the jagged rocks, casting long, wavering shadows over the gathered Illyrian warriors. Nesta stood at the edge of the circle, her arms wrapped around herself, though not from cold. The heat of the flames licked at her skin, but it was the weight of the warriors’ stares that made her shiver.

Cassian’s hand brushed against hers as he stepped forward, his wings half-spread in a silent warning. The warriors parted for him, their murmurs dying into uneasy silence. At the center of the circle, Devlon stood like a storm given form, his face a mask of barely contained fury.

“Who did this?” His voice was a whip-crack, sharp enough to draw blood.

The warriors shifted, their boots scraping against the rocky ground. Some glanced at Bellius, who stood with his arms crossed, his expression smooth as polished stone. Others avoided Nesta’s gaze entirely.

Bellius stepped forward, his voice dripping with false calm. “It wasn’t one of us.”

Nesta’s fingers curled into fists. “Liar.”

The word hung in the air, sharp and unyielding. The warriors flinched, their eyes darting between her and Bellius. A low murmur rippled through the crowd, a sound like distant thunder.

Bellius’s smile didn’t waver, but his eyes darkened. “You have no proof, girl.”

“Girl?” Nesta’s voice was a blade unsheathed. “I saw the way you looked at me when I arrived. Like I was something to be crushed under your boot. And now, suddenly, the camp is attacked? The same night I’m here?” She bared her teeth. “I may not be Illyrian, but I’m not stupid.”

Cassian’s voice cut through the tension, low and lethal. “Enough.” He didn’t look at her, didn’t need to. His presence alone was enough to still the crowd. “This isn’t about blame. It’s about survival.”

Devlon’s jaw tightened, his gaze flicking between Cassian and Bellius. “Then what do you suggest, General?”

Cassian turned to him, his Siphons glowing faintly in the firelight. “We act. Now. We double the watch. We fortify the camp. And we find out who did this before they strike again.”

Bellius scoffed. “And how do you propose we do that? The mountains are vast. The enemy could be anywhere.”

Nesta’s chest burned. “Or they could be right here.”

The warriors bristled, their hands drifting toward their weapons. Cassian’s wings flared slightly, a silent command for restraint. “Nesta’s right. We can’t rule anything out. But we also can’t turn on each other. Not now.”

Devlon’s gaze hardened, his voice a growl. “We train harder. We watch our backs. And we find out who did this.” His eyes locked onto Bellius, then swept over the crowd. “No one leaves this camp without my permission. No one.”

The warriors nodded, their faces grim. One by one, they dispersed, their voices low and urgent as they melted into the night. The fire crackled, the only sound left in the wake of their departure.

Nesta exhaled, her body trembling—not from fear, but from the sheer force of the adrenaline still coursing through her. She had expected to be ignored, dismissed. But for the first time, she hadn’t been.

Cassian stood beside her, his shoulder nearly touching hers. He didn’t look at her, didn’t speak. But he didn’t have to.

For the first time, she didn’t feel alone.
