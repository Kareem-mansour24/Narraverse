CHAPTER 9
The Weight of the Blade

The first light of dawn had not yet crept over the jagged peaks of the Illyrian Mountains when Nesta’s eyes snapped open. The hut was still shrouded in darkness, the air thick with the scent of damp earth and pine. She lay there for a moment, listening to the distant calls of waking birds, the rustle of wind through the trees. Then she moved.

Her body protested with every shift, muscles stiff from yesterday’s training. Bruises bloomed along her ribs, her thighs, her shoulders—each one a reminder of the blows she had taken, the blows she had given. She ignored them. She had learned to ignore the pain months ago.

She dressed in silence, pulling on the rough-spun tunic and leggings that had become her uniform. The fabric was coarse against her skin, but she barely noticed. Her fingers worked mechanically, braiding her hair into a tight coronet at the back of her head. The mirror above the washbasin was cracked, but she didn’t care. She didn’t look at herself anyway.

The training grounds were already alive with activity when she stepped outside. Warriors moved in loose formations, their wings rustling as they stretched and sparred. The air was crisp, the kind of cold that bit at exposed skin, but Nesta didn’t shiver. She had stopped feeling the cold a long time ago.

Devlon stood at the center of the grounds, his arms crossed over his chest, his dark eyes scanning the crowd. He waited until the last of the warriors had gathered before he spoke, his voice cutting through the murmurs like a blade.

“Today’s challenge is endurance,” he announced. “Blunted blades only. No sharp edges, no lethal strikes. You’ll be paired off, and the last pair standing wins.”

A ripple of excitement passed through the crowd. Nesta’s gaze flicked to Bellius, who stood a few paces away, his lips curled into that infuriating smirk. He had been her partner yesterday. And the day before that. And the day before that.

She didn’t react. She didn’t give him the satisfaction.

Devlon’s eyes landed on her. “Nesta. You’re with Bellius again.”

The warriors around them chuckled, a few of them elbowing each other. Nesta didn’t look at them. She didn’t look at Cassian, either, though she felt his presence like a weight at the edge of her vision. He stood apart from the others, his arms crossed, his Siphons gleaming faintly in the early light. He hadn’t spoken to her since they arrived. She hadn’t let him.

She stepped forward, her boots crunching on the frost-kissed grass. The blunted blade Devlon handed her was heavier than she expected, the hilt rough beneath her fingers. She tested its weight, her grip tightening until her knuckles turned white.

Bellius sauntered toward her, his own blade dangling loosely from his hand. “Ready to lose again, little human?”

Nesta didn’t answer. She didn’t need to.

The circle of warriors around them fell silent, the air thickening with anticipation. She could feel their eyes on her, waiting. Waiting for her to fail.

She wouldn’t.

She lifted her blade.

---

The dirt was cold beneath her boots, the morning mist clinging to her skin like a second layer of armor. Nesta tightened her grip on the training sword, the weight of it familiar now, though the ache in her ribs was not. The bruises from yesterday’s session still throbbed, a constant reminder of how little she belonged here.

Bellius smirked, rolling his shoulders as if loosening up for a dance rather than a fight. His wings, dark and heavy, flexed behind him, casting a shadow over her. "Ready to yield already, Archeron?" His voice was a low taunt, meant for her ears alone.

She didn’t answer. The Illyrian warriors gathered around the training ring shifted, their murmurs dying into silence. She could feel their skepticism, their amusement. *Let them watch.*

Bellius lunged.

His strike was fast, brutal—aimed not for her sword, but for the bruises already blossoming along her side. She twisted, barely avoiding the blow, the wind of his swing brushing her ribs. Pain flared, sharp and bright, but she gritted her teeth and countered. Steel met steel with a clang that echoed through the mountains.

Bellius laughed, low and mocking. "Still moving like a human. No wonder Cassian’s wasting his time."

Her fingers clenched around the hilt. She’d heard the whispers—*Cassian’s pet, the High Lady’s sister, the broken girl playing at war.* She’d ignored them. But Bellius’s words slithered beneath her skin, igniting something dark and familiar.

She feinted left, then drove forward, her blade aimed for his jaw. He blocked, but not fast enough. The flat of her sword connected with a satisfying crack. His head snapped to the side, his smirk faltering for the first time.

The crowd’s murmurs swelled. Bellius’s eyes darkened.

Then he struck back.

His fist came out of nowhere, slamming into her side with enough force to send her sprawling. The air left her lungs in a rush as she hit the ground, dirt spraying into her mouth. Pain exploded through her ribs, white-hot and blinding. She gasped, her vision swimming, but she forced herself up on one knee.

Bellius loomed over her, his wings flaring wide. "Yield," he growled.

Her breath came in ragged pants, her body screaming at her to stay down. But the rage—*oh, the rage*—burned hotter than the pain. She bared her teeth. "No."

His laughter rang in her ears, cruel and triumphant. "Then you’re a fool."

She lunged again.

This time, he didn’t hold back. His boot connected with her stomach, sending her crashing back into the dirt. The world tilted, her vision blurring at the edges. She tasted blood, copper and salt, and through the haze of pain, she heard the warriors’ jeers.

Then—

A shadow fell over her.

Cassian’s boots appeared in her line of sight, his wings blocking out the morning sun. His voice was a blade of its own, low and lethal. *"Enough."*

---

The training grounds were quiet now, the clamor of sparring blades and grunts of exertion fading into the crisp mountain air. Nesta wiped the sweat from her brow with the back of her hand, her breath still uneven from the match. Bellius had been a bastard, as always—his strikes too precise, his taunts too personal. But she had held her own. More than held her own.

And yet, Cassian’s boots had appeared in the dirt before her, his wings casting a shadow over her like a storm cloud.

She didn’t look up at him. Didn’t need to. She could feel the weight of his gaze, the way it always seemed to strip her bare, to see the things she tried so hard to hide.

“You fought well,” he said, his voice low, measured.

Nesta finally lifted her chin, meeting his eyes. The crimson Siphons on his knuckles glinted in the sunlight, a stark contrast to the dark leather of his gloves. His face was unreadable, but she knew that look. Knew the way his jaw tightened when he was holding back.

“But?” she prompted, her voice sharp.

His wings shifted slightly, the feathers rustling like a warning. “But you let him get to you.”

Her fingers curled into fists at her sides. “He was trying to kill me.”

“No,” Cassian said, stepping closer. “He was trying to provoke you. And you let him.”

The words stung worse than any blow Bellius had landed. Nesta’s pride flared, hot and bitter. “I handled myself just fine.”

Cassian exhaled through his nose, a sound that was half-laugh, half-frustration. “I’m not saying you didn’t. But you don’t have to prove anything to them.”

“Yes, I do.” The words tore from her, raw and unfiltered. “I have to prove I’m not weak. I have to prove I’m not—” She cut herself off, her throat tightening.

Cassian’s gaze darkened, the gold in his eyes flickering like embers. “You don’t have to prove anything to me, either.”

Nesta barked out a laugh, sharp and humorless. “Oh, spare me the noble warrior act. You don’t care if I’m strong. You just don’t want me to die.”

His wings flared slightly, as if her words had struck a nerve. “That’s not—”

“Isn’t it?” she snapped. “You’ve been following me around like a nursemaid since we got here. Watching my every move. Telling me what to do, what not to do—”

“Because you’re reckless!” His voice rose, cutting through her tirade. “Because you don’t think before you act. Because you’d rather throw yourself into a fight than admit you might need help.”

The words hung between them, heavy and suffocating. Nesta’s chest heaved, her breath coming in short, sharp gasps. She wanted to scream. Wanted to hit something—him, preferably. But she stood there, frozen, her hands clenched so tight her nails bit into her palms.

Cassian’s expression shuttered, his jaw tightening. For a heartbeat, the air between them crackled with something unspoken, something dangerous. Then, without another word, he turned on his heel and walked away.

Nesta watched him go, her heart pounding in her ears. The wind howled through the mountains, carrying the scent of pine and damp earth, but the silence between them lingered, heavy with words left unsaid. Her hands trembled at her sides, her fingers still curled into fists.

She didn’t call after him. Didn’t move.

She just stood there, alone on the training grounds, the weight of the blade between them pressing down on her chest.

---

The hut smelled of damp wood and the faint, metallic tang of blood. Nesta sat on the edge of her narrow cot, the rough wool of her training tunic chafing against her skin. She hadn’t bothered to change. Hadn’t bothered to do much of anything beyond dragging herself back here, her body screaming with every step.

The door creaked open.

She didn’t look up. Didn’t need to. The scent of pine and leather preceded him, familiar now in a way that made her jaw tighten. Kars slipped inside, his broad shoulders nearly brushing the doorframe as he shut it behind him. No knock. No announcement. Just the quiet efficiency of a warrior who knew when to speak and when to move.

He carried a roll of bandages in one hand, a small jar of salve in the other. The sight of them made her ribs throb in reminder.

Nesta didn’t protest as he knelt before her, his dark eyes scanning the bruises blooming across her arms, the split in her lip. She hadn’t even bothered to wipe the blood away. What was the point?

Kars uncorked the jar with his teeth, the sharp scent of herbs cutting through the stale air. His fingers were calloused, rough against her skin as he dabbed the salve onto her split knuckles. She hissed, more from the sting of memory than pain—Cassian’s face, twisted in frustration as she’d driven him away again. As if she were nothing.

“You’re quiet,” Kars murmured.

Nesta said nothing. The silence stretched, thick and heavy, until it pressed against her like the weight of the blade she’d left behind on the training grounds.

His hands stilled. “You want me to go?”

She exhaled through her nose. “No.”

A beat. Then his fingers resumed their work, winding the bandage around her ribs with careful precision. The pressure was firm, grounding. She watched the top of his head, the way his dark hair curled at the nape of his neck, the faint scar above his eyebrow—old, like so many of the marks he carried.

“Why do you help me?” The question slipped out before she could stop it.

Kars didn’t look up. “Because you’re not the only one who’s tired of the rules.”

She studied his face, the hard line of his jaw, the way his mouth pressed into a thin line. “What rules?”

“The ones that say we have to be this.” He gestured vaguely at the hut, the mountains beyond. “The ones that say we can’t question. Can’t want more.”

Nesta’s fingers curled into the edge of the cot. “You think I want more?”

“I think you don’t know what you want.” His gaze flicked to hers, dark and unreadable. “And that scares them.”

A laugh clawed its way up her throat, bitter and hollow. “I scare no one.”

Kars finished the bandage with a sharp tug, tying it off. His hands lingered for a moment, warm against her side. “They’re afraid of you,” he said quietly. “And that makes you dangerous.”

---

The night was a living thing, breathing cold and sharp against her skin. Nesta lay on the pallet in her hut, the rough wool blanket doing little to ward off the chill seeping through the cracks in the walls. The silence was oppressive, pressing down on her chest like a weight she couldn’t shift. She stared at the ceiling, counting the knots in the wooden beams, but her mind refused to quiet.

She had to move.

Throwing off the blanket, she rose and pulled on her boots, not bothering with a cloak. The night air would be brutal, but she welcomed it—the sting of cold, the bite of wind. Anything to feel something other than this hollow ache inside her.

The training grounds were deserted, the fires long since burned to embers. The moonlight painted the rocks in silver and shadow, turning the familiar landscape into something alien, something wild. She walked toward the cliffs, her breath misting in the air, her footsteps soundless on the frost-kissed earth.

The wind howled as she reached the edge, tugging at the loose strands of her braided coronet. Below, the valley stretched out like a dark ocean, endless and untamed. She could almost imagine herself falling, tumbling into the abyss, weightless and free.

Footsteps crunched behind her.

She didn’t turn. She didn’t need to. She knew the sound of his boots, the way the earth seemed to sigh beneath his weight.

Cassian stepped beside her, his wings folded tightly against his back, the crimson of his Siphons muted in the moonlight. He didn’t speak. Didn’t look at her. Just stood there, as if he’d always been there, as if he’d been waiting for her to come.

The wind whipped between them, carrying the scent of pine and snow and something darker, something that was purely him.

“You don’t have to stay,” she said at last, her voice rough.

“I know.”

She swallowed. The words tasted like ash. “Then why are you here?”

He was silent for so long she thought he wouldn’t answer. Then, quietly, “Because I’m not sure you should be alone right now.”

Her fingers curled into fists at her sides. “I don’t need a babysitter.”

“I never said you did.”

She turned her head then, just enough to see the sharp line of his jaw, the way the moonlight caught the angles of his face. He was looking at the horizon, his expression unreadable.

“Then what do you want, Cassian?”

His gaze flicked to hers, dark and fathomless. “I want to know why you’re out here in the middle of the night, staring at the edge of a cliff like it’s the only thing that makes sense.”

Her breath hitched. She looked away, back toward the drop, the endless dark. “Maybe it is.”

His wings shifted, just slightly, as if he were fighting the urge to reach for her. “Nesta.”

“Don’t.” The word was a blade, sharp and final. “Don’t act like you know me. Don’t act like you care.”

“I *do* care.”

She laughed, a bitter, broken sound. “Why? Because Feyre told you to? Because you feel sorry for me?”

“No.” His voice was low, rough. “Because I see the storm inside you. And I’m not sure if it’s going to destroy you or save us all.”

The wind howled between them, a living thing, wild and untamed. She turned to face him fully, her heart pounding in her chest. His eyes burned into hers, dark and unyielding.

She had no answer. No words.

So she said nothing at all.
