CHAPTER 28
The Things We Carry

The predawn air bit at Nesta’s skin as she stepped from the tent, the cold a welcome sting against the numbness that had taken root inside her. She hadn’t slept—hadn’t even tried. The cot had been too still, the silence too loud, and the weight of the past months had pressed down until she’d given up and risen, dressing in the dark with hands that moved by memory alone.

She had just fastened the last clasp of her gown when the voices reached her.

“—not her place to lead us,” one of the warriors was saying, his voice low but sharp with barely contained anger. “She’s no Illyrian. She’s no warrior.”

Nesta didn’t need to see him to know the type. Broad-shouldered, scarred, his pride as much a part of him as the sword at his hip. She recognized the tone, too—the one that had followed her all her life. The one that said she didn’t belong.

Kars stood between the warrior and the tent, his arms crossed over his chest. “She fought beside you at the battle. She bled with you.”

“And what does that matter?” another warrior scoffed. “She’s still a female. Still a mortal.”

Torin, silent until now, let out a slow breath. “She saved lives that day. More than any of you.”

Nesta stepped forward before the argument could escalate, her boots silent against the damp earth. The warriors turned, their postures shifting from irritation to something closer to wariness. She didn’t blame them. She knew what they saw—a female in a fine gown, her hair braided into a coronet that spoke of courtly refinement, not the mud and blood of war. She knew what they didn’t see—the way her fingers still remembered the weight of a blade, the way her body had moved without thought when the battle had demanded it.

She didn’t smile. Didn’t offer reassurance. She simply looked at them, her gaze steady, her expression unreadable.

“You don’t follow me,” she said at last, her voice quiet but carrying. “You follow the choice to survive.”

The words hung in the air between them, heavy with something none of them could name. The warriors exchanged glances, their earlier defiance faltering. She could see it—the way their shoulders tensed, the way their hands twitched toward their weapons, not in threat, but in uncertainty.

One of them, a male with a jagged scar across his jaw, stepped forward. His eyes were dark, his face weathered by years of war. “What do we do now?”

Nesta didn’t look away. Didn’t flinch. “We prepare.”

---

The war tent smelled of sweat and iron, the scent of too many males crammed into too small a space. Nesta stood just inside the flap, her gray gown pristine against the grime of the camp, her braided coronet immaculate despite the wind that howled beyond the canvas walls. The senior warriors shifted in their seats, their wings rustling like dry leaves, their eyes flicking between her and Cassian where he stood at the head of the table.

Devlon didn’t bother to rise. His scarred face twisted into a sneer as he spat onto the dirt floor. “This is a council of war, not a place for females.”

Nesta’s fingers curled into fists at her sides, her nails biting into her palms. She didn’t speak. Didn’t give him the satisfaction.

Cassian’s voice cut through the tent like a blade. “She stays.”

Devlon’s lip curled. “She’s a distraction. A liability. The warriors are restless—”

“Then they can take it up with me.” Cassian’s wings flared slightly, the crimson Siphons on his knuckles glowing like embers. “Unless you’d like to challenge my authority, Devlon.”

The older warrior’s jaw clenched, but he didn’t push. Not yet. Instead, he turned his glare on Nesta. “She doesn’t belong here. She doesn’t *want* to be here. Look at her—standing there like she’s too good for us.”

Nesta bared her teeth. “I *am* too good for you.”

A few of the warriors shifted uncomfortably. Cassian’s wings rustled, a low, warning sound.

Devlon surged to his feet, his chair scraping against the dirt. “You’re a spoiled bitch who doesn’t know the first thing about war. You’re not one of us. You never will be.”

Cassian’s voice was ice. “Enough.”

Devlon didn’t listen. “She’s going to get someone killed. Maybe you, maybe one of the others. But mark my words—”

“Get out.” Cassian’s voice was a whipcrack.

Devlon’s face darkened, but he didn’t argue. He stormed past Nesta, his shoulder brushing hers with deliberate force. She didn’t flinch.

The tent fell silent.

Cassian exhaled sharply, his wings settling. He didn’t look at her. “You’re not making this easy.”

Nesta’s voice was quiet, but it carried. “I’m not here to make things easy for you.”

His shoulders tensed. When he finally turned to face her, his expression was unreadable, his dark eyes searching hers. “Then what are you here for, Nesta?”

She didn’t answer.

His wings rustled again, the sound sharp with frustration.

---

The wind howled off the cliffs, biting through the thin fabric of Nesta’s tunic as she lunged forward, her blade slicing through the empty air. The training ground was deserted at this hour, the Illyrian warriors still asleep in their tents or nursing their wounds from the previous day’s drills. She didn’t care. She welcomed the solitude, the way the cold seeped into her bones and numbed the noise in her head.

Her muscles burned, but she pushed harder, driving herself through another set of forms. The sword in her hand was a familiar weight now, its edge honed to a razor’s precision. She had spent weeks learning its balance, its temper, the way it sang when it cut through the air. It was one of the few things that made sense anymore.

A shadow moved at the edge of her vision. She didn’t turn, didn’t pause. If it was Cassian, she’d ignore him until he left. If it was one of the other warriors come to gawk at the High Lady’s sister, she’d give them a reason to regret it.

But it wasn’t Cassian. The figure stepped fully into the pale morning light, and Nesta recognized the scarred face of Kars, one of the Illyrian commanders. His dark eyes assessed her with the same detached precision she’d seen him use on his own soldiers.

“You’re up early,” he said, his voice rough from years of shouting orders over the wind.

Nesta wiped the sweat from her brow with the back of her wrist, her breath steady despite the exertion. “So are you.”

A ghost of a smile touched his lips. “I don’t sleep much these days.” He gestured to the sword in her hand. “You’re improving.”

She didn’t bother with false modesty. “I know.”

Kars chuckled, low and knowing. “Cassian said you’d be trouble.”

Nesta’s grip tightened on the hilt. “Cassian talks too much.”

“He’s worried about you.”

“He shouldn’t be.”

Kars studied her for a long moment, then nodded toward the open space between them. “Spar with me.”

Nesta hesitated. She had trained with Cassian, with Azriel, even with a few of the other warriors, but never with Kars. He was a legend among the Illyrians, a warrior who had survived battles that had broken stronger men. But the challenge in his eyes was unmistakable, and she found herself nodding.

“Fine.”

Kars drew his own blade, the steel glinting in the weak sunlight. He didn’t give her time to second-guess herself, lunging forward with a speed that belied his size. Nesta parried, the impact reverberating up her arm, but she didn’t retreat. She countered, her movements fluid, instinctive. The clash of their blades echoed off the cliffs, a rhythm as old as war itself.

They moved together like dancers, a brutal, beautiful exchange of strikes and blocks. Kars was stronger, his swings designed to overwhelm, but Nesta was faster, her footwork precise. She had learned to read her opponents, to anticipate their next move before they made it. It was the only advantage she had, and she used it ruthlessly.

Sweat dripped into her eyes, stinging, but she didn’t blink. Kars feinted left, then twisted right, his blade aimed for her ribs. Nesta pivoted, deflecting the strike with her own sword, then drove her elbow into his gut. He grunted, stepping back, but there was approval in his gaze.

“You’re not the girl who arrived here,” he said, his breath coming in controlled bursts.

Nesta wiped her sleeve across her forehead, her chest heaving. “No. I’m not.”

Kars sheathed his blade, his gaze lingering on her. “Good. Because the girl who arrived here wouldn’t have survived what’s coming.”

---

The war tent smelled of sweat and iron, the scent clinging to the heavy canvas walls like a second skin. Nesta stood just inside the entrance, her gray gown immaculate despite the grime of the camp, her braided coronet as precise as if she’d dressed for court rather than a battlefield. The Illyrian warriors gathered around the central table barely spared her a glance—save for Devlon, whose lip curled in silent disdain.

Azriel was the first to look up. His shadows slid over his neck like living things, restless and watchful. The dim light caught the scars on his fingers as he set down a rolled map, the parchment whispering against the wood. “They’ve doubled their numbers,” he said, voice low. “Bellius has allied with the northern clans. They’re moving south by nightfall.”

Cassian crossed his arms, the crimson Siphons on his knuckles flaring faintly. “How many?”

“Enough to burn this camp to the ground.” Azriel’s gaze flicked to Nesta, assessing. “They’re not just after territory. They want blood.”

A muscle feathered in Cassian’s jaw. “Then we meet them head-on.”

Nesta exhaled through her nose. The weight of the tent’s silence pressed down on her, thick with the unspoken expectation that she would remain quiet, that she would let the warriors decide her fate as they had for weeks. She stepped forward, her skirts brushing against the table’s edge. “No.”

Devlon scoffed. “And what would you suggest, *lady*? A tea party?”

She ignored him, her fingers tracing the map’s contours. The northern passes were narrow, jagged things—perfect for ambushes. “They’re expecting a fight,” she said. “So we give them one. Just not the kind they’re prepared for.” Her pulse thrummed in her throat, but her voice didn’t waver. “We lure them into the ravine near the eastern ridge. The rocks there are unstable—one well-placed strike, and the whole thing comes down on top of them.”

Azriel’s brows lifted. “You’re suggesting we bury them alive.”

“If that’s what it takes.” She met his stare, unflinching. “They won’t see it coming. They’ll be too busy chasing the bait.”

Cassian’s wings shifted, the leather of his armor creaking. “And who, exactly, is volunteering to be that bait?”

Nesta didn’t hesitate. “Me.”

A beat of silence. Then Devlon barked a laugh. “You? You can barely hold a sword.”

She bared her teeth. “I don’t need a sword to outthink them.”

Azriel’s shadows coiled tighter around his throat, as if considering. Then, slowly, he nodded. “It could work. If the timing is precise.”

Cassian’s Siphons burned brighter, the crimson light painting his face in harsh lines. “It’s too risky. If they catch her—”

“Then I’ll be dead before they can blink,” Nesta cut in. “But they won’t. Because they’ll be too busy dying themselves.” She turned to Azriel. “You can scout the ravine. Confirm the weak points.”

The shadowsinger studied her for a long moment. Then, to her surprise, he inclined his head. “Done.”

Cassian’s hands flexed at his sides, his frustration a living thing. “Nesta—”

She finally looked at him, her chest tight. “Do I ever do anything half-heartedly?”

His gaze locked onto hers, a storm of pride and frustration and something deeper, something she refused to name. The tent held its breath. Then, quietly, he said, “No. You don’t.”

---

The wind off the Illyrian cliffs was a living thing, howling and snarling as it clawed at the rocks. It bit through Nesta’s gown, through the layers of her skin, straight to the bone. She didn’t move. Didn’t flinch. The darkness before her was endless, a void that mirrored the one inside her chest.

She had come here to escape the weight of the tent, the weight of the stares, the weight of *him*. But the mountains offered no solace. Only the wind, and the cold, and the vast, unyielding night.

Footsteps crunched over stone behind her. She knew who it was before he spoke. Knew the sound of his breathing, the way the wind shifted around his wings. Cassian didn’t say anything at first. He just stood there, close enough that she could feel the heat of him at her back, but not close enough to touch.

The silence stretched between them, thick and suffocating. She waited for him to speak. Waited for the anger, the frustration, the words that would cut deep. But when he finally did, his voice was raw, stripped bare.

“I don’t know how to do this with you.”

The words hung in the air, fragile as glass. Nesta turned slowly, her braided coronet catching in the wind. Cassian stood with his wings folded tight, his Siphons dull in the moonlight. His face was unguarded in a way she rarely saw—no smirk, no challenge, just exhaustion. And something else. Something that made her chest ache.

She should have had a retort. Should have snapped at him, pushed him away. But the fight had drained out of her, leaving only the hollow echo of her own voice. “Neither do I.”

No anger. No bite. Just the truth, laid bare between them.

Cassian exhaled sharply, as if she’d struck him. His gloved hands flexed at his sides, like he was fighting the urge to reach for her. “I keep thinking if I just—if I push harder, or wait longer, or say the right thing—” He cut himself off with a shake of his head. “But nothing changes.”

Nesta swallowed. The wind howled, whipping strands of hair across her face. “Maybe it’s not supposed to.”

His eyes flicked to hers, dark and searching. “What does that mean?”

She didn’t have an answer. Not one she could voice. So she just shook her head.

Cassian took a step closer, the leather of his flying gear creaking. “You’re not the only one who’s lost things, Nesta.”

The words were quiet, but they hit her like a blow. She knew he wasn’t just talking about the war. Knew he meant the years, the fights, the way they’d circled each other like predators, always too proud to yield.

She didn’t look away. Didn’t flinch. “I know.”

Something in his expression shifted. A flicker of hope, quickly smothered. He reached out, his fingers brushing her cheek—so light, so fleeting, she might have imagined it. Then he pulled back, his hand curling into a fist at his side.

The wind howled between them, carrying the sound of distant wings.

And for the first time, Nesta didn’t flinch.
