CHAPTER 13
The Weight of the Sky

The first light of dawn had not yet crept over the jagged peaks of the Illyrian Mountains when Nesta woke. She lay still for a moment, the cold seeping through the thin mattress beneath her, the weight of exhaustion pressing down like a stone. Every muscle ached, every bone throbbed. The mock battle yesterday had been brutal, a relentless onslaught of blows and taunts, and though she had held her own, the bruises now blooming across her skin were a bitter reminder of how far she still had to go.

She sat up slowly, her breath misting in the frigid air. The hut was silent, the only sound the distant howl of the wind against the cliffs. She dressed quickly, pulling on the same gray gown she had worn the day before, the fabric stiff with dried sweat. Her fingers trembled as she braided her hair into its usual coronet, the familiar motion doing little to steady her nerves.

Outside, the training grounds were already stirring. The warriors moved in clusters, their laughter sharp and mocking as they stretched and sparred. Nesta ignored them, her jaw set as she made her way to the edge of the cliff where Devlon stood, his arms crossed over his broad chest.

"Today," Devlon announced, his voice carrying over the murmurs of the gathered warriors, "we test endurance. A winged race—from here to the eastern ridge and back. You will carry a weighted pack, and you will not drop it. Not once."

The warriors grinned, their eyes gleaming with anticipation. Nesta knew what this meant. They wanted to see her fail. They wanted to watch her struggle, to prove that she did not belong here.

"And if we do drop it?" she asked, her voice steady despite the tremor in her hands.

Devlon’s lips curled into a smirk. "Then you start again. Until you finish."

Nesta swallowed hard. She had seen the packs—leather sacks filled with stones, strapped tightly to the warriors’ backs. The weight alone would be enough to drag her down, but the flight… She had never flown with such a burden. She had barely flown at all.

The warriors were already strapping on their packs, their wings flexing as they prepared for the challenge. Nesta stepped forward, her chin lifted, and took the pack Devlon handed her. The weight settled onto her shoulders like an anchor, pulling her down before she had even taken a step.

Bellius sauntered over, his grin widening as he took in her stance. "Let’s see how long you last, little mouse."

Nesta said nothing. She adjusted the straps of the pack, her fingers brushing over the rough leather, and turned toward the starting line. The wind howled around her, biting at her exposed skin, but she did not flinch. She would not give them the satisfaction.

Not today.

---

The horn’s blast still rang in her ears as Nesta lunged forward, the weighted pack digging into her shoulders like a pair of iron claws. The ground beneath her boots was uneven, rocky—meant for wings, not feet. Above her, the Illyrian warriors took to the sky with a grace that made her stomach twist. Their laughter carried on the wind, sharp and mocking.

She didn’t look up. Couldn’t.

Her breath came in ragged gasps, her lungs burning with every step. The pack was too heavy, the straps cutting into her collarbones, but she refused to adjust them. Refused to show weakness.

A shadow passed over her, and then the wind hit her like a fist. Dust and gravel sprayed her face as Bellius dove low, his wings stirring the air into a storm. She stumbled, her ankle twisting on a loose stone, but she caught herself before she could fall. The warriors above hooted, their voices a chorus of derision.

“Run, little mouse!” Bellius taunted, circling back. “Or are you already tired?”

Nesta bared her teeth but said nothing. She wouldn’t give him the satisfaction of a response. Instead, she pushed harder, her thighs screaming in protest. The finish line was still so far away.

Another warrior—Kars—swooped in from the side, his wings nearly clipping her shoulder. She ducked instinctively, her pulse roaring in her ears. They were playing with her. Toying with her like a cat with a wounded bird.

She hated them. Hated the sky, the wind, the way the mountains seemed to stretch endlessly before her. Hated that she was here at all.

But most of all, she hated that Cassian was watching.

She could feel his gaze on her, heavy and unreadable. He stood apart from the others, his arms crossed over his chest, his Siphons glinting in the morning light. His jaw was tight, his expression carefully blank, but she knew him well enough to see the tension in his shoulders. The way his fingers flexed at his sides, as if he were fighting the urge to intervene.

She didn’t need his help. Didn’t want it.

The ground sloped upward, the path narrowing between two jagged rocks. Nesta’s legs trembled as she forced herself forward, her boots slipping on loose gravel. The pack felt like a boulder now, dragging her down with every step.

Bellius landed in front of her, his wings flaring wide to block her path. She skidded to a halt, her chest heaving.

“Had enough?” he sneered.

She said nothing. Just glared at him, her hands curling into fists at her sides.

He laughed, low and cruel. “This is what happens when you try to be something you’re not.”

For a heartbeat, she wavered. The weight of the pack, the burn in her muscles, the exhaustion clawing at her bones—it all threatened to pull her under. Her vision swam, dark spots dancing at the edges.

Then she forced herself to stand taller.

“I’m not done,” she rasped.

Bellius’s smirk faltered for the briefest moment. Then he bared his teeth. “We’ll see about that.”

He launched himself back into the sky, his wings beating the air as he rejoined the others. Nesta didn’t watch him go. She just turned back to the path, her legs shaking, and took another step forward.

One step. Then another.

She would not break. Not today.

---

The wind howled across the training grounds, biting at Nesta’s skin as she staggered toward the finish line. Her lungs burned, her legs trembling with every step, but she refused to falter. Not in front of them.

Devlon stood at the end of the path, his arms crossed over his broad chest, his face unreadable. The warriors around him murmured, their voices a low hum of approval and disdain. Bellius stood beside him, his chest puffed out, a smug grin on his face.

Nesta’s vision blurred as she forced herself forward, her body screaming in protest. She had pushed too hard—again. But she would not let them see her break. Not now. Not ever.

“Bellius takes the win,” Devlon declared, his voice cutting through the wind. The warriors around him cheered, their voices a mix of respect and mockery. Nesta didn’t look at them. She didn’t need to see their faces to know what they thought of her.

She reached the finish line, her breath coming in ragged gasps, her body trembling with exhaustion. She bent over, her hands on her knees, her lungs burning as she tried to catch her breath. The cold air stung her throat, but she refused to let the pain show.

Footsteps approached, heavy and deliberate. She didn’t need to look up to know who it was.

“You pushed too hard,” Cassian said, his voice low, rough with concern.

Nesta straightened, her body protesting the movement, but she forced herself to stand tall. She turned to face him, her eyes flashing with defiance. “I didn’t ask for your concern.”

Cassian’s gaze darkened, his jaw tightening. “No. But you *need* it.”

The words hung between them, heavy with unspoken tension. Nesta’s chest ached, but she refused to let him see the effect his words had on her. She turned away, her body screaming in protest as she took a step forward.

The warriors around them had dispersed, their voices fading into the wind. Only Cassian remained, his presence a constant, unwelcome shadow.

“You’re going to break yourself,” he growled, his voice a low rumble behind her.

Nesta didn’t turn. She kept walking, her steps heavy, her body aching with every movement. “Then let me.”

---

The hut smelled of damp wool and old smoke. Nesta shoved the door open with her shoulder, the wood groaning in protest. Her arms trembled—not from the weight of the door, but from the weight of the sky pressing down on her. Every muscle screamed, every breath a rasp in her throat. She hadn’t realized how much she’d been holding until she stepped inside and the tension in her spine unraveled like a frayed thread.

Kars was already there, perched on the edge of her pallet like a silent sentinel. The jar of salve in his hands glinted in the dim light filtering through the cracks in the walls. He didn’t look up as she entered, didn’t speak. Just lifted his chin toward the stool beside him.

Nesta hesitated. The last thing she wanted was to sit, to let her body fold in on itself like a broken thing. But the exhaustion won. She sank onto the stool with a sharp exhale, her legs protesting the movement.

Kars uncorked the jar. The scent of mint and something earthy filled the air between them. He dipped his fingers into the salve, the movement deliberate, unhurried. Then his hands were on her, peeling back the torn edges of her tunic where the fabric had stuck to the bloodied cuts beneath.

She hissed at the first touch, her fingers curling into fists in her lap.

“You’re lucky,” Kars murmured, his voice rough but not unkind. “These aren’t deep.”

Nesta said nothing. She didn’t feel lucky.

His fingers worked in silence for a moment, spreading the salve over the raw skin of her shoulders, her back. The coolness of it was a small relief, but not enough to dull the throb of her muscles, the ache in her bones.

“You’re not weak,” he said suddenly.

Nesta stiffened. The words were too close to the ones she’d been hearing in her own head for months. *Weak. Useless. Broken.*

Kars didn’t look at her as he continued, his hands still moving with practiced ease. “But you’re fighting the wrong battle.”

She swallowed. “What’s the right one?”

His fingers stilled. He set the jar down on the pallet between them, the glass clinking softly against the wood. Then he met her gaze, his dark eyes unreadable. “Learning to trust yourself.”

The words settled over her like a weight. Not the crushing kind—the kind that pressed down just enough to make her feel the ground beneath her feet.

Nesta opened her mouth, then closed it. She had no answer.

Kars didn’t wait for one. He picked up the roll of bandages and began winding them around her ribs, his touch careful, precise. “You think this is about them,” he said, nodding toward the door, toward the training grounds beyond. “Proving something. But it’s not.”

She exhaled sharply. “Then what is it about?”

He tied off the bandage with a quick tug. “You.”

The silence that followed was thick, heavy. Nesta stared at the dirt floor, at the scuffed toes of her boots. She wanted to argue, to tell him he was wrong. But the words wouldn’t come.

Kars stood, gathering the jar and the remaining bandages. He moved toward the door, then paused, his hand on the latch. “Rest,” he said, his voice quieter now. “You’ll need it.”

Then he was gone, the door clicking shut behind him.

Nesta didn’t move. She sat there, her body aching, her mind spinning. The hut felt smaller now, the walls pressing in. She stood slowly, her legs unsteady, and made her way to the pallet. The straw crunched beneath her as she lay down, her back protesting every movement.

She stared up at the thatched roof, at the cracks where the moonlight seeped through. Kars’s words echoed in her head, a silent question.

*What if the real battle isn’t out there—it’s in here?*

---

The night air was a blade against her skin, sharp and unrelenting. Nesta stepped out of the hut, the wooden door groaning shut behind her, sealing away the dim warmth within. The Illyrian Mountains stretched endlessly around her, their jagged peaks clawing at the sky like the fingers of some ancient, slumbering beast. The wind howled, carrying with it the scent of pine and frost, and for a moment, she let it bite at her cheeks, let it remind her that she was still alive.

Still breathing.

Still hollow.

She walked toward the cliffs, her boots crunching over the frozen earth. The training grounds were silent now, the warriors long since retired to their huts or the warmth of the hearth. Only the stars bore witness to her restless pacing, their cold light casting long shadows at her feet. She stopped at the edge of the precipice, the drop yawning before her like an open maw. The wind tugged at her braided coronet, pulling strands loose to whip across her face.

*What if the real battle isn’t out there—it’s in here?*

The question coiled in her mind, a serpent waiting to strike. She had spent months running from it, drowning it in wine and silence and the sharp, brittle edges of her own rage. But here, in the quiet of the night, there was nowhere to hide. The emptiness inside her was a chasm, vast and echoing, and she was tired—so gods-damned tired—of pretending it didn’t exist.

A rustle of wings cut through the stillness.

Nesta didn’t turn. She didn’t need to. She knew the sound of his approach, the way the air shifted when he was near. Cassian landed lightly behind her, his boots barely making a sound against the rocky ground. His wings folded tightly against his back, the crimson Siphons on his knuckles glowing faintly in the dark like embers.

For a long moment, neither of them spoke. The wind howled between them, carrying the weight of all the things they had left unsaid.

Then, finally, Nesta broke the silence. Her voice was barely a whisper, lost almost instantly to the night. "Why do you care?"

Cassian didn’t flinch. Didn’t look away. His gaze remained fixed on the horizon, where the first hints of dawn were beginning to bleed into the sky. "Because I see what you’re capable of." His voice was low, rough with something she couldn’t name. "And I’m not going to let you throw it away."

The words struck her like a physical blow. Her breath caught, her chest tightening as if his hands were wrapped around her ribs, squeezing. She wanted to lash out, to snarl at him that he didn’t know her, didn’t know the first thing about what she was capable of—or what she wasn’t. But the words died on her lips, choked by the sudden, traitorous sting of tears behind her eyes.

She swallowed hard. "You don’t get to decide that."

Cassian turned his head then, his dark eyes meeting hers. They burned with an intensity that made her skin prickle, made her want to look away. But she didn’t. She couldn’t.

"I’m not trying to decide anything for you, Nesta," he said, his voice raw. "I’m trying to remind you that you don’t have to do this alone."

The wind howled between them, a living thing, as if the mountain itself was holding its breath.

Nesta’s hands trembled at her sides. She clenched them into fists, her nails biting into her palms. "I don’t know how to let you in."

The admission tore from her, raw and bleeding. She hated the vulnerability in it, hated the way it made her feel exposed, like a wound laid bare. But Cassian didn’t flinch. Didn’t look away.

He stepped closer, the heat of him cutting through the cold like a brand. His fingers brushed against hers, tentative, as if he expected her to pull away. When she didn’t, he laced his fingers through hers, his calloused skin rough against her own.

"Then let me show you," he said.
