CHAPTER 27
The Silence Between Us

The tent flap was cold against her cheek when she rolled onto her side, the rough wool of the cot scratching at her bare arms. Nesta didn’t remember lying down. Didn’t remember much of anything after the fighting stopped, after the blood had dried black on her skin and the screaming had faded into the kind of silence that pressed against her ears like a physical weight.

She sat up, her body protesting with a chorus of aches—deep in her bones, in the muscles she hadn’t known she possessed until they’d been pushed to their limits. The camp was quiet, the kind of predawn stillness that made the world feel suspended between one breath and the next. The scent of rain hung heavy in the air, mingling with the acrid tang of smoke that still clung to the mountainside.

Nesta dressed quickly, her fingers moving with practiced efficiency despite the stiffness in her joints. The gray gown she pulled over her head was immaculate, as if the blood and grime of yesterday had never touched it. She braided her hair back into its coronet, the motion automatic, her reflection in the small mirror warped by the uneven surface.

Outside, the training grounds were empty save for a few warriors moving like ghosts between the tents. Their eyes flicked toward her as she passed, some lingering a beat too long, others darting away as if she might burn them with a glance. She ignored them all, her boots crunching over the frost-kissed earth.

The fire pits were cold, the embers long since reduced to ash. She wondered if anyone had slept at all.

“You’re up early.”

Kars fell into step beside her, his wings tucked tight against his back. He was one of the few who didn’t flinch when she looked at him.

“Couldn’t sleep,” she said, the words coming out rough.

Kars grunted, his dark eyes scanning the camp. “Few of us could.” A pause. “Devlon called a meeting. In the war tent.”

Nesta’s fingers curled into fists at her sides. “About?”

“Bellius’s lot.” Kars’s voice was low, careful. “They’re demanding retribution. Saying the battle wasn’t fought fair.”

A laugh clawed its way up her throat, sharp and bitter. “Fair.” As if anything about war was fair. As if she hadn’t spent the last months learning exactly how unfair it could be.

Kars didn’t smile. “Devlon’s divided. Some of the others, too.”

“And you?”

He hesitated. “I saw what you did out there. What you became.” His throat bobbed. “But they don’t care about that. They care about the old ways. The old rules.”

Nesta said nothing. She didn’t need to. The old ways had nearly gotten them all killed. The old rules had been written by men who saw her as nothing more than a liability, a woman playing at a warrior’s game.

The war tent loomed ahead, its canvas sides rippling in the cold wind. Voices spilled out from within—low, heated, the kind of tone that carried the weight of a blade unsheathed.

Kars stopped just outside, his hand brushing her arm. “They won’t listen to reason,” he said quietly. “Not about this.”

Nesta met his gaze. “When have they ever?”

He didn’t answer. He didn’t need to.

Inside, the air was thick with the scent of sweat and iron, the kind of tension that made her skin prickle. Devlon stood at the head of the table, his face a mask of controlled fury. Around him, the other commanders shifted, their wings rustling like restless birds.

And then there were the others. The ones who glared at her as she entered, their lips curling back from their teeth.

Devlon’s eyes locked onto hers, cold and assessing. “You’re late.”

Nesta didn’t bother with a response. She took her place at the edge of the circle, her spine straight, her chin lifted. Let them look. Let them stare.

One of Bellius’s men—a hulking brute with a scar running down his cheek—stepped forward. “This isn’t over,” he spat. “She broke the code. She fought like an animal.”

A murmur rippled through the tent. Nesta didn’t move. Didn’t react.

Devlon’s voice cut through the noise, sharp as a whip. “She saved your lives.”

“She’s not one of us,” another snarled. “She never will be.”

Nesta let the words wash over her, let them slide off her skin like rain. She had heard it all before. The sneers, the jeers, the way they said *woman* like it was a curse.

Kars shifted beside her, his shoulder brushing hers. A silent warning.

Devlon’s gaze flicked between them, his jaw tightening. “Enough.”

But the damage was already done. The air in the tent had shifted, the tension coiling tighter, like a bowstring pulled to its limit.

Kars leaned in, his voice barely a breath. “Devlon won’t let this go. Neither will they.”

---

The war tent stank of sweat and old leather, the scent of Illyrian masculinity so thick it coated her tongue. Nesta stepped inside, the canvas flaps whispering shut behind her, and the murmurs died as if she’d slit their throats.

Devlon stood at the head of the long table, his arms crossed over his broad chest, his face twisted in a sneer. The other warriors—senior males with scars and scowls—watched her with varying degrees of hostility. Cassian was there too, leaning against the far wall, his wings tucked tight, his crimson Siphons glinting in the dim light. His jaw was set, but his eyes… they were on her, sharp and assessing.

“Well,” Devlon drawled, the word dripping with venom. “Look what the wind blew in.”

Nesta didn’t flinch. She lifted her chin, meeting his gaze head-on. “I’m here to discuss the fire.”

Devlon’s laugh was a bark, ugly and mocking. “Oh, we’re discussing it. Starting with how you undermined my authority in front of the entire camp.”

The warriors shifted, their eyes flicking between her and Devlon like spectators at a blood sport. Cassian pushed off the wall, his boots thudding against the dirt floor, but Nesta spoke before he could open his mouth.

“I did what was necessary to save this camp,” she said, her voice cold, precise. “If you’d rather it burned, say so now.”

Silence.

Devlon’s face darkened, his nostrils flaring. “You don’t give the orders here, girl.”

“No,” Nesta agreed, stepping forward. The hem of her gray gown brushed against the dirt, immaculate despite the filth around her. “But I don’t take them, either.”

A muscle twitched in Devlon’s jaw. The warriors were watching her now, not him. She could feel their hesitation, their curiosity. Cassian’s gaze burned into her side, but she didn’t look at him. She didn’t need his defense.

“You think you’re clever,” Devlon spat. “Playing the hero while the rest of us clean up your mess.”

Nesta bared her teeth. “I didn’t ask for your approval. I acted. If you have a problem with that, take it up with Rhysand.”

Devlon’s smirk was a blade. “Oh, we will.” He leaned forward, his voice dropping to a growl. “You’ve made your bed, girl. Now lie in it.”

---

The war tent’s flap snapped shut behind her, the wind howling through the Illyrian peaks like a living thing. Nesta didn’t wait to see if Cassian followed. She knew he would.

Her boots crunched over frost-bitten rock as she strode toward the cliff’s edge, the gray gown she wore—immaculate, as always—fluttering against her legs. The cold should have bothered her. It didn’t. Nothing did.

Footsteps sounded behind her, deliberate, unhurried. She didn’t turn.

“Nesta.”

His voice was rough, the kind of roughness that came from years of shouting orders over battlefields, from swallowing down words he’d rather say. She kept walking.

“Don’t.” The word was flat, final. “Not now.”

A hand closed around her arm, warm even through the layers of fabric. She stopped, but didn’t look at him. The horizon stretched before her, endless and empty, the sky a bruise of purples and blues as dusk bled into night.

“You can’t just walk away every time it gets hard,” Cassian said.

She laughed, sharp and humorless. “Watch me.”

His grip tightened, not enough to hurt, but enough to make it clear he wasn’t letting go. “This isn’t you.”

“No?” She turned then, yanking her arm free. His crimson Siphons glinted in the fading light, the only color in the monochrome world around them. “Then who the hell am I, Cassian? Because I don’t know anymore.”

His jaw clenched. “You’re Nesta Archeron. Stubborn, infuriating, brilliant—”

“Stop.” The word cracked like a whip. “Just stop.”

The wind tore between them, carrying the scent of pine and snow and something darker, something like regret. Cassian’s wings rustled, the feathers shifting as if they, too, were restless. “You can’t keep pushing everyone away.”

“Why not?” She bared her teeth. “It’s easier.”

“Easier for who?” His voice rose, frustration bleeding through. “For you? Because it sure as hell isn’t easier for the rest of us.”

She wanted to scream. Wanted to claw at the hollow space inside her chest where her heart should have been. But all she could do was stare at him, at the warrior who had seen her at her worst and still refused to look away. “What do you want from me?”

His throat worked. “I want you to fight.”

“I *am* fighting.”

“No.” His voice dropped, raw and quiet. “You’re surrendering.”

The words hit like a physical blow. She took a step back, her heel brushing the edge of the cliff. The drop was sheer, endless. One wrong move and she’d fall. Just like she always did.

Cassian reached for her again, but she flinched away. His hand hovered in the space between them, empty. “Nesta—”

“Don’t.” Her voice broke. “Please.”

For a heartbeat, neither of them moved. Then his wings shifted, the feathers whispering against the wind as he stepped back. His eyes burned into hers, dark and unreadable. “I’m not your enemy, Nesta.”

The words settled between them, heavy and final. She didn’t answer. She couldn’t.

And then he was gone, the wind swallowing the sound of his retreat. Leaving her alone with the silence.

---

The war tent smelled of iron and sweat, the scent clinging to the canvas walls like a second skin. Nesta stepped inside, her gray gown whispering against the dirt floor, the fabric too fine for this place of blood and bone. Torin sat on a low stool, his back to her, fingers working deftly to wrap a strip of linen around his forearm. The wound beneath was still weeping, dark and angry.

He stiffened at the sound of her approach, his shoulders tensing beneath the leather straps of his armor. But he didn’t turn.

Nesta stopped a few paces away, studying the way his hands moved—steady, practiced. The same hands that had fought beside her in the skies, that had held a sword with the precision of a man twice his age. She had seen him that day, seen the way he had moved through the battle like a shadow, like death itself. And when she had called for retreat, he had been the first to follow.

“You’re going to need stitches,” she said.

Torin’s fingers stilled. Then, slowly, he turned his head just enough to glance at her over his shoulder. His face was younger than she remembered, the lines of exhaustion etched deep around his mouth. “It’s not the first time.”

Nesta exhaled through her nose. “And it won’t be the last, if you keep fighting like that.”

A ghost of a smile touched his lips, there and gone. “You sound like my mother.”

She didn’t smile back. “Your mother would be right.”

Silence stretched between them, thick and heavy. Torin finished tying off the bandage and flexed his fingers, testing the give of the linen. Then, finally, he turned to face her fully. His dark eyes were wary, but not unkind. “Did you need something, my lady?”

My lady. The title sat wrong on her tongue, even when it wasn’t hers to speak. She ignored it. “I wanted to ask you something.”

Torin waited.

“Why did you fight for me?” The question came out sharper than she intended. “You didn’t know me. You didn’t owe me anything.”

He blinked, as if the answer should have been obvious. Then, slowly, he shook his head. “Because you didn’t ask for permission.” His voice was quiet, but it carried the weight of something unshakable. “You just did what needed to be done.”

The words struck her like a physical blow. She had expected excuses, flattery, something polished and rehearsed. Not this. Not the raw, unvarnished truth.

Nesta swallowed. “And if I lead you into something worse next time?”

Torin’s gaze didn’t waver. “Then we’ll face it together.”

She had no answer for that. No clever retort, no biting remark. Just the strange, hollow ache in her chest, the one that had been there since the war ended, since she had realized that surviving didn’t mean living.

Nesta held out her hand.

Torin looked at it, then at her. A beat of hesitation. Then he reached out and took it, his calloused fingers closing around hers.

“We’ll do it again,” she said, her voice steadier than she felt.

Torin’s grip tightened. “For the camp.”

---

The wind howled across the cliffs, sharp as a blade and twice as cold. It bit into Nesta’s skin, but she didn’t flinch. She welcomed the sting, the way it numbed her fingers and made her breath come in ragged white clouds. Below, the Illyrian camp sprawled like a wound in the earth, fires flickering like dying stars. She had spent the last hour standing here, watching the darkness swallow the mountains whole.

She didn’t hear him approach. She *felt* him.

The shift in the air, the way the wind seemed to still for half a breath before his presence pressed against her back. She didn’t turn. She didn’t need to.

Cassian’s voice was rough when he finally spoke, the words scraping out of him like they’d been lodged there for too long. “You shouldn’t be out here alone.”

Nesta exhaled through her nose. “I’m not alone.”

A beat of silence. Then, quieter, “No. I suppose you’re not.”

The wind carried the scent of him—leather and steel and something wild, something that had once made her pulse quicken. Now, it only made her chest ache. She clenched her jaw, willing the feeling away.

“I don’t know how to do this anymore.”

The words hung between them, raw and unguarded. Nesta turned then, slowly, her braided coronet catching the faint moonlight. Cassian stood a few paces away, his wings half-furled, the crimson Siphons on his knuckles dull in the dark. His face was all sharp angles, his jaw shadowed with stubble, his eyes—his eyes were haunted.

She should have said something cutting. Should have driven him away with a look, a word, anything to keep the distance between them. But the wind howled again, and the sound of it was too much like the silence inside her.

“Neither do I.”

The admission slipped out before she could stop it. Cassian’s breath hitched, just for a second. His fingers twitched at his sides, like he wanted to reach for her but didn’t dare.

She didn’t either.

The space between them was a chasm, wide and deep and filled with all the things they couldn’t say. All the things they *had* said, in anger, in pain, in moments when the world had pressed too hard and they had lashed out instead of holding on.

Cassian’s hand lifted, just an inch. His fingers brushed against hers, so faintly she might have imagined it. Then he pulled back, like even that small contact had burned him.

The wind howled again, and this time, it carried something else—the distant sound of wings. Nesta’s gaze snapped to the sky, but there was nothing. Only darkness.

Cassian’s voice was low, urgent. “Someone’s watching.”

She knew. She *felt* it, the way she felt the cold, the way she felt the hollow space inside her chest where her heart should have been.

The reckoning was far from over.
