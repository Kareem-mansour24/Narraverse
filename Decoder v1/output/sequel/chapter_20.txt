CHAPTER 20
The Blood That Binds

The cold bit into her bones before the sun had even thought to rise.

Nesta woke with a gasp, her body screaming in protest as she pushed herself up from the thin pallet. Every muscle ached, every bruise a fresh reminder of the knife that had come for her throat in the dark. She exhaled sharply, her breath curling in the frigid air of the hut, and forced herself to stand.

The camp was silent—too silent. No clatter of weapons, no grunts of sparring warriors, no laughter. Just the whisper of wind through the cracks in the wooden walls, as if the mountains themselves were holding their breath.

She dressed quickly, the gray gown clinging to her frame, the fabric too fine for this place of sweat and blood. Her fingers trembled as she braided her hair into its usual coronet, the motions automatic, her mind elsewhere. The assassin’s face flashed behind her eyes—pale, desperate, those fingers closing around her throat. She had killed him. And worse, she had *wanted* to.

Nesta flexed her hands, the phantom sensation of his blood still warm on her skin.

The training grounds were empty when she stepped outside, the frost crunching beneath her boots. The warriors who usually filled the space were absent, their usual posturing replaced by something far more unsettling: *silence*. She could feel their eyes on her, though. Watching from the shadows of their huts, from behind half-drawn curtains. Some with fear, others with something like awe. As if she were a wild animal they weren’t sure was tame.

She ignored them, moving to the weapons rack. The weight of the sword in her hands was familiar now, grounding. She swung it in slow, controlled arcs, the blade cutting through the dawn air with a whisper. The rhythm soothed the chaos in her chest, if only for a moment.

“You’re up early.”

She didn’t turn at the sound of Cassian’s voice. Didn’t stop moving. “Couldn’t sleep.”

A pause. Then the rustle of wings as he landed beside her, his boots thudding against the frozen earth. He was dressed for flight, his leathers creaking with the movement, the crimson Siphons on his knuckles dull in the dim light. “You should’ve rested.”

She shot him a glare. “I don’t need your concern.”

His jaw tightened, but he didn’t rise to the bait. “Devlon called a meeting. The challenge is canceled.”

Nesta lowered the sword, her fingers flexing around the hilt. “Why?”

Cassian’s wings shifted, the feathers ruffling in the wind. “Because someone tried to kill you last night. Because the warriors are on edge. Because no one knows what the hell to do with you now.”

She bared her teeth. “I can handle myself.”

“I know.” His voice was quiet, too quiet. “But that doesn’t mean you’re safe here.”

The words settled over her like a shroud. She turned away, staring out at the cliffs, the jagged edges of the mountains cutting into the pale sky. The wind howled, carrying the scent of snow and pine. “Where, then?” she asked, her voice hollow. “Where am I safe, Cassian?”

He didn’t answer. He didn’t have to.

Nesta’s fingers curled into fists, her nails biting into her palms.

---

The morning air was thin and sharp, biting at Nesta’s lungs as she stood at the edge of the central clearing. The Illyrian warriors had gathered in loose ranks, their wings rustling like dry leaves in the wind. She could feel their eyes on her—some curious, some hostile, all of them weighing her worth.

Devlon stepped forward, his broad frame cutting through the crowd like a blade through flesh. The murmurs died instantly, the warriors falling into silence as their commander raised a hand.

“An investigation will be conducted into the attack on the female,” Devlon announced, his voice carrying across the clearing. His tone was flat, dismissive. “But it was likely an outsider. Someone who slipped in unnoticed.”

Nesta’s jaw tightened. She didn’t need his pity, his half-hearted assurances. She needed answers.

“An outsider?” she said, her voice cutting through the stillness. “You think someone just wandered into your camp, past your patrols, past your sentries, and decided to take a shot at me?”

Devlon’s eyes flicked to her, cold and unyielding. “It’s possible.”

“Bullshit.” The word was a whipcrack. The warriors shifted, their wings twitching. She didn’t care. “You know as well as I do that this wasn’t some random thief. This was someone in your camp. Someone who knew exactly where I’d be.”

Bellius smirked from the front row, his gaze locked onto hers like a predator sizing up prey. She bared her teeth at him.

Cassian stepped forward then, his crimson Siphons gleaming in the pale light. His wings were half-spread, a silent warning to anyone who might think to challenge him. “This wasn’t an outsider,” he said, his voice a command. “And we will find out who did this.”

Devlon’s mouth twisted, but he didn’t argue. He didn’t dare.

The warriors murmured, their loyalty fractured. Some nodded in agreement with Cassian, others glared at Nesta as if she were the problem.

Bellius’s voice cut through the noise, smooth and mocking. “Or maybe she’s making it up. To gain sympathy.”

The words hung in the air, heavy and ugly. Nesta’s breath came fast, her fingers curling into invisible claws. She didn’t look at him. She didn’t have to.

“I don’t need sympathy,” she said, her voice a whisper that carried farther than she intended. “I need a weapon.”

---

The wind howled through the jagged peaks of the Illyrian Mountains, biting at Nesta’s exposed skin like a thousand tiny teeth. She stood at the edge of the training camp, the gray gown she wore—immaculate despite the grime of the place—fluttering around her legs. The fabric was too thin for this altitude, for the cruelty of the wind, but she refused to acknowledge the cold. Refused to let it touch the hollow space inside her chest where something vital had once been.

Behind her, the camp buzzed with the usual clamor of male voices, the clatter of weapons, the occasional thud of a body hitting the ground. She didn’t turn. Didn’t care. The only thing that mattered was the weight of the dagger at her hip—a poor substitute for the sword she truly wanted, but it would have to do.

A shadow fell across her boots.

Nesta didn’t need to look up to know who it was. The scent of sweat and steel, the faintest hint of something wild and untamed beneath it—she recognized it instantly. Kars. The warrior who had once stood beside Bellius, who had watched as the others had tried to break her. Who had done nothing.

She lifted her chin. “What do you want?”

Kars didn’t flinch at the bite in her voice. His dark eyes were steady, his expression grim. “I heard what you said in there.” A nod toward the meeting hall, where the air still hummed with the tension of their confrontation. “About Bellius.”

Nesta’s fingers twitched toward the dagger. “And?”

Kars exhaled sharply, his breath curling in the frigid air. “He’s planning something. Something worse than an assassination attempt.”

The words settled over her like a shroud. She had known, of course. Had felt the weight of Bellius’s hatred pressing down on her from the moment she’d arrived. But worse? Worse than the knives in the dark, the poison in her food, the “accidents” that had nearly sent her tumbling off the cliffs?

“Why are you telling me this?” she asked, her voice low.

Kars met her gaze, unflinching. “Because I’m tired of watching innocents get hurt.”

Something in his tone made her pause. Not the words themselves—she had heard similar platitudes before, from Cassian, from Feyre, from anyone who had ever tried to pry open the shell she’d built around herself. But the way Kars said it. As if he, too, had been hollowed out by the things he’d seen. By the things he’d done.

She studied him for a long moment. The lines etched into his face, the scars crisscrossing his knuckles. A warrior’s hands. A warrior’s guilt.

“You’re not innocent,” she said at last.

Kars’s jaw tightened. “No. I’m not.”

A beat of silence stretched between them, thick with unspoken things. Then, from the corner of her eye, Nesta caught movement. Cassian. He stood a dozen paces away, his arms crossed over his chest, his wings slightly flared. His crimson Siphons glinted in the pale sunlight, but his face was unreadable.

His gaze was locked on Kars.

Nesta turned back to the warrior before her. “What do they plan?”

Kars hesitated. “I don’t know the details. But it’s soon. And it’s not just you they’re after.”

Her stomach twisted. Not just her. That meant Cassian. Emerie. Gwyn. The others who had dared to stand against Bellius’s faction.

She swallowed hard. “We need to act. Before they do.”

Kars nodded once, sharp and decisive. “I’ll find out what I can.”

He turned to leave, but Nesta caught his arm. “Why now?” she demanded. “Why come to me now?”

Kars looked down at her hand on his bicep, then back up at her face. “Because I saw the way you looked at Bellius today. Like you were already calculating how to kill him.” A faint, humorless smile touched his lips. “I’d rather be on the winning side.”

Then he was gone, melting into the crowd of warriors as if he’d never been there at all.

Nesta exhaled, her breath shaky. The wind howled again, and this time, she let it sting her cheeks. Let it remind her that she was still alive. Still standing.

Cassian was at her side in an instant, his presence a storm of barely leashed fury. “What the hell was that?”

She didn’t look at him. “An ally.”

“Kars?” His voice was a growl. “He’s one of Bellius’s men.”

“He was,” she corrected. “Now he’s tired of watching innocents get hurt.”

Cassian’s wings twitched, the feathers rustling like dry leaves. “You don’t know what you’re asking.”

Nesta finally turned to face him. His dark eyes were stormy, his jaw clenched so tight she wondered if his teeth might crack. She stepped closer, close enough to see the flecks of gold in his irises, close enough to feel the heat radiating off his body.

“Then make me understand.”

---

The hut was too small for the both of them. Too small for the weight of what hung between them, unspoken and raw.

Cassian’s wings brushed the wooden walls, his crimson Siphons glowing faintly in the dim light filtering through the cracks. He hadn’t even bothered to remove his gloves, as if he knew this conversation wouldn’t take long. As if he knew she wouldn’t listen.

“You have to leave,” he said, voice low but edged with something sharp. Not anger—no, never anger with her. Frustration, maybe. Fear.

Nesta didn’t look up from where she sat on the narrow cot, her fingers tracing the rough wool of the blanket. “No.”

A muscle feathered in his jaw. “Nesta.”

“I said no.” She lifted her gaze then, meeting his head-on. The gold in his irises burned like embers, but she didn’t flinch. “I’m not running. Not again.”

Cassian exhaled through his nose, a sound like a bull about to charge. “This isn’t about running. It’s about surviving.”

“Then let me survive on my own terms.”

His hands flexed at his sides, the leather of his gloves creaking. “You think this is a game? That you can just—just *decide* how this goes?”

“I think I’m tired of people deciding for me.” Her voice was steady, too steady. Like the calm before a storm. “Feyre sends me away. You drag me here. Amren watches from the sidelines like it’s all some grand spectacle. Well, I’m done being the prize in everyone’s little war.”

Cassian’s wings flared slightly, as if he wanted to take flight right then and there. “You think I *want* this? You think I *want* to be the one telling you to leave?”

“Then don’t.”

“Someone has to!” The words tore from him, rough and raw. “Someone has to be the one to say it, because you sure as hell won’t listen to anyone else.”

Nesta stood, the movement sharp enough to make the cot groan. “I don’t need a keeper, Cassian.”

“No?” His laugh was bitter. “Then what do you call this?” He gestured between them, the space that had always been too small, too charged. “What do you call me, following you around like a gods-damned fool, trying to keep you from—”

“From what?” she snapped. “From living? From making my own choices?”

“From *dying*!” The words echoed off the wooden walls, sharp as a blade. His chest heaved, his breath coming too fast. “You think I don’t see it? You think I don’t *know* what’s happening to you?”

Nesta went still. “And what’s that?”

“You’re not just angry.” His voice dropped, rough with something like pain. “You’re *hungry*. For anything that’ll make you feel something, even if it’s pain. Even if it’s destruction.”

Her fingers curled into fists at her sides. “Maybe I am.”

“And what happens when that hunger gets you killed?” His voice was a growl now, low and dangerous. “What happens when you push too far, and there’s no one there to pull you back?”

“Then that’s my choice.”

Cassian’s breath hitched, his gaze burning into hers. “You’re going to get yourself killed.”

Nesta didn’t look away. “Maybe,” she whispered. “But it’ll be my choice.”

---

The wind howled across the training camp, biting through the thin fabric of Nesta’s gown. She barely felt it. The cold had long since settled into her bones, a familiar companion to the hollowness inside her chest. Around her, the Illyrian warriors moved with practiced ease, their wings rustling like dry leaves as they sparred or sharpened weapons. None spared her a glance. She was an outsider here—an intruder in their world of blood and sky.

Devlon stood near the edge of the training ring, barking orders at a group of young warriors. His broad back was turned to her, but she knew he’d sensed her approach. The way his shoulders tensed, the slight tilt of his head—he was aware of her, even if he pretended otherwise.

Nesta stopped just behind him, close enough to see the sweat glistening on the nape of his neck. “I want in.”

Devlon didn’t turn. “In where?”

“The investigation.” Her voice was steady, devoid of the tremor she felt in her hands. She clenched them into fists at her sides. “The traitor. The attacks. I want to help.”

That got his attention. Devlon turned slowly, his dark eyes narrowing as they raked over her. “You?” The word was a sneer. “What do you know about investigations, girl?”

Nesta didn’t flinch. “I know how to listen. I know how to watch. And I know what it’s like to be hunted.” The last words slipped out before she could stop them, raw and unguarded. She swallowed hard, forcing her expression back into something colder. “I can help.”

Devlon let out a sharp laugh. “You’re a liability. A distraction.” His gaze flicked to something over her shoulder. “And you’re not one of us.”

“No,” Nesta agreed. “But I’m not asking to be. I’m asking to be useful.”

“Useful?” Devlon’s voice dropped to a growl. “You want to be useful? Then stay out of the way. Let the warriors handle this.”

Nesta bared her teeth. “You want to find the traitor? Then let me help. Or are you too proud to accept it?”

Devlon’s hand twitched toward the sword at his hip. “Careful, girl.”

“Or what?” Nesta stepped closer, close enough to see the flecks of gold in his irises. “You’ll send me back to Velaris? Lock me in a pretty house and pretend I don’t exist?” Her voice rose, sharp as a blade. “I’m not some fragile thing to be tucked away. I’m—”

“You’re what?” Devlon cut in, his voice a whip crack. “A human? A broken girl playing at being a warrior?” He leaned in, his breath hot against her face. “You’ll get yourself killed. And then what? Who do you think will have to clean up the mess?”

Nesta’s chest heaved, her fingers curling into claws at her sides. She opened her mouth to retort, but a shadow fell over them both.

Cassian stepped between them, his wings half-spread, a barrier of muscle and leather. His Siphons glowed faintly, a warning. “She’s right.”

Devlon’s head snapped toward him. “What?”

Cassian’s gaze didn’t waver. “We need all the help we can get. And Nesta’s been through enough to know what’s at stake.” His voice was low, steady, the voice of a commander. “She stays.”

Devlon’s jaw tightened. “She’s not a warrior.”

“No,” Cassian agreed. “But she’s not helpless, either.” He glanced at Nesta, just for a second, before turning back to Devlon. “And if she gets in the way, she’s on her own.”

Nesta’s breath caught. She hadn’t expected him to stand up for her. Not after everything. Not after she’d pushed him away, again and again.

Devlon’s gaze flicked between them, his expression darkening. “Fine.” The word was a growl. “But if she fails, it’s on your head.”

Cassian met Nesta’s gaze, his voice steady. “She won’t.”

---

The wind howled through the Illyrian peaks, biting at Nesta’s exposed skin as she stood amidst the training camp. The warriors had scattered like crows at their approach, but Cassian had made it clear—none were to leave until they’d been questioned. Now, they lingered in small, tense clusters, their dark eyes flicking between her and the warrior at her side.

Cassian’s wings were slightly flared, a silent warning to any who might think to challenge them. But Nesta didn’t need his protection. Not here. Not like this.

The first warrior stepped forward, his jaw set. “What do you want?”

Nesta tilted her head, studying him. His arms were crossed, his stance wide—defensive. Not hostile, but not helpful either. “You were on patrol the night of the attack,” she said, her voice cool. “Did you see anything unusual?”

The warrior’s gaze flicked to Cassian before returning to her. “Nothing.”

“Nothing at all?” she pressed. “No unfamiliar scents? No sounds out of place?”

He shifted, his wings rustling. “I said nothing.”

Nesta exhaled through her nose. “And what about Bellius?” she asked, watching his face. “He’s been vocal about his… opinions.”

The warrior’s expression darkened. “Bellius speaks for many of us.”

“Does he?” Nesta’s voice was soft, dangerous. “Or does he just speak the loudest?”

The warrior said nothing, but his silence was answer enough.

Cassian stepped forward, his voice low. “Answer her.”

The warrior’s throat bobbed. “I have nothing to say.”

Nesta didn’t look at Cassian as she dismissed the man with a wave of her hand. The next warrior was already stepping forward, this one younger, his eyes darting between them with something like fear.

“You were there that night,” Nesta said. “What did you see?”

The young warrior swallowed. “I—I didn’t see anything. But I heard…” He hesitated.

“Heard what?” Cassian prompted, his voice gentler than before.

The warrior’s fingers twitched at his sides. “Whispers. About the blood oath. About how it’s not just about loyalty to the Illyrians anymore.”

Nesta’s pulse quickened. “What do you mean?”

The warrior shook his head. “I don’t know. Just… whispers. That it’s changing. That it’s not what it used to be.”

Nesta exchanged a glance with Cassian. His jaw was tight, his Siphons glowing faintly, as if he were holding back his power.

The next warrior was older, his face lined with years of battle. He didn’t wait for her to ask. “Bellius and his followers have been meeting in secret,” he said, his voice rough. “They don’t trust the High Lords. They don’t trust anyone who isn’t Illyrian.”

Nesta’s fingers curled into her palms. “And what do they want?”

The old warrior’s eyes were hard. “What they’ve always wanted. Power. Control. A return to the old ways.”

Cassian’s wings shifted, a restless movement. “And the blood oath?”

The warrior’s gaze flicked to him. “They say it’s been corrupted. That it’s not just about binding us to the land anymore. That it’s about binding us to *them*.”

Nesta’s breath caught. “Them?”

The warrior didn’t answer. He didn’t need to.

Cassian’s voice was a growl. “Who?”

The warrior’s lips pressed into a thin line. “You know who.”

Nesta’s mind raced. The Hybern king. The remnants of his forces. The shadows that still lingered in the darkest corners of Prythian.

She turned away from the warrior, her skirts swirling around her ankles as she paced. The wind tugged at her braid, pulling strands loose to whip against her cheeks. Cassian watched her, his expression unreadable.

“You’re good at this,” he said quietly.

Nesta didn’t look at him. “I’ve had a lot of practice.”

His wings rustled, a sound she felt more than heard. “Practice at what?”

She stopped pacing, her gaze locking onto his. The wind stilled, as if the world itself were holding its breath.

“Surviving.”

---

The hut was little more than a drafty box of stone and timber, but it was hers—at least for now. Nesta collapsed onto the narrow bed, the thin mattress doing little to cushion the ache in her bones. The fire in the hearth had long since burned to embers, casting the room in flickering shadows that danced like specters along the walls.

She should have been asleep. The day had been endless—training with Cassian until her muscles screamed, then more training with the other warriors until her vision blurred. But her mind refused to quiet. It clawed at her, sharp and relentless, picking apart every detail of the attack in the training ring. The way the blade had come for her throat. The way the warrior had moved, not with the brutal efficiency of a soldier, but with something colder. Calculated.

A knock at the door had her bolting upright, her hand already reaching for the dagger she kept beneath her pillow. The door creaked open before she could answer, and Cassian filled the frame, his wings brushing the sides of the doorway. His expression was storm-dark, his jaw set in a way that made her stomach tighten.

"You're still awake," he said, stepping inside and shutting the door behind him. The hut suddenly felt too small, the air too thick.

Nesta didn’t bother to soften her voice. "What do you want?"

Cassian exhaled sharply, running a hand through his dark hair. The crimson Siphons on his knuckles glinted in the firelight. "We’re missing something."

She stilled. "About the attack?"

He nodded, his gaze flicking to the dagger still clutched in her hand before meeting her eyes. "It wasn’t just about killing you."

The words settled over her like a shroud. She had known it, of course—had felt it in the way the blade had hesitated, in the way the warrior’s eyes had burned with something beyond bloodlust. But hearing Cassian say it aloud made it real.

"Then what was it about?" she asked, her voice low.

Cassian hesitated, as if weighing whether to speak at all. Then, with a slow exhale, he said, "A message."

Nesta’s breath caught. "To who?"

His voice dropped to a whisper. "To all of us."

The fire popped, sending a shower of sparks up the chimney. Nesta’s fingers tightened around the dagger. "What kind of message?"

Cassian’s wings flared slightly, as if the weight of his thoughts was too much to bear. "That we’re not safe here. That they can get to us anywhere. That they’re watching."

A cold knot formed in her chest. She had spent months feeling nothing—hollow, numb, as if the world had carved her out and left only an empty shell. But now, something stirred beneath the ice. Something sharp and hungry.

She lifted her chin. "Then we send a message back."

Cassian’s gaze snapped to hers, his dark eyes burning. "And what message is that?"

Nesta’s lips curled into a smile, all teeth and no warmth. "That they picked the wrong fight."
