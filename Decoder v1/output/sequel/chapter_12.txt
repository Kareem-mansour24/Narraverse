CHAPTER 12
The First Crack

The first light of dawn had yet to breach the jagged peaks of the Illyrian Mountains when Nesta’s eyes snapped open. She lay still for a heartbeat, listening to the wind howl through the cracks in her hut, the cold seeping into her bones. Her body ached—every muscle, every joint, a chorus of protest from yesterday’s endurance climb. The climb she had barely survived.

She exhaled through her nose, slow and controlled, and sat up. The furs slipped from her shoulders, pooling around her waist. The fire in the hearth had long since died, leaving the air thick with the scent of damp wood and frost. She dressed in silence, her fingers moving with practiced efficiency as she laced her leather training gear, the material stiff against her skin. The bruises along her ribs throbbed, but she ignored them. Pain was just another sensation. And sensations could be endured.

Outside, the training grounds were already stirring. The warriors moved like shadows in the predawn gloom, their voices low murmurs carried on the wind. Nesta stepped into the cold, her breath curling in front of her, and made her way toward the central fire pit. The heat licked at her face, a fleeting comfort. She didn’t join the others. She never did.

Devlon stood at the edge of the gathering, his arms crossed over his broad chest, his dark eyes scanning the warriors before landing on her. A slow, cruel smile curled his lips. “Today,” he announced, his voice cutting through the murmurs, “we test your strength in the pits.”

A ripple of excitement passed through the crowd. Nesta didn’t react. She knew what the pits meant. Mud. Blood. Humiliation.

“Bellius,” Devlon called, gesturing to the hulking warrior with a scar running down his cheek. “You’ll take the lead. Kars and Riven will join you.” His gaze flicked to Nesta. “And you’ll face them.”

The warriors turned as one, their eyes gleaming with anticipation. Bellius cracked his knuckles, his grin widening. “Looking forward to it, girl.”

Nesta said nothing. She stepped forward, her boots sinking slightly into the damp earth, and made her way toward the pits. The mud was already churned from previous battles, the ground slick and treacherous. She could feel their stares pressing into her back, heavy as stones. She didn’t look at them. She didn’t look at Cassian, either, though she could sense him—his presence, his tension, the way his hands clenched at his sides as he watched from the sidelines.

The pit was deeper than she remembered, the walls steep and slippery. She stepped down carefully, the mud sucking at her boots. The cold seeped through the leather, numbing her toes. She flexed her fingers, testing the weight of the practice sword in her hand. It was heavier than she liked. Or maybe that was just the exhaustion dragging at her limbs.

Bellius dropped into the pit beside her, followed by Kars and Riven. The warriors circled her like wolves, their grins sharp. “Try not to cry too much,” Bellius sneered.

Nesta didn’t answer. She adjusted her grip on the sword and waited.

Devlon raised his hand. “Begin.”

The first strike came fast. Bellius lunged, his blade aimed for her ribs. Nesta twisted away, the mud spraying up around her boots as she pivoted. The impact of the blow reverberated up her arm, but she gritted her teeth and countered. The clash of steel rang out, sharp and brutal.

Kars came at her from the side, his sword swinging low. She barely dodged in time, the blade grazing her thigh. A hot line of pain flared, but she didn’t falter. She drove her elbow into his gut, sending him stumbling back into the mud.

Riven was on her then, his movements quick, precise. She blocked his strike, but the force of it sent her skidding backward, her boots losing traction in the slick earth. She hit the ground hard, the mud splashing up around her, coating her face, her hair. The warriors laughed.

Nesta pushed herself up, her breath ragged. The mud clung to her, heavy and suffocating. She wiped her face with the back of her hand, her fingers trembling—not from fear, but from the effort of holding herself together.

Bellius loomed over her, his sword raised. “Yield,” he growled.

Nesta bared her teeth. “No.”

The next blow came down like a hammer. She barely got her sword up in time, the impact shuddering through her bones. The warriors pressed in, their strikes relentless, their laughter ringing in her ears.

She didn’t know how long it lasted. Time blurred, the world narrowing to the mud beneath her feet, the weight of the sword in her hand, the sting of every hit, every near-miss. She fought until her arms burned, until her lungs screamed for air, until the mud was no longer cold but hot with her own blood.

And still, she didn’t yield.

When Devlon finally called an end to it, Nesta stood alone in the pit, her chest heaving, her body trembling with exhaustion. The warriors climbed out, their laughter fading as they turned their backs on her. She didn’t look at them. She didn’t look at Cassian, either.

But she could feel his eyes on her. And for the first time in months, she felt something—something sharp and bitter, coiling in her chest.

She ignored it. She always did.

---

The mud was thick and cold beneath Nesta’s boots as she braced herself, the weight of the wooden training sword familiar in her grip. Around her, the Illyrian warriors shifted, their wings rustling like dry leaves in the wind. Bellius stood at the forefront, his smirk a blade aimed straight at her.

“Ready to lose, Archeron?” he drawled, his voice dripping with false sweetness.

Nesta didn’t answer. She didn’t need to. The mock battle was about to begin, and words were useless here.

Cassian’s voice cut through the tension, sharp as the wind whipping down the cliffs. “Begin.”

Bellius moved first, his allies fanning out around him like a pack of wolves. Nesta sidestepped, her body remembering the drills Cassian had forced upon her—remembering the way her muscles burned, the way her breath came in ragged gasps. She parried a strike from one of Bellius’s lackeys, the impact vibrating up her arm, and countered with a swift jab to his ribs. He grunted, stumbling back, but Bellius was already there, his own sword swinging low.

She dodged, but not fast enough. The wooden blade grazed her side, sending a jolt of pain through her. She gritted her teeth, refusing to flinch.

“Pathetic,” Bellius sneered. “You’re slower than I remember.”

Nesta didn’t rise to the bait. She had spent months feeling nothing, and she wasn’t about to let him be the one to crack that hollow shell. She feinted left, then twisted right, aiming for his exposed flank. Her sword connected—solid, satisfying. Bellius hissed, his smirk faltering for the briefest moment.

Then he struck back.

His blow was brutal, precise. The flat of his blade slammed into her side, knocking the breath from her lungs. She staggered, her boots slipping in the mud, and went down hard. The impact sent a shockwave through her bones, the cold seeping through her clothes as she sprawled in the muck.

Laughter erupted around her. The warriors jeered, their voices grating against her ears. Nesta pushed herself up on trembling arms, her vision swimming. Bellius loomed over her, his shadow blocking out the pale morning light.

“Stay down,” he growled, his breath hot against her face.

Nesta’s fingers curled into the mud, the damp earth squelching between them. Her side throbbed, her lungs burned, but she lifted her head. Her voice was a whisper, raw and unyielding.

“Never.”

---

The wind howled across the training grounds, carrying the scent of damp earth and pine. Nesta wiped the sweat from her brow with the back of her hand, her breath still ragged from the match. The weight of the sword in her grip was familiar now, almost comforting, though she’d never admit it aloud.

Cassian’s shadow fell over her before she heard him approach. His wings were half-spread, the crimson Siphons on his knuckles glinting in the pale afternoon light. She didn’t need to look up to know his expression was stormy.

“You fought well,” he said, voice low.

Nesta exhaled sharply through her nose. “Don’t patronize me.”

“I’m not.” His jaw tightened. “But Bellius is gunning for you. He won’t let this go.”

She finally turned to face him, her fingers curling into fists at her sides. “I don’t need you to warn me about Bellius. I can handle myself.”

Cassian’s gaze darkened, the gold in his eyes flickering like embers. “That’s not what I meant, and you know it.”

“Then what *did* you mean?” she snapped, stepping closer. The wind tugged at the loose strands of her braided coronet, whipping them across her face. “That I should be careful? That I should hold back? That I should let them win?”

“No.” His voice was a growl. “I meant that you’re not just fighting *them*.” He took a step toward her, the space between them shrinking. “You’re fighting *yourself*.”

Nesta’s breath hitched. The words struck too close, too true. She wanted to lash out, to deny it, but the truth of it coiled in her chest, tight and suffocating.

“You don’t know what you’re talking about,” she said, but her voice lacked its usual bite.

Cassian’s wings rustled, the sound like dry leaves skittering across stone. “Don’t I?” He was close enough now that she could see the flecks of gold in his irises, the way his pupils dilated as he held her gaze. “You think I don’t see it? The way you push, the way you *refuse* to let yourself feel anything but anger?”

Nesta’s teeth bared. “I don’t need to feel anything.”

“Bullshit.” His voice was a whip-crack. “You’re drowning in it, and you won’t even admit it to yourself.”

She turned away, her boots kicking up dust as she strode toward the cliffs. The wind howled louder here, tugging at her clothes, her hair, as if trying to pull the words from her lips. But she wouldn’t give him the satisfaction.

Behind her, she heard the soft rustle of his wings as he stepped back, giving her space—but not leaving.

Nesta stopped at the edge of the cliff, the drop yawning before her. The wind whipped through her hair, stinging her cheeks, but she didn’t flinch. Cassian’s words echoed in her mind, a challenge she couldn’t ignore.

*You’re not just fighting them. You’re fighting yourself.*

---

The hut smelled of damp wood and old smoke, the scent clinging to the rough-hewn walls like a second skin. Nesta stepped inside, her body screaming with every movement. The training session had been brutal—worse than usual. Cassian had pushed her harder, his wings snapping with impatience as she faltered, his voice a low growl in her ear. *You want to be strong? Then act like it.*

She hadn’t answered. Hadn’t even looked at him.

Kars was already inside, perched on the edge of her narrow cot like he’d been waiting for hours. Maybe he had. His dark eyes flicked up as she entered, assessing the damage before he reached for the roll of bandages and the jar of salve at his feet. He didn’t speak, just patted the cot beside him in silent invitation.

Nesta hesitated, her fingers twitching at her sides. She should tell him to leave. Should slam the door in his face and let the cold night air swallow the offer in his gaze. But her legs ached, her ribs throbbed where a particularly vicious kick had landed, and the thought of bending to tend to the wounds herself made her stomach twist.

She sat.

Kars moved without ceremony, his calloused hands gentle as he peeled back the torn fabric of her tunic. The salve was cool against her skin, a small mercy against the fire of her injuries. She watched him work, the way his brow furrowed in concentration, the way his fingers never lingered where they shouldn’t. He wasn’t like the others. Wasn’t like Cassian, who touched her like she was made of glass, or the warriors who sneered at her like she was a stain on their precious mountains.

“Why do you keep helping me?”

The question slipped out before she could stop it, quiet and rough. Kars didn’t look up, but his hands stilled for the briefest moment. Then he resumed his work, winding the bandage around her forearm with practiced ease.

“Because you’re not the only one who’s tired of being told what to do.” His voice was low, measured. “But you’re the only one who’s *fighting* it.”

Nesta’s breath hitched. She wanted to scoff, to tell him he didn’t know what he was talking about. But the words died on her tongue, because he wasn’t wrong. She *was* fighting. Fighting the Illyrians, fighting Cassian, fighting the hollow ache inside her that refused to fade no matter how hard she tried to ignore it.

Kars finished with the bandage and sat back, his gaze finally meeting hers. There was something in his eyes—something like understanding, like recognition. It made her skin prickle.

“They think we’re weak,” he said, tilting his head toward the door, toward the distant sounds of the training grounds. “But weakness isn’t the problem. It’s the pretending. The lying to ourselves about what we really want.”

Nesta swallowed. She didn’t know what she wanted. Not anymore.

Kars stood, brushing his hands on his thighs. “Get some rest,” he said, nodding toward the cot. Then he was gone, slipping out into the night like a shadow.

Nesta didn’t move. The hut felt too small, the walls pressing in on her. She stood abruptly, her body protesting, and stepped outside.

The cold hit her like a slap, the wind howling down from the peaks and biting at her exposed skin. She wrapped her arms around herself, her breath fogging in the air. The training grounds were quiet now, the warriors either asleep or gathered around fires, their laughter carrying on the wind. But beneath it, beneath the clink of glasses and the low murmur of voices, there was something else.

A whisper.

Not of wind. Not of the night.

Of dissent.

Nesta turned her head, her gaze locking onto the distant cliffs, their jagged edges silvered by moonlight. Somewhere out there, beyond the laughter and the lies, something was shifting. And for the first time in months, she didn’t feel entirely alone in it.

---

The wind bit at Nesta’s face as she stepped onto the cliff’s edge, the jagged rocks beneath her boots slick with frost. Below, the world stretched out in a sea of black, the distant lights of the Illyrian camp flickering like dying stars. She had come here to escape the noise, the pitying glances, the weight of her own failure. But the silence was worse.

It pressed in on her, suffocating, until she could barely breathe.

She didn’t hear him approach. Didn’t need to. She had always known when Cassian was near, even before the war, even before the mating bond had been severed. It was something deeper than instinct, something that hummed in her bones.

He stood beside her now, his wings folded tightly against his back, the crimson of his Siphons muted in the dim light. He didn’t speak. Didn’t offer empty words or false comfort. He simply *was*—a solid, unyielding presence in the darkness.

Nesta’s fingers curled into fists at her sides. “You don’t have to stay.”

His voice was quiet, raw. “I know.” A pause. “But I *want* to.”

The words struck her like a blow. She wanted to lash out, to scream at him to leave, to stop pretending he cared. But the anger coiled in her chest, tight and painful, refused to unravel into words. Instead, her hands trembled, her breath coming too fast.

“Why?” The question was barely a whisper, torn from her before she could stop it.

Cassian turned to her then, his dark eyes burning in the night. “Because I see you, Nesta.” His voice was steady, sure. “And I’m not going anywhere.”

The world tilted. The wind howled around them, but it was nothing compared to the storm inside her. Rage, yes—but something else, too. Something she couldn’t name, didn’t want to name. It clawed at her throat, at her ribs, threatening to split her open.

She wanted to run. Wanted to scream. Wanted to *feel* something, anything, other than this crushing weight.

But she didn’t move.

Cassian stepped closer, his gloved hand brushing against hers. A silent promise. A challenge.

The moment stretched, fragile and electric, the air between them charged with something she wasn’t ready to face.

Then, finally, she turned away.

Her heart pounded in her ears as she walked back toward the huts, her boots crunching over frost-bitten grass. She didn’t look back. Didn’t need to.

She knew he was still there, watching her.

And for the first time in months, she didn’t feel entirely alone in the storm.
