CHAPTER 29
The Edge of the Blade

The alarms tore through the predawn stillness like a blade through flesh.

Nesta’s eyes snapped open, her body already moving before her mind had fully registered the sound. The war horns blared again, a deep, guttural wail that vibrated through the tent’s canvas, through her bones. She was on her feet in an instant, the gray gown she’d slept in whispering against her legs as she lunged for the weapons belt draped over the chair beside her cot.

The camp outside was chaos. Shouting. The clang of steel. The roar of wings. She didn’t hesitate.

The tent flap ripped open, and Kars stumbled inside, his face streaked with blood, his dark eyes wild. “They’re here,” he panted, his chest heaving. “Bellius’s clan—attacking at dawn. They’ve breached the outer perimeter.”

Nesta didn’t bother with questions. She strapped the belt around her waist, the weight of the dagger at her hip familiar, comforting. “Cassian and Azriel?”

“Holding the line,” Kars said, wiping sweat from his brow. “But they’re outnumbered.”

She didn’t let herself think about what that meant. Didn’t let the hollow space inside her chest widen into something worse. She grabbed the sword leaning against the tent pole, the hilt cold and solid in her grip.

Kars’s gaze flicked to the blade, then back to her face. “You don’t have to—”

“I know,” she cut him off, her voice steady. “But I’m going.”

He didn’t argue. Just nodded, his jaw tight, and stepped aside to let her pass.

The camp was a storm of movement. Warriors sprinting toward the cliffs, their wings unfurling as they took to the skies. Arrows hissed through the air, thudding into the earth or finding flesh with sickening precision. Nesta didn’t flinch. Didn’t slow.

She ran.

The wind bit at her face, her braided coronet whipping against her back as she wove through the chaos. The scent of blood and smoke filled her lungs, sharp and metallic. She didn’t look at the bodies strewn across the ground. Didn’t let herself wonder if any of them were hers to mourn.

The battle lines were a mess of snarling faces and flashing steel. Cassian was a whirlwind at the center, his sword a silver arc of death, his wings casting monstrous shadows over the men who dared to challenge him. Azriel fought beside him, his blades moving with lethal grace, his face a mask of cold fury.

And then there was Bellius.

The rogue commander stood atop a rocky outcrop, his wings spread wide, his mouth twisted in a sneer as he barked orders to his men. His gaze locked onto Nesta as she stepped into the fray, and his lips curled back in a grin.

She didn’t give him the satisfaction of a reaction.

The first man who lunged at her died before he could blink. Her sword flashed, a clean, brutal strike across his throat. Blood sprayed, hot and wet against her skin, and she didn’t even pause.

The battle raged around her, a symphony of screams and steel. Nesta moved through it like a shadow, her blade a relentless extension of her will. She didn’t think. Didn’t feel.

She fought.

And when Bellius finally descended from his perch, his own sword drawn, his eyes burning with hatred, she met him without hesitation.

The first strike rang out like a declaration.

---

The clash of steel against steel was a language Nesta had learned to speak fluently. It sang through her bones, a rhythm as natural as breathing, as necessary. The battlefield stretched before her, a churning sea of bodies and blades, the air thick with the metallic tang of blood and the acrid bite of smoke.

She moved like a shadow given form, her own sword a silver extension of her arm. The weight of it was familiar now, the balance perfect. She had spent months honing her body into a weapon, and today, it showed. Every step was deliberate, every swing precise. She was not the girl who had once flinched at the sight of blood. She was something else entirely.

A cry of pain cut through the din, and Nesta’s head snapped toward the sound. A young warrior—barely more than a boy—was backed against a jagged rock, his sword trembling in his grip as three of Bellius’s men advanced. His eyes were wide, his breath coming in ragged gasps. He was going to die.

Nesta didn’t think. She moved.

Her blade found the first man’s throat before he even registered her presence. The second turned just in time to meet her sword with his own, but she twisted, using his momentum against him, and drove her knee into his gut. He doubled over, and she brought the pommel of her sword down on the back of his skull. The third man lunged, but she sidestepped, her boot connecting with his ribs. He staggered, and she finished him with a single, clean stroke.

The boy stared at her, his face pale, his chest heaving. “Fall back,” she snapped, her voice sharp as the edge of her blade. “Now.”

He didn’t question. He didn’t hesitate. He turned and ran, disappearing into the chaos of the battlefield.

Nesta didn’t watch him go. She was already moving again, her body coiled and ready. The fighting was thickest near the center of the field, where the Illyrian warriors had formed a loose circle around their war tent. She could see the crimson glow of Siphons cutting through the smoke, hear the roar of the warriors as they pushed back against Bellius’s forces.

And then she saw him.

Cassian.

He was a storm given form, his wings spread wide, his Siphons blazing like embers in the dim light. His sword moved in arcs of fire, cutting down enemy after enemy. There was no hesitation in him, no mercy. He was death incarnate, and for the first time, Nesta understood why the Illyrians followed him. Why they would die for him.

Their eyes met across the field.

It was only a second—less, even—but it was enough. Something unspoken passed between them. Trust, maybe. Or something far more dangerous.

Then the moment was gone, shattered by the scream of a man as Cassian drove his blade through his chest.

Nesta turned away, her heart pounding. She didn’t have time to dissect the feeling. She didn’t want to.

She fought her way forward, her sword a blur of motion. She was close to the war tent now, close enough to see the Illyrian banners snapping in the wind. Close enough to hear the shouts of the warriors inside, barking orders, coordinating their defense.

A hand grabbed her arm, yanking her back. She spun, her blade raised, but it was Kars, his face streaked with blood, his eyes wild. “They’re breaking through the eastern flank,” he shouted over the noise. “We need to reinforce—”

“No.” Nesta’s voice was steady, certain. “We hold here. If they break through, they’ll take the tent. We can’t let that happen.”

Kars hesitated, then nodded. He didn’t question her. He didn’t argue. He just turned and barked orders to the warriors around them, his voice cutting through the chaos like a knife.

Nesta exhaled, her grip tightening on her sword. She could feel the weight of the battle pressing down on her, the lives of the warriors around her resting on her decisions. It should have terrified her. It should have made her hesitate.

But it didn’t.

She was exactly where she was meant to be.

Then, cutting through the noise like a blade through silk, she heard it.

“Nesta!”

Cassian’s voice.

She turned, her breath catching in her throat.

He was pointing toward the ridge, his expression grim. And there, standing atop the rocky outcrop, was Bellius.

Watching.

Smug.

---

The wind howled through the Illyrian peaks, biting at Nesta’s exposed skin as she and Cassian crested the slope. The ridge loomed ahead, jagged and unforgiving, and there—perched like a vulture—was Bellius. His dark eyes gleamed with triumph, his lips curled into that infuriating smirk.

Nesta’s grip tightened around the hilt of her blade. The weight of it was familiar now, a counterbalance to the storm inside her. She had spent months feeling nothing, but the sight of Bellius ignited something raw and unrelenting. Rage. Or maybe it was fear, sharp and acidic, clawing at her ribs.

Cassian’s voice cut through the wind, low and urgent. “He’s not alone.”

She followed his gaze. Below Bellius, a contingent of warriors stood at attention, their weapons glinting in the pale morning light. Rogue clan. Traitors. And among them—

Devlon.

Her breath hitched. Devlon, who had trained her, who had sneered at her but never betrayed her. Not like this.

Bellius spread his arms wide, as if welcoming them. “Nesta Archeron,” he called, his voice carrying on the wind. “Come to surrender at last?”

She didn’t answer. Her pulse roared in her ears.

“Devlon here tells me the camp is ready to yield,” Bellius continued, gesturing to the warrior beside him. Devlon’s jaw was set, his eyes fixed on some point beyond her. “A wise choice, don’t you think? No more bloodshed.”

Nesta’s fingers twitched against the blade. The wind tugged at her braid, her hair coming loose in wild strands. She could feel the weight of the warriors behind her—Kars, Torin, the others who had fought beside her, who had bled with her. They were watching. Waiting.

Cassian’s voice was a murmur at her side. “We don’t have to do this alone.”

She didn’t look at him. Couldn’t. The words settled into her bones, heavy and undeniable. She had spent so long pushing everyone away, drowning in the hollow space inside her. But this—this was not about her.

This was about them.

Nesta lifted her blade, the steel catching the light. The wind stilled. The world narrowed to the ridge, to the traitors before her, to the warriors at her back.

“Who stands with us?” Her voice rang out, clear and unyielding.

For a heartbeat, there was silence. Then—

Kars stepped forward, his sword raised. Torin followed, his wings flaring wide. One by one, the others moved, their weapons glinting, their voices rising in a roar that shook the mountains.

Bellius’s smirk faltered. His eyes flicked between them, his confidence wavering. And for the first time, Nesta saw it—fear.

It was not the fear of a warrior facing death. It was the fear of a man who had underestimated her. Who had thought her broken.

She bared her teeth.

He was wrong.

---

The wind howled across the cliffs, whipping Nesta’s braided coronet into a frenzy as she stood at the edge of the war tent, the scent of blood and iron thick in the air. Below, the battlefield stretched like a gnarled scar across the mountainside, the clash of steel and the cries of the wounded rising in a cacophony that should have drowned out thought. But all she heard was the steady thud of her own heartbeat, the rhythm of it syncing with the pulse of the blade in her hand.

Cassian landed beside her, his wings snapping taut before folding neatly against his back. His Siphons glowed crimson, casting eerie light over the sharp angles of his face. “Ready?” he asked, voice low, rough with battle.

She didn’t look at him. Couldn’t. Not when the weight of what was coming pressed down on her like a physical force. “Yes.”

His hand brushed her arm—just once, a fleeting touch—but it was enough to make her flinch. She stepped away before he could say anything else, before the warmth of him could seep into the cold that had taken root in her bones.

The Illyrian warriors around them shifted, their wings rustling like dry leaves in the wind. Kars and Torin stood at the front, their faces set in grim lines, their own blades already drawn. They had followed her here. Followed *him*. And now they would see what she was made of.

Nesta exhaled, sharp and controlled, and lifted her sword.

The moment she stepped forward, the world narrowed. The battlefield became a blur of movement and sound, her body moving on instinct, her blade a silver extension of her will. She cut through Bellius’s forces like a scythe through wheat, her muscles burning, her breath coming in ragged gasps. Beside her, Cassian fought with the precision of a man who had spent centuries perfecting the art of war, his own sword a whirl of deadly grace. They moved together, not in sync—never in sync—but in a rhythm born of shared purpose, of shared blood.

And then she saw him.

Bellius.

He stood at the center of the fray, his armor dented, his face smeared with dirt and blood, but his eyes—his eyes were bright with the same cruel amusement she remembered from the training ring. The same look he had given her when he had broken her bones, when he had laughed as she had bled.

Her vision tunneled.

She was on him before she realized she had moved, her blade clashing against his with a force that sent shockwaves up her arms. He grinned, his teeth stained red. “Still playing at being a warrior, little mouse?”

Nesta didn’t answer. She didn’t need to. Her sword did the talking for her, a relentless storm of steel that forced him back, step by step, until his heel caught on a rock and he stumbled. She didn’t hesitate. She disarmed him in one fluid motion, her blade pressing against the pulse in his throat.

Bellius froze. His breath came in shallow, ragged bursts, his eyes darting to the side where his warriors had fallen, where his cause had crumbled. “You don’t have the stomach for this,” he sneered, his voice a whisper, a taunt. “You never did.”

Her grip tightened on the hilt of her sword. The cold metal bit into her palm, grounding her. She could do it. One quick motion, one flick of her wrist, and it would be over. No more Bellius. No more threats. No more shadows of the past clinging to her like a second skin.

But then—

A hand closed over hers.

Cassian.

His fingers were warm, calloused, the weight of them familiar even through the haze of battle. “You don’t have to,” he said, his voice steady, unshaken.

She wanted to scream. Wanted to shove him away, to tell him she didn’t need his interference, didn’t need his *mercy*. But the words died in her throat, choked by the sudden, overwhelming weight of what she was about to do.

Bellius’s lips curled into a smirk. “Weak,” he spat. “Just like your sister.”

Something inside her snapped.

Nesta wrenched her hand free of Cassian’s grip and stepped back, her blade lowering. The wind howled around them, carrying the scent of blood and damp earth, the cries of the wounded and the dying. She met Bellius’s gaze, her own eyes cold, empty.

“Exile him,” she said, her voice slicing through the noise like a blade. “Let him live with what he’s done.”

Bellius’s face twisted in rage, his mouth opening to spew more venom, but Kars and Torin were already there, dragging him away. His screams echoed across the cliffs, swallowed by the wind, until there was nothing left but the silence of the mountains and the weight of her own breath in her lungs.

---

The wind howled through the jagged peaks of the Illyrian Mountains, carrying with it the scent of blood and smoke. Nesta stood amidst the wreckage of the war camp, her body trembling—not from the cold, but from the sheer exhaustion that seeped into her bones. The battle was over. The camp was in ruins. And yet, she remained.

Her fingers curled into fists at her sides, the nails biting into her palms hard enough to leave crescent moons in her skin. She didn’t know why she was still standing here. Why she hadn’t just walked away the moment the fighting stopped. Maybe it was the way the wind tugged at the loose strands of her braided coronet, or the way the horizon stretched endlessly before her, a reminder of how small she was in the grand scheme of things.

Or maybe it was the weight of the silence.

She didn’t hear him approach. Didn’t need to. She had always known when Cassian was near, even when she pretended not to.

"You did it."

His voice was quiet, rough with exhaustion. It sent a shiver down her spine, though she refused to acknowledge it. She didn’t look at him. Didn’t want to see the crimson glow of his Siphons, the way his dark hair was matted with sweat and dirt, the way his wings hung heavy at his back. She kept her gaze fixed on the horizon, where the last traces of sunset bled into the night.

"We did."

The words tasted like ash in her mouth. A half-truth. A lie. Because she hadn’t done anything. Not really. She had fought, yes, but it had been mechanical. Hollow. Like her body was moving on its own, detached from the storm of emotions raging inside her.

Cassian exhaled, a slow, steady sound. She could feel the heat of him beside her, the way his presence seemed to press against her skin even when he wasn’t touching her. The silence between them wasn’t angry this time. It wasn’t sharp or biting. It was just… heavy. The weight of everything they had survived. Everything they had lost.

Then his fingers brushed against hers.

A featherlight touch. Barely there. But it sent a jolt through her, sharp and unexpected. She didn’t pull away. Didn’t flinch. Just stood there, her breath catching in her throat as his calloused skin grazed hers.

"What now?"

The question hung between them, suspended in the cold mountain air. She wanted to laugh. Wanted to scream. What now? As if there was an answer. As if there was a path forward that didn’t feel like walking through fire.

She turned to face him then, her eyes unguarded for the first time in months. The wind whipped at her face, tugging at the strands of hair that had escaped her braid, but she didn’t care. She just looked at him—really looked at him—and saw the exhaustion in the lines of his face, the way his shoulders sagged beneath the weight of his wings, the way his dark eyes searched hers for something she wasn’t sure she could give.

"I don’t know."

The words were barely a whisper, carried away by the wind almost as soon as they left her lips. But he heard them. She saw the way his throat bobbed, the way his fingers twitched at his sides, as if he wanted to reach for her again but wasn’t sure if he should.

And then, from somewhere in the distance, the sound of wings cut through the silence. Some retreating. Others waiting.

Neither of them pulled away.
