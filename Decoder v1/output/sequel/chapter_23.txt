CHAPTER 23
The Breaking Point

The drums woke her before dawn.

Nesta lay still on the narrow cot, the furs rough against her skin, listening to the rhythmic thudding that vibrated through the wooden walls of the barracks. It wasn’t the usual morning call—this was something darker, something urgent. The sound coiled around her ribs, tightening with each beat.

She sat up, the cold mountain air biting at her bare arms. The camp was already alive, warriors moving like shadows in the predawn gloom. No one met her eyes as she passed. Their whispers died as she approached, only to flare back to life once she was out of earshot. She caught snatches of it—*Bellius*, *gathering*, *war*—before the wind swallowed the words.

Nesta’s fingers curled into fists. She had been here for three days, and already she could feel the weight of their hatred pressing down on her. Not just for being a foreigner, not just for being Fae-born, but for being *her*. The woman who had refused their training, who had spat at their traditions, who had made it clear she wanted nothing to do with any of them.

She found Kars near the training grounds, his broad back bent over a whetstone as he sharpened the edge of a dagger. The scrape of metal on stone was a familiar sound, one that usually grated on her nerves. Today, it was almost soothing.

“You’re up early,” she said, stopping a few paces away.

Kars didn’t look up. “Could say the same about you.”

She ignored the jab. “What’s with the drums?”

His hands stilled. For a long moment, he said nothing, just ran his thumb along the blade’s edge, testing its sharpness. Then, quietly, “Bellius called a gathering. At the northern ridge.”

Nesta’s stomach twisted. “And?”

Kars finally lifted his gaze, his dark eyes unreadable. “And it’s not for you. Or Cassian. Or Devlon.”

Her breath hitched. “Why?”

“Because they’re not planning a training exercise.” He set the dagger down with deliberate care. “They’re planning a war.”

The words hung between them, heavy and suffocating. Nesta’s pulse roared in her ears. She had known, of course. Had felt the tension simmering beneath the surface since the moment she arrived. But to hear it spoken aloud—

“Against who?” she demanded.

Kars shook his head. “Does it matter?”

She bared her teeth. “It matters to me.”

He exhaled sharply through his nose, then stood, wiping his hands on his leathers. “You shouldn’t be here when they return.”

Nesta didn’t move. “I’m not leaving.”

“Nesta—”

“Tell me why they’re doing this.” Her voice was low, dangerous. “Tell me why they’re cutting Cassian out.”

Kars’s jaw tightened. “Because he’s too soft. Because he’s spent too much time in the courts, playing diplomat. Because he’s forgotten what it means to be Illyrian.”

“And what does that mean?” she snapped. “That they get to decide who’s worthy? That they get to start a war just because they’re bored?”

Kars’s expression darkened. “This isn’t about boredom. This is about survival. About reclaiming what was taken from us.”

Nesta let out a bitter laugh. “Oh, please. Spare me the martyr act. You’re not fighting for survival. You’re fighting because you’re angry. Because you’ve been angry for centuries, and you don’t know how to stop.”

Kars flinched, just slightly. Then he stepped closer, his voice dropping to a growl. “You don’t know anything about us. About what we’ve lost.”

“No,” she agreed, tilting her chin up. “But I know what it’s like to be trapped in your own rage. To let it eat you alive until there’s nothing left.”

Silence stretched between them, thick and suffocating. The drums continued their relentless pounding, a countdown to something Nesta couldn’t name.

Kars turned away first, his shoulders rigid. “Go back to the barracks. Stay out of sight.”

Nesta didn’t answer. She was already moving, her boots crunching over frost-bitten grass as she headed toward the northern ridge. The wind howled around her, carrying the scent of pine and iron, of snow and blood.

She didn’t know what she would find when she reached the gathering. Didn’t know if she even wanted to.

But she knew one thing with absolute certainty: she wasn’t running.

Her fingers tightened around the hilt of her dagger as she watched the distant ridge, the drums echoing like a heartbeat in her skull.

---

The war tent stank of sweat and iron, the scent of old blood and newer ambition. Nesta stepped inside, her boots silent on the worn canvas floor, but the tension in the air was a living thing, coiling around her throat. Cassian stood beside the war table, his wings half-furled, the crimson Siphons on his knuckles glowing faintly in the dim light. Across from him, Devlon leaned over a map, his broad shoulders blocking the details, his face set in that permanent sneer.

Devlon didn’t look up as she entered. “This is Illyrian business, girl. Stay out of it.”

The words were a slap. Nesta’s fingers twitched at her sides, her nails biting into her palms. She didn’t bother correcting him about the *girl*—she wasn’t here for his respect. “This isn’t about Illyrians or outsiders,” she said, her voice sharp as a blade. “It’s about traitors.”

Devlon’s head snapped up, his dark eyes narrowing. “And what would you know about traitors, Archeron?”

Cassian’s jaw clenched, the muscle feathering beneath his skin. He opened his mouth, but Nesta stepped forward before he could speak, her chin lifting. “I know what it looks like when someone’s playing both sides. I know what it looks like when they’re too cowardly to face the consequences.”

Devlon’s lip curled. “You’re out of your depth.”

Cassian’s voice cut through the tension, low and commanding. “We’re handling it.”

Nesta turned her gaze to him, unflinching. His Siphons pulsed brighter, as if reacting to the storm in his chest. “Are you?” she asked, her voice quieter now, but no less dangerous.

The air between them crackled, thick with things unsaid. Cassian’s throat worked, his wings shifting slightly, as if he wanted to reach for her—or shake her. Instead, he turned away, his shoulders rigid. The Siphons on his hands flared crimson, casting shadows across the map. “You don’t understand what’s at stake,” he said, his voice barely audible, rough with something she couldn’t name.

---

The training grounds were a graveyard of dust and sweat, the air thick with the metallic tang of old blood. Nesta didn’t care. She welcomed the burn in her muscles, the way her lungs screamed with every breath. It was better than the nothingness that had taken root inside her chest.

She struck the dummy again, her fist connecting with the worn leather. The impact shuddered up her arm, but she barely felt it. Her knuckles were raw, the skin split in places, but she didn’t stop. She couldn’t. If she stopped, she’d have to think. And thinking was the last thing she wanted.

The sun hung low in the sky, casting long, jagged shadows across the camp. The warriors around her moved like ghosts, their wings tucked tight against their backs as they went about their drills. None of them so much as glanced her way. Good. She didn’t want their pity. Didn’t want their stares.

She spun, driving her knee into the dummy’s gut. The force of it sent the thing swaying, but she didn’t let up. Another punch. Another kick. Her breath came in ragged gasps, her braid unraveling with every movement, strands of hair sticking to the sweat on her neck.

“You’ll break your hand if you keep that up.”

Nesta didn’t turn. She knew that voice. Kars. The warrior who had been watching her for weeks, his dark eyes always calculating, always measuring. She didn’t answer. Just drove her fist into the dummy again.

“They’re moving sooner than we thought.”

That made her pause. She turned slowly, her chest heaving, her fingers curling into claws at her sides. Kars stood a few paces away, his arms crossed over his chest, his wings half-furled. He looked as tired as she felt.

“Who?” she demanded.

“Bellius’s faction.” His voice was low, steady. “They’re planning to ambush you at dusk. When the camp’s distracted by a false alarm.”

Nesta bared her teeth. “Let them come.”

Kars didn’t flinch. “They won’t fight fair.”

“Neither will I.”

A muscle feathered in his jaw. “You don’t understand what’s at stake for them. For us.”

Nesta stepped closer, her voice a snarl. “Then explain it to me.”

Kars held her gaze, his dark eyes unreadable. For a long moment, neither of them spoke. The wind howled through the mountains, carrying the scent of pine and damp earth. Then, quietly, he said, “Because you’re the only one who sees us as more than pawns.”

The words hit her like a physical blow. She staggered back a step, her breath catching. “What?”

Kars didn’t look away. “The others—they see us as weapons. Tools. Even Cassian, for all his talk of change, still sees us as soldiers first. But you?” He shook his head. “You look at us like we’re people.”

Nesta’s throat tightened. She wanted to argue, to deny it, but the words died on her lips. Because he was right. She *had* seen them. The way they flinched when the commanders barked orders. The way they hesitated before striking a weaker opponent. The way they carried themselves like men who had long since forgotten what it meant to be free.

“Why tell me this?” she asked finally.

Kars exhaled sharply. “Because if Bellius succeeds, nothing changes. And I’m tired of waiting for someone else to fix it.”

Nesta stared at him, the weight of his words settling over her like a shroud. The sun dipped lower, painting the sky in hues of gold and crimson. Dusk was coming.

She flexed her fingers, feeling the sting of her split knuckles. “Then let’s give them a fight they won’t forget.”

---

The door to Cassian’s quarters slammed shut behind her before she could think better of it.

Nesta didn’t wait for an invitation. The space was small, cramped—just a narrow bed, a washstand, a trunk overflowing with maps and weapons. The scent of him, of leather and wind and something wild, filled her lungs, and she hated that it made her pulse quicken.

Cassian stood by the bed, his dark wings half-furled, the crimson Siphons on his knuckles glowing faintly in the dim light. He didn’t turn. “If this is about the training schedule—”

“It’s not about the training schedule.” Her voice was sharp enough to cut. “It’s about you letting them walk all over you. Over *us*.”

That got his attention. He turned slowly, his jaw tight. “This isn’t about *us*, Nesta.”

“No?” She stepped closer, her skirts whispering against the rough-hewn floor. “Then what is it about? You bending over backward to keep the peace while they sneer at me behind your back? While they act like I’m some spoiled human pet you dragged along for your amusement?”

His wings twitched, the Siphons flaring brighter. “This is about keeping this camp from tearing itself apart. You think I *want* to play mediator? You think I enjoy watching them test you, waiting for you to break?”

“Then stop treating me like I’m the problem!” The words tore from her, raw and unfiltered. “Stop acting like I’m some fragile thing that needs protecting. I can handle myself.”

“Can you?” His voice rose, frustration boiling over. “Because from where I’m standing, you’ve spent the last month doing everything in your power to *prove* you don’t belong here. You snap at everyone, you refuse to train, you act like you’re above it all—”

“And whose fault is that?” she shot back. “You dragged me here. You *made* me come.”

“Because you were drowning in Velaris!” He took a step toward her, his wings flaring fully now, the crimson light casting shadows across his face. “Because you were so lost in your own misery that you couldn’t even see the people who were trying to help you!”

Nesta’s breath hitched. The truth of it struck too close, too deep. She bared her teeth. “I don’t need your help.”

“No?” His voice was a low growl, dangerously soft. “Then what do you need, Nesta? Because I sure as hell don’t know anymore.”

The question hung between them, heavy and suffocating. She wanted to scream. She wanted to hit him. She wanted—

She didn’t know what she wanted.

Cassian stepped closer, close enough that she could see the flecks of gold in his dark eyes, the way his chest rose and fell with each ragged breath. “You want to be part of this? Fine. But you don’t get to question my choices. Not when you won’t even *try*.”

The words were a spark to tinder. “I *am* trying,” she snarled. “Or did you miss the part where I just spent the last hour getting my ass handed to me by a bunch of Illyrian brutes?”

“That’s not what I meant and you know it.” His voice was rough, exhausted. “You’re so busy pushing everyone away that you can’t even see—”

“See what?” she demanded.

His gaze dropped to her mouth. Just for a second. But it was enough.

The air between them crackled, charged with something neither of them dared name.

Nesta’s breath caught.

Cassian’s wings flared wider, the Siphons glowing crimson. “You’re impossible,” he snarled, but there was no heat in it. Only exhaustion. And something else—something raw and aching that made her chest tighten.

Neither of them moved. Neither of them spoke.

The silence stretched, taut as a bowstring.

And then, without another word, Cassian turned away.

---

The wind howled through the Illyrian peaks, biting at Nesta’s cheeks as she crouched in the underbrush. The training camp sprawled below, a jagged scar of torchlight against the darkening sky. Kars moved beside her, silent as the shadows that pooled between the rocks. His hands were steady as he set the last of the snares, the wire glinting dully in the fading light.

“You don’t have to do this,” he murmured, not looking at her.

Nesta’s fingers tightened around the hilt of her dagger. The metal was cold, familiar. “Yes, I do.”

Kars exhaled through his nose, a sound that might have been a laugh if it weren’t so weary. “Why?”

The question hung between them, heavy as the storm clouds gathering overhead. Nesta stared at the camp, at the warriors who moved like ghosts through the flickering torchlight. Somewhere down there, Bellius was waiting. Smirking. Planning.

She could run. She could vanish into the mountains, let the wind and the snow swallow her whole. No one would blame her. No one would even notice.

But then she saw it—her reflection in the blade of her dagger. Hollow eyes. A mouth set in a line too sharp to be called a smile.

“I’m tired of running,” she said at last.

Kars stilled. For a heartbeat, she thought he might argue. Might tell her she was a fool, that this wasn’t her fight. But then he nodded, once, as if she’d confirmed something he’d already known.

“Then we fight.”

The words settled over her like armor.

Night fell swiftly in the mountains, the last traces of twilight swallowed by the encroaching dark. The camp was a chaos of shouting voices and clattering weapons as the false alarm was raised—wolves at the perimeter. Warriors scrambled, their Siphons flaring to life like fallen stars. Nesta watched from the shadows as they surged toward the tree line, leaving the heart of the camp exposed.

And then she saw him.

Bellius stepped into the clearing, his Siphons casting eerie red light across his smirking face. He didn’t even glance at the retreating warriors. His gaze locked onto hers, unshaken.

“Did you really think you could win?”

Nesta didn’t answer. She didn’t need to.

The wind carried the scent of pine and snow as she rose from her crouch, her boots silent against the frozen earth. The dagger in her hand gleamed, catching the moonlight like a sliver of ice.

Bellius’s smirk faltered for the barest second.

Nesta stepped into the clearing, her voice steady, her pulse a drumbeat in her veins.

“I don’t have to win,” she said. “I just have to make sure you lose.”

---

The night was a living thing, breathing and snarling around them. Nesta’s blade cut through the dark, her body moving before her mind could catch up—instinct, pure and vicious, guiding her hand. The weight of the sword was familiar now, the grip slick with sweat and something darker. Blood.

Bellius’s faction had come without warning, their blades flashing like teeth in the moonlight. Kars fought at her side, his loyalty a silent vow, his own sword a blur of motion. She didn’t have time to think, didn’t have time to feel. There was only the fight, the press of bodies, the metallic tang of blood in the air.

Then—wings.

A gust of wind tore through the clearing, sending leaves and dirt spiraling into the night. Nesta didn’t need to look to know who it was. The Siphons on his knuckles blazed crimson, casting eerie light over the chaos.

Cassian landed with a thud, his boots sinking into the damp earth. His voice cut through the snarls and grunts, a command that brooked no argument. *“Enough.”*

Bellius didn’t listen. Of course he didn’t. He lunged for Nesta, his blade aimed for her throat. She twisted, her own sword flashing up to meet his. Steel clashed, sparks flying between them. She didn’t hesitate. Didn’t flinch. Her blade found his side, biting deep.

Bellius stumbled, his smirk twisting into a snarl. *“This isn’t over.”*

Nesta didn’t answer. She didn’t have to.

Cassian was there then, his presence a storm at her back. His hand closed around her wrist, his grip firm but not unkind. *“Are you hurt?”*

She yanked her arm free, her breath ragged. *“I’m fine.”*

But her gaze lingered on him. The crimson light of his Siphons painted his face in stark relief, highlighting the sharp angles of his jaw, the concern in his dark eyes. Her heart pounded—not just from the fight, but from the weight of everything unsaid between them.

She turned away before he could see it. Before she could let herself feel it.
