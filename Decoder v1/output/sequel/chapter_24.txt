CHAPTER 24
The Edge of the Blade

The cold bit into Nesta’s bones before the dawn even dared to creep over the peaks. She woke with a gasp, her body screaming in protest—every muscle, every bruise a testament to the night before. The tent flap rustled in the wind, and for a moment, she lay there, staring at the canvas above her, listening to the unnatural quiet of the camp.

No laughter. No clatter of weapons. No drunken boasts from the warriors who had once swaggered past her like she was nothing more than a nuisance.

Now, they avoided her entirely.

She sat up, wincing as her ribs protested, and reached for the gray gown draped over the wooden chest at the foot of her cot. The fabric was immaculate, as always—Elain’s doing, no doubt. Nesta ran her fingers over the smooth wool, the only thing in this gods-forsaken place that didn’t feel like a punishment.

Outside, the air was sharp, the kind of cold that seeped into your lungs and made every breath ache. The training grounds were empty, save for a few warriors who glanced her way before quickly looking elsewhere. She ignored them, striding toward the central firepit where the embers still glowed faintly.

Kars stood apart from the others, his arms crossed over his broad chest, his dark eyes fixed on the northern ridge. He didn’t turn as she approached, but she knew he sensed her.

“You’re up early,” he said, voice low.

“So are you.”

A muscle in his jaw twitched. “Couldn’t sleep.”

Nesta followed his gaze. The northern cliffs loomed in the distance, their jagged edges swallowed by the predawn gloom. “Bellius’s men.”

Kars exhaled sharply. “They haven’t been seen since the ambush.”

“And?”

“And,” he said, finally turning to face her, “there are whispers. They’re regrouping.”

Her fingers curled into fists at her sides. “Where?”

“Near the cliffs.” His voice dropped even lower. “They’re not done with you.”

Nesta bared her teeth. “Let them come.”

Kars studied her for a long moment, his expression unreadable. Then, without another word, he turned and walked away, leaving her standing there, the wind tugging at the loose strands of her braided coronet.

She didn’t move. Didn’t blink.

The shadows on the ridge seemed to shift, slithering like serpents between the rocks. Her hand drifted to the dagger at her hip, fingers tightening around the hilt.

Let them come.

---

The war tent smelled of sweat and iron, the scent of old blood clinging to the canvas walls. Nesta stepped inside, her boots silent on the packed earth, her gray gown whispering against her legs. The fabric was too fine for this place, too clean, but she didn’t care. Let them see her as she was—unbroken, unbothered.

Devlon stood over the war table, his broad back to her, maps spread beneath his calloused fingers. Cassian was there too, his wings half-furled, the crimson Siphons on his knuckles glinting in the dim light. His dark hair was tied back, the sharp lines of his face unreadable.

They both turned at her entrance.

Devlon’s mouth twisted. “This is no place for a lady.”

Nesta didn’t flinch. “I’m not here to be a lady.”

Cassian’s gaze flicked to her, something unspoken passing between them. He knew why she was here. He always knew.

Devlon exhaled through his nose, a sound like a bull about to charge. “If you’ve come to whine about Bellius—”

“He’s a coward,” Nesta cut in, her voice slicing through the tension like a blade. “And you’re a fool for letting him lead.”

Devlon’s face darkened. “You know nothing of war, girl.”

“I know enough to see when a commander is leading his men to slaughter.” Her fingers curled at her sides, nails biting into her palms. “You’d rather let this camp burn than admit you’re wrong.”

Cassian stepped forward, his wings flaring slightly. “Enough.”

Devlon’s eyes narrowed. “You’d take her side over mine?”

“This isn’t about sides,” Cassian said, his voice low, dangerous. “It’s about survival.”

Nesta didn’t look away from Devlon. “Bellius will get them all killed. And you’ll let him.”

Devlon’s jaw clenched. “You overstep.”

“And you’re a coward,” she snapped.

Cassian moved between them, his body a barrier, his gaze locked onto hers. “We move at dawn,” he said, his voice leaving no room for argument. “Be ready.”

Nesta held Devlon’s stare for one heartbeat longer before turning on her heel. The tent flaps fell shut behind her, the cold mountain air biting at her skin.

But she didn’t stop. Not until she heard Cassian’s wings rustle, felt the weight of his presence behind her.

“This ends now,” he growled, his voice a low warning.

She didn’t turn around. She didn’t need to.

The edge of the blade was already at her throat.

---

The training grounds were silent save for the whisper of wind through the jagged peaks and the rhythmic scrape of Nesta’s blade against the whetstone. She had been at it for hours, her arms burning, her breath coming in sharp, controlled bursts. The morning mist clung to her skin, cool and damp, but she barely felt it. Her mind was a storm, a tempest of Cassian’s words, of Bellius’s sneer, of the way her own pulse had roared in her ears when the knife had pressed against her throat.

She hadn’t flinched. Not then. Not now.

The whetstone slid along the edge of her sword, the metal singing in protest. She had chosen the blade herself—plain, unadorned, its hilt wrapped in worn leather. It was nothing like the elegant weapons the Illyrian warriors favored, but it was hers. And it was sharp.

A shadow fell across the stone bench where she sat. She didn’t look up. “I’m not in the mood for company, Kars.”

The warrior leaned against the wooden post beside her, his arms crossed over his broad chest. His dark hair was tied back, a few strands escaping to frame his weathered face. “You’ve been at this since dawn,” he said, his voice low, unhurried. “Most would’ve collapsed by now.”

Nesta exhaled through her nose, her grip tightening on the hilt. “Most aren’t me.”

Kars hummed, a sound that might have been amusement. “No. They’re not.” He pushed off the post and crouched beside her, his knees cracking. “You’re not just training your body, you know.”

She finally looked at him, her gray eyes sharp as flint. “What’s that supposed to mean?”

He nodded at the sword in her hands. “You’re sharpening more than steel.”

Nesta bared her teeth. “I don’t need riddles.”

Kars didn’t flinch. “Then what do you need?”

The question hung between them, heavy and unanswered. She turned back to her blade, the whetstone moving in slow, deliberate strokes. “I need to not feel like I’m going to crawl out of my skin every time someone looks at me.”

Kars was quiet for a long moment. Then, softly, “Why are you still here, Nesta?”

Her hand stilled. The wind howled through the peaks, biting at her exposed skin. She could lie. She could tell him it was because Feyre had ordered it, because Cassian had dragged her here, because she had nowhere else to go. But the words died on her tongue.

“Because I’m tired of being afraid,” she said at last, her voice barely above a whisper.

Kars didn’t smile. Didn’t offer empty comfort. He simply nodded, as if she had confirmed something he already knew. “Then we fight.”

Nesta swallowed hard. The words settled into her bones, heavy and true. She had spent so long running—from the war, from her grief, from herself. But here, in the cold and the dirt and the relentless grind of training, she had stopped. And for the first time in months, she wasn’t sure she wanted to run anymore.

Kars reached into the pouch at his belt and pulled out a second whetstone, its surface smooth and well-used. He held it out to her. “Sharpen your blade,” he said, his voice steady. “And your mind.”

---

The night air was thick with the scent of pine and damp earth, the Illyrian Mountains looming like silent sentinels around the training grounds. Nesta moved with deliberate precision, her fingers brushing over the taut wire of the trap she was setting. The metal was cold, unforgiving—just like the stares she felt boring into her back.

Kars worked beside her, his movements fluid, practiced. He didn’t speak, but the tension in his shoulders was answer enough. The camp was too quiet. Not the quiet of rest, but the quiet of a held breath, of waiting.

“They’re watching,” Nesta murmured, her voice barely above the rustle of the wind through the trees.

Kars didn’t look up. “They’re always watching.”

She glanced over her shoulder. Shadows shifted at the edges of the torchlight—warriors, some leaning against the rough-hewn walls of the training ring, others lurking near the war tent. Their faces were unreadable, but their eyes were not. Some held curiosity, others something sharper. Hostility.

Nesta turned back to the trap, her jaw tightening. “They don’t know what to make of us.”

“They know what to make of you,” Kars corrected, his voice low. “They see a High Lady’s sister, a woman who doesn’t belong here. They see weakness.”

Her fingers stilled. “And what do you see?”

Kars finally met her gaze, his dark eyes unflinching. “I see someone who’s still standing.”

The words settled between them, heavy. Nesta exhaled sharply, resuming her work. The wire snapped into place with a quiet *click*.

“They’re waiting to see who wins,” Kars continued. “The warriors who follow Cassian, the ones who follow Devlon, and now… you.”

Nesta’s lips curled. “Then we give them a reason to choose us.”

Kars studied her for a long moment before nodding, just once. “Good.”

A gust of wind swept through the camp, carrying with it the faintest whisper of movement. Nesta’s head snapped up, her instincts prickling. The warriors in the shadows had gone still, their attention shifting toward the tree line.

Then—a howl.

Not the cry of a wolf. Too deep, too guttural. Too close.

Kars stiffened, his hand flying to the dagger at his belt. “That’s not—”

The howl came again, echoing through the mountains, closer this time. The torches flickered, as if the night itself had drawn in a breath.

Nesta’s pulse pounded in her ears. She didn’t wait for Kars to finish. She didn’t wait for the warriors to react.

She ran toward the sound.

---

The night air was thick with the scent of pine and damp earth, the Illyrian Mountains looming like silent sentinels around the training grounds. Nesta’s breath came in sharp, controlled bursts as she sprinted toward the sound—the clash of metal, the grunt of effort, the unmistakable rhythm of a fight.

She didn’t slow as she reached the edge of the clearing. Shadows moved between the trees, swift and predatory. Blades flashed in the moonlight, and then she saw them—Bellius’s faction, their faces twisted in grim satisfaction as they surrounded Kars and a handful of other warriors. Kars was holding his own, but barely. Blood streaked his arm, his wings half-furled in the tight space between the trees.

Nesta didn’t think. She lunged.

Her blade was in her hand before she could register drawing it, the weight familiar now after weeks of training. The first warrior didn’t see her coming. She drove her sword into his side, twisting as he gasped, his knees hitting the dirt. She was already moving, already turning, her body reacting before her mind could catch up.

Kars’s eyes widened as she appeared at his shoulder. “Nesta—”

“Stay behind me,” she snarled, baring her teeth.

The next attacker came at her with a snarl of his own, his blade arcing toward her throat. She parried, the impact vibrating up her arms, but she didn’t falter. She stepped into the strike, driving her knee into his gut before slashing upward. He staggered back, clutching his ribs, and she didn’t give him time to recover.

Something primal roared inside her. She had spent months feeling hollow, feeling nothing at all, but now—now there was fire in her veins. The blade in her hand was an extension of her rage, her frustration, her sheer, unrelenting will to survive.

A warrior lunged at Kars, and Nesta intercepted, her sword clashing against his. She twisted, using her momentum to shove him back, and then she was moving again, her feet light, her body fluid. She had trained for this. She had bled for this. And now, she would make them pay for every second of it.

Then—wings.

A gust of wind sent leaves and dirt swirling as Cassian landed in the center of the fray, his crimson Siphons blazing like embers in the dark. His wings were spread wide, his expression thunderous. “Enough.”

The word was a command, a warning, a promise of violence if they didn’t heed it.

Bellius stepped forward, his smirk sharp enough to cut. “This doesn’t concern you, Cassian.”

“Like hell it doesn’t.” Cassian’s voice was low, dangerous. His gaze flicked to Nesta, just for a second, and something unreadable passed over his face. Then he was moving, his sword drawn, his body coiled like a spring.

Bellius’s eyes locked onto Nesta. “You should have stayed in your pretty little house, Nesta Archeron.”

She didn’t answer. She didn’t need to.

Bellius lunged.

Nesta sidestepped, her blade flashing in the moonlight. She felt the resistance as steel met flesh, heard his sharp inhale as her sword found his side. He stumbled, his smirk twisting into a snarl. “This isn’t over.”

She didn’t lower her weapon. “It is for tonight.”

Bellius’s wings snapped open, and with a final, mocking laugh, he launched himself into the sky. His warriors followed, their shadows swallowed by the night.

Silence fell, broken only by the ragged breathing of those left behind. Nesta’s hands trembled, but she kept her grip on her sword. She didn’t look at Cassian. She didn’t look at anyone.

Kars clapped a hand on her shoulder, his voice rough with admiration. “You fight like a demon.”

Nesta didn’t answer. She was too busy listening to the echo of Bellius’s laughter, ringing in her ears like a promise.

This wasn’t over. Not by a long shot.

---

The wind howled through the Illyrian peaks, carrying the scent of snow and iron. Nesta’s breath came in sharp, ragged bursts, her fingers still clenched around the hilt of her blade. The weight of it was familiar now—comforting, even. The training grounds lay in quiet ruin around them, the earth churned by boots and the occasional scorch of Siphon fire.

Cassian’s wings were half-spread, a barrier between her and the warriors who had emerged from the shadows. His crimson Siphons glowed faintly, a warning. His voice was low, rough. “Are you hurt?”

Nesta didn’t look at him. Her gaze remained fixed on the warriors stepping forward, their faces a mix of curiosity and disdain. She shook her head. “No.” The word was a blade itself. “But they will be.”

Cassian’s jaw tightened. She could feel the tension radiating off him, the way his body coiled, ready to strike. But before he could speak, Devlon shouldered his way through the crowd.

The warrior’s face was a mask of smug superiority, his dark eyes flicking between them. “This isn’t over, General.”

Cassian didn’t flinch. His voice was a growl, low and lethal. “No. It’s not.”

Nesta’s fingers twitched around her sword. The metal was warm from use, the edge still slick with the remnants of the fight. She didn’t wait for Devlon to speak again. She didn’t need to.

She stepped forward, her boots crunching on the frozen earth. The warriors around them stilled, their breath misting in the cold air. Nesta’s voice cut through the silence, steady and unyielding.

“Then let’s finish it.”
