CHAPTER 14
The First Spark

The cold bit into Nesta’s bones before the sun had even thought of rising. She lay in the narrow cot, the thin wool blanket doing little to stave off the mountain chill, and listened to the distant howl of wind against the stone walls of the barracks. Her body ached—every muscle, every joint, a chorus of protest from the winged endurance race the day before. The memory of Kars’ words slithered through her mind like a serpent, coiling tight around her ribs.

*You’ll never be one of us. You’re weak. A liability.*

She shoved the thought away, as she had shoved away so many others. The room was dark, the other warriors still asleep, their breaths deep and even. Nesta swung her legs over the side of the cot, wincing as her feet met the frigid floor. She dressed quickly, her fingers numb as she braided her hair into its usual coronet, the ritual of it grounding her.

The training grounds were empty when she stepped outside, the first hints of dawn painting the sky in streaks of pale gold and lavender. She moved through the familiar drills—stretching, lunging, the slow, deliberate motions of a body learning to fight. The bruises from yesterday’s race throbbed, but she ignored them. Pain was just another sensation. Another thing to endure.

By the time the others began to stir, the sky had lightened to a dull, overcast gray. The warriors emerged from the barracks, their wings rustling as they stretched, their voices low and rough with sleep. Nesta kept her back straight, her face blank. She would not give them the satisfaction of seeing her falter.

Devlon stood at the center of the training grounds, his arms crossed over his broad chest, his expression unreadable. The warriors gathered around him, their murmurs dying into silence as he raised a hand.

“Today,” he said, his voice cutting through the stillness, “you will spar with live blades.”

A ripple of excitement passed through the crowd. Nesta’s stomach twisted.

“Pair up,” Devlon continued. “And remember—this is not a game. These are real weapons. One mistake, and you could kill your opponent.”

Nesta’s breath hitched. She had sparred with wooden swords before, but this—this was different. The weight of a real blade in her hand, the knowledge that a single misstep could end in blood.

Bellius stepped forward, his lips curling into a smirk as his gaze locked onto her. “I’ll take the human.”

The warriors around them chuckled, the sound sharp and mocking. Nesta said nothing. She walked to the weapons rack, her boots crunching on the frost-kissed earth, and selected a sword. The hilt was cold against her palm, the blade heavier than she remembered. She tested its weight, her fingers trembling.

Bellius twirled his own weapon with practiced ease, his eyes never leaving hers. “Scared, little mouse?”

Nesta didn’t answer. She adjusted her grip, her knuckles white against the leather-wrapped hilt.

Devlon’s voice rang out again. “Begin.”

Bellius lunged.

---

The world narrowed to the whistle of steel and the rasp of her own breath.

Bellius moved like a storm given form—relentless, brutal. His sword was an extension of his arm, each strike aimed to maim, to humiliate. Nesta met the first blow with a block, the impact vibrating up her arms, but she held firm. The second came low, a feint to her ribs, and she twisted away, her braid whipping over her shoulder.

“Still standing, are you?” Bellius sneered, circling her. “Thought you’d have collapsed by now.”

She didn’t answer. Words were a luxury she couldn’t afford. The warriors around them watched, their expressions a mix of amusement and disdain. Kars stood apart, arms crossed, his face unreadable. Devlon’s gaze was sharp, assessing, as if she were a puzzle he hadn’t yet solved.

Cassian’s voice cut through the tension like a blade. “Focus, Nesta.”

She didn’t look at him. Couldn’t. If she did, she’d see the concern in his eyes, the frustration, and she couldn’t bear it. Not now.

Bellius lunged again, his sword a silver arc toward her throat. She parried, the clash of steel ringing in her ears, but his strength sent her stumbling back. Her boots skidded on the loose gravel, and for a heartbeat, she teetered on the edge of the training ground, the drop to the valley below yawning beneath her.

A snarl tore from her throat. She righted herself, her grip tightening on the hilt of her sword.

“Pathetic,” Bellius taunted. “No wonder the High Lord sent you here to rot.”

Rage flared, white-hot and searing. She didn’t think. She struck.

Her blade grazed his side, a shallow cut that drew a line of crimson across his ribs. Bellius hissed, his eyes flashing with something darker than anger. “Bitch.”

He retaliated before she could blink.

His sword slashed toward her throat, and she barely ducked in time, the wind of its passage ruffling her hair. She countered, driving her knee toward his gut, but he twisted away, his laughter mocking. “Is that all you’ve got?”

She gritted her teeth. The warriors’ murmurs rose around them, a chorus of derision. She could feel their eyes on her, judging, waiting for her to break.

Cassian’s voice rang out again, sharper this time. “Nesta—”

She ignored him. Pride burned in her chest, a fire that refused to be smothered. She wouldn’t yield. Not to Bellius. Not to any of them.

Bellius feinted left, then drove his sword toward her ribs. She blocked, but the force of the blow sent her sprawling. Her back hit the ground hard, the breath knocked from her lungs. The warriors’ laughter erupted around her, cruel and mocking.

Kars’s expression darkened.

Bellius loomed over her, his sword raised for the final strike. “Stay down.”

She bared her teeth.

Cassian’s wings flared wide, his voice a growl that cut through the laughter like thunder. *“Enough.”*

---

The wind howled across the training grounds, carrying the scent of sweat and damp earth. Nesta’s muscles burned, but she refused to let the ache show as she straightened from her crouch. Around her, the jeers of the Illyrian warriors still rang in her ears, their laughter sharp as blades. She didn’t need to look to know they were watching, waiting for her to crumble.

Cassian’s shadow fell over her before she heard his footsteps. His wings were still half-spread, the crimson Siphons on his knuckles gleaming like fresh blood in the afternoon light. His jaw was set, his dark eyes stormy.

“You fought well,” he said, voice low.

Nesta didn’t answer. She didn’t need his praise. She didn’t need anything from him.

“But Bellius wasn’t just trying to humiliate you.” Cassian’s voice dropped further, rough with something she couldn’t name. “He was trying to *break* you.”

She whirled on him, her fingers curling into fists. “And what if he had? What do you care?”

His gaze darkened. “You know what I care about.”

“Survival?” she spat. “Is that what this is about? Making sure I don’t get myself killed before you’ve finished your little experiment?”

“This isn’t an experiment,” he growled.

“Then what is it?” She stepped closer, her chest nearly brushing his. “A way to salve your conscience? To prove you’re not the monster you think you are?”

His breath hitched. For a heartbeat, the air between them crackled, charged with something far more dangerous than anger. His eyes flicked to her mouth, then back up, his voice a rasp. “You think I don’t see what you’re doing?”

She bared her teeth. “And what am I doing, Cassian?”

“You’re not just fighting them.” His wings shifted, the leather of his gloves creaking as his hands flexed. “You’re fighting *me*.”

The words struck like a physical blow. She recoiled, her pulse roaring in her ears. “That’s not—”

“It is.” His voice was raw. “Every time you push me away, every time you refuse to let me in, you’re fighting me just as much as you’re fighting them.”

She wanted to scream. She wanted to hit him. She wanted to do anything but stand there, drowning in the weight of his words.

Instead, she turned on her heel and walked away.

The cliffs loomed ahead, the wind tearing at her braid as she strode toward the edge. The drop was sheer, the rocks below jagged and unforgiving. She didn’t stop until her toes curled over the precipice, the cold air biting at her skin.

Behind her, Cassian didn’t follow. But she could feel him there, a silent presence at her back, his unspoken question hanging between them like a blade.

*Why won’t you let me in?*

---

The hut smelled of damp wool and old smoke. Nesta sat on the edge of the narrow cot, her body screaming in protest with every movement. The training session had been brutal—worse than usual. The warriors had made sure of it.

She didn’t bother to undress, just tugged the laces of her leather vest loose enough to let the fabric gape open. The bruises blooming across her ribs were already darkening, a map of the day’s failures.

A knock at the door.

Nesta didn’t answer. She didn’t have to.

Kars slipped inside, his broad shoulders nearly filling the doorway. He carried a roll of bandages and a jar of salve, his expression unreadable. Without a word, he knelt before her, setting the supplies on the floor.

Nesta watched him, her throat tight. “You don’t have to do this.”

Kars didn’t look up as he uncorked the jar. The scent of mint and arnica filled the air. “I know.”

She flinched as his fingers brushed the worst of the bruises, his touch surprisingly gentle. “Then why?”

He paused, his dark eyes lifting to meet hers. “Because you’re not the only one who’s tired of being told what to do.” His voice was low, rough. “But you’re the only one who’s *fighting* back.”

Nesta’s breath hitched. The words settled into her bones, heavy and true. She had spent so long feeling hollow, so long drowning in the silence of her own mind, that the idea of someone else seeing her—*really* seeing her—felt like a blade twisting in her chest.

Outside, the warriors’ laughter echoed through the camp, sharp and cruel. A reminder of the war she was still waging, one she wasn’t sure she could win.

Kars worked in silence, his hands steady as he wrapped the bandages around her ribs. The pressure was a relief, the ache easing slightly beneath the binding.

When he finished, he didn’t rise immediately. Instead, he stayed there, kneeling before her, his voice barely above a whisper.

“They’re watching you, Nesta.” His gaze held hers, unwavering. “And not all of them want to see you fail.”

---

The night air was a blade against her skin, sharp and unrelenting. Nesta welcomed it. Welcomed the way it numbed her fingers, her cheeks, the hollow space behind her ribs where something—*everything*—had once lived. The Illyrian Mountains loomed around her, jagged and unforgiving, their peaks lost in the dark. She had come here to be forgotten. To forget.

But the wind carried whispers, and the whispers carried his name.

She didn’t turn when she heard the soft rustle of wings, the quiet shift of leather against muscle. She didn’t need to. She knew the weight of his presence, the heat of him even before he stepped beside her at the cliff’s edge.

Cassian said nothing. Just stood there, his wings folded tight against his back, his Siphons dark in the moonlight. The wind tugged at the strands of his dark hair, but his gaze remained fixed on the horizon, as if he could see something she couldn’t. As if he could see *her*.

Nesta swallowed. The silence between them was a living thing, coiled and waiting.

“Why do you care?”

The words slipped out before she could stop them, raw and brittle. She didn’t look at him. Didn’t want to see whatever pity or frustration might be in his eyes. But she felt his attention shift, felt the weight of it like a physical touch.

“Because I see what you’re capable of.” His voice was low, rough. “And I’m not going to let you throw it away.”

Her hands curled into fists at her sides, nails biting into her palms. The pain was a distraction, a fleeting anchor. “You don’t know anything about me.”

“I know you’re stronger than this.” A beat. “I know you’re better than this.”

The words struck something deep inside her, something she had buried so thoroughly she had almost convinced herself it didn’t exist. Rage flared, hot and bright, and she whirled on him, her braid whipping over her shoulder. “Better than *what*, Cassian? Better than a hollow shell? Better than a woman who can’t even feel her own gods-damned heart beating?”

His jaw tightened, but his eyes—*gods*, his eyes burned. “Better than the person you’re letting yourself become.”

She wanted to scream. Wanted to claw at him, at the world, at the suffocating weight of her own skin. But the words died on her tongue, choked by something she refused to name.

Cassian took a step closer, his wings shifting slightly, as if he were fighting the urge to reach for her. “You think I don’t see it? You think I don’t *know* what it’s like to feel like you’re drowning?” His voice dropped, rough with something that sounded dangerously like pain. “I’ve been there, Nesta. I’ve *been* there.”

She wanted to laugh. Wanted to tell him he didn’t understand, that no one did. But the truth was a blade in her chest, and she had no defense against it.

His fingers brushed against hers, a fleeting touch, there and gone. But it was enough. Enough to send a jolt through her, enough to make her breath catch.

She pulled away, her voice raw. “*Don’t.*”

---

The wind howled through the Illyrian peaks, biting at Nesta’s exposed skin as she turned from the training grounds. The gray gown she wore—immaculate despite the dust and grime of this place—fluttered around her legs, the fabric whispering against the rocky earth. She had no intention of staying to hear whatever empty platitudes Cassian had prepared for her this time.

But then he was there, stepping into her path with that infuriating ease of his, wings half-spread to block her retreat. His dark hair was windswept, his crimson Siphons glinting like embers in the fading light. The leather of his flying gear creaked as he shifted, his jaw set in that way that made her teeth clench.

“Nesta, wait.” His voice was low, urgent, the kind of tone that suggested he actually believed she’d listen.

She didn’t. She sidestepped, but he mirrored her, his wings rustling like a warning. “Move.”

“There’s something you need to know.”

“I don’t *need* to know anything from you.” The words came out sharper than she intended, but she didn’t take them back. She never took anything back.

Cassian exhaled through his nose, a sound that might have been a laugh if it weren’t so devoid of humor. “Fine. But you *should* know it.” He leaned in slightly, close enough that she caught the scent of leather and wind and something darker—something like the storm brewing in his eyes. “The warriors are talking. Some of them are starting to see you as more than just the High Lady’s sister. They’re seeing you as a fighter.”

Nesta barked a laugh. “How flattering. Did you come all this way to tell me I’m finally not a complete disappointment?”

His gaze hardened. “No. I came to tell you that others see it too—and not in a good way.” A pause. “Bellius is planning something.”

The name sent a flicker of something through her—annoyance, maybe, or the faintest prickle of awareness. She ignored it. “I don’t care what that overgrown peacock is planning.”

“You should.” Cassian’s voice dropped, rough with an edge that made her skin tighten. “He’s not just posturing. He’s looking for a way to put you in your place, and he won’t stop at words.”

Nesta crossed her arms. “Let him try.”

Cassian’s wings twitched, as if the very idea of it offended him. “Damn it, Nesta—”

“I don’t need your warnings, Cassian.” The words lashed out before she could stop them, her voice a blade honed to a razor’s edge. “I can handle myself.”

“I know you can.” His voice was quiet now, but no less intense. “But you don’t have to do it alone.”

The words hit her like a physical blow, stealing the breath from her lungs. She recoiled, her fingers curling into fists at her sides. “What the hell is that supposed to mean?”

Cassian didn’t flinch. “It means I’m not your enemy. No matter how much you want me to be.”

The wind howled between them, whipping strands of hair across her face. She didn’t bother to brush them away. “Why are you telling me this?” The question slipped out before she could stop it, her voice barely more than a whisper.

For a heartbeat, he just looked at her—really looked at her—and the weight of it was almost unbearable. Then his mouth twisted, like he was about to say something that would only make this worse.

But she didn’t wait to hear it.

Her breath caught, her chest tight with something she refused to name. For the first time, she hesitated.

---

The night air was thick with the scent of pine and damp earth, the kind of cold that seeped into bones and refused to let go. Nesta stood frozen on the cliff’s edge, the wind tugging at the loose strands of her braided coronet, her fingers clenched so tightly her nails bit into her palms.

Cassian’s wings were a dark slash against the starlit sky, his voice raw in a way she hadn’t heard before. *"Because I need you to *see* me, Nesta. Not as your jailer. Not as your enemy. As someone who—"*

The words died on his lips, his jaw tightening as if he’d swallowed them whole. Her pulse hammered in her throat, her mind a whirl of half-formed thoughts, of things she didn’t want to feel, didn’t want to acknowledge. The wind howled between them, carrying the weight of everything unsaid.

Then—a shout.

Not the usual drunken bellow of warriors at their revelry, but something sharper. Angrier.

Cassian’s head snapped toward the sound, his body tensing. *"Stay here."* The command was a whip-crack, leaving no room for argument.

He was gone before she could reply, his wings unfurling with a snap of leather and wind. The darkness swallowed him whole.

Nesta didn’t think. She didn’t let herself.

Her feet moved before her mind could catch up, sprinting after him, her boots kicking up dirt and pebbles as she raced down the narrow path toward the training grounds. The shouts grew louder, a chorus of raised voices, the clatter of weapons. Her breath came in ragged gasps, her lungs burning, but she didn’t slow.

She burst into the clearing just as Cassian landed with a thud, his wings flaring wide to halt his momentum. The warriors were a seething mass of muscle and fury, their faces twisted in anger. At the center of it all, a young warrior knelt in the dirt, his arm bent at an unnatural angle, his face pale with pain.

Bellius stood over him, his expression smug, his voice dripping with venom. *"He’s weak. Just like her."*

Nesta’s blood turned to ice.

The words hung in the air, heavy with implication. The warriors shifted, their gazes flickering between Bellius, Cassian, and—now—her. She could feel their judgment, their disgust, as if it were a physical thing pressing against her skin.

Cassian’s wings bristled, his Siphons flaring crimson in the torchlight. *"Enough."*

But Nesta’s focus was on the young warrior. On the way his breath hitched, the way his good hand trembled against the dirt. She didn’t think. She didn’t let herself.

She stepped forward.

---

The crowd of warriors parted before her like a sea, their murmurs dying as Nesta pushed through. The scent of sweat and iron hung thick in the air, the training grounds alive with the clatter of wooden swords and the grunts of sparring males. But all of it faded when she saw him—the young warrior crumpled on the dirt, his face pale as death, his arm bent at an angle that made her stomach clench.

"What happened?" Her voice cut through the noise, sharp as a blade.

The warrior’s dark eyes flickered up at her, glazed with pain. His lips pressed into a thin line, teeth digging into the lower one as if to stifle a cry. Behind him, Bellius stood with his arms crossed, a smirk twisting his mouth. "He fell," the warrior sneered. "Just like you always do."

Nesta’s vision narrowed. The words should have stung. Should have sent her spiraling into the familiar rage, the need to lash out, to prove him wrong. But the warrior’s pain was a living thing between them, and something older, deeper, rose in her instead.

Instinct.

She dropped to her knees beside him, her gray gown pooling in the dust. The warriors around them muttered, their expressions shifting from amusement to something like shock. She ignored them. Ignored the way Cassian stepped forward, his voice low and warning. *"Nesta—"*

She didn’t look at him.

Her hands moved without thought, fingers probing the warrior’s arm with a precision that surprised even her. The bone was misaligned, the flesh hot and swollen beneath her touch. The young male hissed, his body tensing, but she didn’t flinch. "Hold still," she ordered, and there was something in her voice—something that brooked no argument.

He obeyed.

She didn’t hesitate. One hand braced his shoulder, the other gripped his wrist. A swift, sharp pull. A crack that echoed through the sudden silence.

The warrior gasped, his back arching off the ground, but the pain in his eyes eased almost instantly. His breath came in ragged bursts, his good hand clawing at the dirt before relaxing.

The crowd was silent. Even Bellius had gone still, his smirk slipping.

Nesta stood, brushing the dust from her knees. The young warrior blinked up at her, his face still pale but no longer twisted in agony. "Better?" she asked.

He nodded, swallowing hard. "Yes. Thank you."

She didn’t acknowledge the gratitude. Didn’t need it. The satisfaction of the job done was enough.

Bellius’s smirk had vanished entirely now, his jaw tight. "Lucky shot," he muttered.

Nesta’s gaze locked onto his. "No," she said, quiet and lethal. "Just skill."
