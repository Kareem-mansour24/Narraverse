CHAPTER 22
The Weight of Wings

The predawn air was sharp enough to flay skin from bone. Nesta welcomed it, the sting of the wind against her face as she stepped from the rough-hewn barracks. The Illyrian camp sprawled around her, silent and watchful, as if the very mountains held their breath.

Her body ached. Not just from the training—though the bruises along her ribs and the fire in her muscles were proof enough of that—but from the weight of last night’s confrontation. The way Bellius had sneered at her, the way his warriors had closed ranks, their eyes gleaming with something darker than hatred. She had faced it all with her chin lifted, her mouth a thin, unyielding line.

And yet, the hollowness inside her had not lessened. If anything, it had deepened, a yawning chasm where something vital should have been.

She moved through the camp, her boots crunching over frost-kissed gravel. The warriors she passed averted their gazes, their murmurs dying on their lips as she neared. She ignored them, ignored the way her pulse quickened at the silence, the way her fingers twitched at her sides.

A shadow detached itself from the gloom near the training grounds. Kars. His broad shoulders were hunched against the cold, his dark eyes fixed on the horizon. He turned as she approached, his expression unreadable.

“You’re up early,” he said, his voice rough.

Nesta didn’t bother with pleasantries. “What is it?”

Kars exhaled through his nose, his breath curling in the frigid air. “Bellius’s men. They’re talking.”

“And?”

“And they’re not done with you.” His jaw tightened. “Whatever they have planned, it’s not over.”

Nesta bared her teeth. “I didn’t expect it would be.”

Kars studied her, his gaze lingering on the bruises along her collarbone, the way her hands flexed at her sides. “You should be careful.”

“Careful,” she echoed, the word bitter on her tongue. “Like I was last night?”

“Last night was a warning.” His voice dropped. “Next time, they won’t hold back.”

Nesta laughed, a sharp, humorless sound. “They never do.”

Kars didn’t smile. “I’m serious, Nesta. These aren’t the kind of men who forget a slight. And they don’t forgive.”

She tilted her head, the wind tugging at the loose strands of her braided coronet. “Then I won’t ask for forgiveness.”

For a moment, Kars said nothing. Then, quietly, “You’re not what I expected.”

Nesta’s fingers curled into fists. “And what did you expect?”

“Someone broken,” he admitted. “Someone who would crumble.”

She met his gaze, unflinching. “I’m not broken.”

Kars held her stare, then gave a slow nod. “No. You’re not.”

The wind howled between them, biting at her skin, but Nesta didn’t shiver. She lifted her chin, her eyes fixed on the horizon where the first pale light of dawn crept over the peaks.

“Then I’ll be ready.”

---

The wind howled through the jagged peaks of the Illyrian Mountains, biting at Nesta’s exposed skin like a thousand tiny teeth. She stood at the edge of the training camp, the gray gown she wore—immaculate despite the grime of the place—whipping around her legs. The dawn light had fully bled into the sky now, painting the cliffs in hues of gold and rose, but the beauty of it was lost on her. She had spent the last hour watching the warriors spar, their grunts and the clash of steel a distant hum in her ears. She had not joined them. Not yet.

A shadow passed over her, and she didn’t need to look up to know it was him.

Cassian landed with a soft thud behind her, his wings folding neatly against his back. The crimson Siphons on his knuckles glinted in the morning light, a stark contrast to the dark leather of his gloves. He didn’t speak at first, just stood there, his presence a weight she could feel even before he opened his mouth.

“You shouldn’t be out here alone,” he said, his voice low.

Nesta didn’t turn. “I’m not alone. There are dozens of warriors within shouting distance.”

“That’s not what I meant.” His voice was tight, the kind of tight that came from holding back words he didn’t want to say. She knew that tone. Knew it well.

She finally turned to face him, her sharp cheekbones catching the light. “Then what did you mean?”

Cassian’s jaw clenched. “We need to talk. Somewhere private.”

Nesta crossed her arms. “If this is about yesterday—”

“It’s not about yesterday.” His wings shifted, restless. “It’s about what’s coming.”

A cold prickle ran down her spine, but she kept her expression carefully blank. “Then say it.”

Cassian exhaled sharply through his nose, as if debating whether to push. Then he stepped closer, his voice dropping to a murmur. “Not here. Follow me.”

She wanted to refuse. Wanted to dig her heels into the rocky ground and tell him she wouldn’t be ordered around like some errant child. But the look in his eyes—the grim set of his mouth—made her pause. She nodded once, curtly, and followed him away from the camp, toward the cliffs where the wind screamed loud enough to drown out their words.

They didn’t speak until they were far enough that the sounds of the camp were a distant murmur. Cassian turned to her, his dark eyes unreadable. “Bellius’s faction has been in contact with the Bloodfang Clan.”

Nesta’s stomach twisted. She knew the name. Everyone in Illyria did. The Bloodfang were the worst of them—brutal, ruthless, the kind of warriors who didn’t just kill their enemies but made sure they suffered first. “And?”

“And they’re planning something bigger than an assassination.” Cassian’s voice was flat, but his hands flexed at his sides, the Siphons flaring. “An uprising. They’re not just targeting you. They’re targeting the entire Illyrian leadership. They want to burn the system to the ground and take control.”

Nesta’s breath came a little faster. She had known Bellius was a threat, but this—this was something else. This was war. “How do you know?”

“I saw the messages myself. They’re moving weapons, gathering allies. They’re not just talking. They’re preparing.” He stepped closer, his voice dropping. “Nesta, you need to leave. Now.”

She laughed, a sharp, humorless sound. “Leave? You want me to run?”

“This isn’t a joke.” His wings flared slightly, the feathers ruffling in the wind. “If they get their hands on you—”

“They won’t.” She cut him off, her voice steady. “I’m not some helpless damsel, Cassian. I’m not running.”

“Damn it, Nesta.” His voice rose, frustration bleeding through. “This isn’t about pride. This is about survival. If they catch you, they’ll use you against us. Against *me*.”

“Then don’t let them catch me.” She met his gaze, unflinching. “I’m not leaving. Not from them, not from you.”

Cassian’s wings flared fully now, the feathers bristling with his anger. “You’re being reckless.”

“And you’re being overbearing.” She bared her teeth. “I didn’t ask you to protect me. I didn’t ask for any of this.”

“No, you just *demand* it.” His voice was a low growl, the words laced with something darker, something that made her chest tighten. “You demand I train you, demand I treat you like one of them, and then you throw it back in my face when I try to keep you alive.”

Nesta’s fingers curled into fists. “Because you don’t get to decide what I do with my life. Not anymore.”

Cassian’s breath came fast, his chest rising and falling. For a moment, she thought he might reach for her, might try to shake some sense into her. But he didn’t. He just stood there, his wings trembling with the effort of holding himself back.

“This isn’t a game, Nesta,” he said, his voice raw. “People will die.”

She didn’t look away. “Then let’s make sure it’s not me.”

---

The wind howled through the Illyrian peaks, a bitter song that cut through the thin fabric of Nesta’s gown. She had long since stopped shivering. The cold was just another layer of numbness, another weight pressing down on skin that had forgotten how to feel.

She crouched in the shadows of the training camp’s outer wall, her breath shallow, her fingers curled into the rough stone. The meeting had been called without her—of course it had. Devlon had made it clear from the start that her presence was tolerated, not welcomed. That she was a problem to be managed, not a warrior to be trained.

The murmur of voices drifted from the central fire pit, where the senior warriors had gathered. She caught snatches of conversation—Bellius’s name, spoken in a tone that made her stomach twist.

*They’re planning something.*

She had known it, of course. Had felt the shift in the air, the way the warriors’ gazes lingered on her a second too long, the way their voices dropped when she passed. But hearing it confirmed—hearing the edge in Devlon’s voice when he said, *Tonight*—sent a jolt through her.

A rustle of movement. Nesta tensed, pressing herself deeper into the darkness as a figure detached from the group. Kars. His face was tight, his usual easy grin absent. He moved with purpose, scanning the shadows until his eyes locked onto hers.

“They’re moving tonight,” he said, voice low. “Bellius wants to make an example of you.”

Nesta’s pulse spiked, but she kept her expression blank. “An example.”

Kars nodded, his jaw tight. “He’s been pushing for it since you arrived. Tonight, he convinced Devlon.”

She should have been afraid. Should have felt something—rage, panic, anything. But there was only the hollow ache in her chest, the same emptiness that had followed her from Velaris.

Then Cassian stepped into the firelight.

His wings were half-spread, the crimson of his Siphons glowing like embers in the dark. His gaze found hers instantly, as if he had known exactly where she would be.

“We need to act,” he said, his voice rough. “Now.”

Nesta’s breath hitched. She had told him to leave her alone. Had thrown his kindness back in his face, had made it clear she wanted nothing from him. And yet here he was, standing between her and the wolves at her door.

Kars shifted beside her, his eyes darting between them. “What’s the plan?”

Cassian didn’t look away from her. “We get her out of here. Then we deal with Bellius.”

Nesta’s fingers curled into fists. She could run. Could let Cassian spirit her away, could let him fight her battles for her. But the thought of it—of being helpless, of being *saved*—made her stomach churn.

The shadows of the mountains loomed around them, vast and unforgiving. Time was running out.

And for the first time in months, Nesta felt something.

Not fear. Not anger.

A choice.

---

The wind howled through the Illyrian peaks, biting at Nesta’s cheeks as she stood before the gathered warriors. The training camp sprawled below them, a jagged scar of tents and weapons, but her focus was on the male before her—Devlon, his broad shoulders rigid, his jaw set in a line that spoke of stubborn pride.

Cassian stood at her side, his wings half-spread, the crimson glow of his Siphons casting long shadows across the rocky ground. The warriors around them shifted, their murmurs dying into silence as Nesta stepped forward.

“You know what Bellius is doing,” she said, her voice cutting through the wind like a blade. “And yet you do nothing.”

Devlon’s mouth twisted. “This is not your concern, lady.”

“It is my concern when he’s turning this camp into a battleground.” Her fingers curled into fists at her sides. “You’d rather let him tear this place apart than admit you’re wrong.”

The warriors around them stilled. A few exchanged glances, their loyalty fraying at the edges.

Devlon’s eyes darkened. “You overstep.”

Nesta bared her teeth. “And you’re a coward.”

A muscle feathered in his jaw. “Watch your tongue.”

“Or what?” She tilted her chin up. “You’ll punish me? Fine. But the warriors see it. They see you letting Bellius poison this camp.”

Cassian’s voice was low, lethal. “This ends tonight.” His wings flared slightly, the Siphons on his knuckles burning brighter. “With or without you.”

Devlon’s gaze snapped to him, his own temper flaring. “You forget your place, General.”

Cassian didn’t flinch. “And you’re a fool if you think this won’t end in blood.”

The wind howled between them, a living thing, as the warriors watched—waiting.

---

The last of the daylight bled from the sky, staining the jagged peaks of the Illyrian Mountains in hues of violet and gold. Nesta moved through the forms Kars had drilled into her—each strike precise, each step measured, her body a blade honed by weeks of relentless training. The warriors watched from the edges of the training grounds, their shadows long and silent. They had learned not to approach her when she was like this, when the world narrowed to the weight of her sword and the burn in her muscles.

Kars lingered near the armory, his arms crossed over his chest. "You're holding back," he called, voice rough but not unkind.

Nesta didn't answer. She twisted, bringing her sword down in a vicious arc that would have split a man from collarbone to hip. The wind caught the blade, whispering against the steel like a warning.

She wasn't holding back. She was waiting.

For what, she didn't know.

The sun dipped below the horizon, and the air turned sharp with the promise of night. Nesta lowered her sword, her breath steady despite the exertion. The warriors had dispersed, their laughter and rough voices fading into the dark. Only Kars remained, his gaze heavy on her back.

"You should rest," he said.

Nesta wiped the sweat from her brow with the back of her hand. "I'm not tired."

Kars exhaled through his nose, a sound that might have been a laugh if it weren't so weary. "Stubborn as the mountains themselves." He turned to leave, then paused. "Cassian's here."

Her fingers tightened around the hilt of her sword.

Kars didn't wait for a response. The crunch of his boots on gravel faded, leaving Nesta alone with the wind and the weight of what was coming.

She didn't turn when she heard the soft rustle of wings, the familiar tread of boots. Cassian stopped a few paces away, close enough that she could feel the heat of him, the steady rhythm of his breathing. She kept her gaze fixed on the horizon, where the last traces of light clung to the peaks.

"We need to talk," he said.

Nesta rolled her shoulders, as if she could shrug off the tension coiling in her chest. "About what?"

"About what happens next."

She finally looked at him. His dark hair was windswept, his crimson Siphons glinting dully in the twilight. The flying leathers he wore were worn but immaculate, molded to the hard lines of his body. He was every inch the warrior, every inch the male she had spent months pretending didn't exist.

Nesta turned away. "There's nothing to discuss."

Cassian stepped closer. "Bellius won't let this go. Neither will the others."

"I can handle myself."

"I know you can." His voice was low, rough. "But you don't have to. Not alone."

The words settled between them, heavy and unspoken. Nesta swallowed hard. She had spent so long building walls, so long pretending she didn't care, that she had almost convinced herself it was true. But the hollow place inside her chest ached at the sound of his voice, at the quiet certainty in his words.

She forced herself to meet his gaze. "What do you want from me, Cassian?"

His jaw tightened. "I want you to let me in."

The wind howled through the mountains, carrying the sound of distant wings—someone was watching. Nesta's breath hitched as Cassian closed the distance between them, his voice barely audible. "I won't let them hurt you."

She should have pushed him away. Should have told him she didn't need his protection, his promises. But the words died on her lips, and all she could manage was a whisper. "I know."

The wind carried the truth of it between them, raw and unguarded. And for the first time in months, the hollow place inside her didn't feel so empty.
