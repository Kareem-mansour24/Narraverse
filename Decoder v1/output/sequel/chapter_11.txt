CHAPTER 11
The Breaking Point

The hut was too quiet.

Nesta had woken before dawn, as she always did now, her body a map of aches and bruises from the previous day’s knife-throwing. The competition had been a farce—Bellius and his cronies had made sure of that, their laughter sharp as the blades they’d hurled just wide enough to graze her skin. She hadn’t flinched. Hadn’t given them the satisfaction.

She dressed in silence, pulling on the rough-spun trousers and tunic that had become her uniform. The fabric was stiff against her skin, still damp from yesterday’s rain. She braided her hair with practiced efficiency, the motion so familiar it barely required thought. Her fingers moved on their own, weaving the strands into a tight coronet at the crown of her head. The mirror above the washbasin was cracked, but she didn’t need it to know how she looked: hollow-eyed, sharp-angled, every line of her face carved by exhaustion.

The training grounds were already stirring when she stepped outside. The air was thick with the scent of damp earth and the faint metallic tang of blood from yesterday’s sparring. She ignored it, ignored the way her muscles protested as she stretched, ignored the way her ribs ached where Bellius’s boot had connected during the last round of hand-to-hand.

Devlon stood at the center of the clearing, his arms crossed over his chest, his expression unreadable. The warriors gathered around him in a loose semicircle, their wings rustling in the predawn breeze. Nesta took her place at the edge of the group, her back straight, her face a mask.

“Today,” Devlon said, his voice cutting through the murmur of the crowd, “we test endurance.” A few of the warriors shifted, exchanging glances. Nesta didn’t react. She already knew what was coming. “The northern cliffs. First to the top wins.”

Bellius smirked, cracking his knuckles. “And who do I get to race, Commander?”

Devlon’s gaze flicked to Nesta. “Her.”

A ripple of laughter ran through the crowd. Nesta didn’t look at them. She didn’t need to. She could feel their amusement like a physical weight, pressing down on her shoulders.

Cassian wasn’t there. Not yet. She told herself she didn’t care.

Devlon continued, “The rules are simple. No wings. No Siphons. Just you, the rock, and whatever strength you’ve got left.” His eyes lingered on Nesta for a beat too long. “And if you fall, you’d best hope the rocks break your neck before the healers get to you.”

Nesta exhaled through her nose. She’d climbed these cliffs before. She knew their jagged edges, their treacherous handholds. She also knew Bellius would cheat.

She didn’t care.

“Begin when you’re ready,” Devlon said, stepping back.

Nesta didn’t wait. She turned on her heel and strode toward the base of the cliffs, her boots crunching over loose stones. The rock face loomed above her, a sheer wall of gray and black, its surface slick with morning dew. She flexed her fingers, feeling the stiffness in her joints, the way her muscles trembled with exhaustion before she’d even begun.

Bellius sauntered up beside her, his grin wide and mocking. “Try not to break anything important, Archeron.”

She didn’t look at him. She didn’t need to. She could hear the smugness in his voice, could practically feel the heat of his breath on her skin.

Devlon raised his arm. “Go.”

Nesta moved.

Her hands found purchase on the cold rock, her fingers digging into the crevices. She pulled herself up, her muscles burning, her breath coming in sharp, controlled bursts. Below her, Bellius’s grunts echoed as he scrambled after her, his movements clumsy, his arrogance slowing him down.

She didn’t look down. She didn’t look back.

The wind howled around her, whipping at her clothes, her hair. The higher she climbed, the colder it became, the air thinning, her lungs straining. Her fingers were numb, her grip slipping. She gritted her teeth and kept going.

She could hear Bellius below her, his breath ragged, his curses carrying on the wind. He was gaining.

Nesta’s arms shook. Her legs burned. But she didn’t stop.

She wouldn’t stop.

Not for him. Not for them.

Not for anyone.

At the base of the cliffs, Devlon lowered his arm. The warriors watched in silence as Nesta and Bellius ascended, their figures growing smaller against the rock face.

Bellius smirked, cracking his knuckles again. “This’ll be over quick.”

Devlon didn’t answer.

Nesta’s breath was steady, but her hands trembled. She didn’t look down.

She wouldn’t.

Bellius’s grin widened. “Ready to lose, Archeron?”

Devlon gave the signal.

And Nesta climbed.

---

The rock bit into her palms, cold and unyielding. Nesta ignored the sting, the way her fingers ached as she hauled herself up another inch. Below, the wind howled through the cliffs, carrying the murmurs of the warriors gathered to watch. Their voices were a distant hum, drowned out by the pounding of her own blood in her ears.

Bellius moved above her with infuriating ease, his massive frame scaling the cliff face as if it were nothing more than a set of stairs. He didn’t bother to check if she followed—he knew she would. Or at least, he thought he did.

She gritted her teeth and reached for the next handhold, her muscles screaming in protest. The climb was brutal, the rock face jagged and unforgiving, but she refused to slow. Refused to let him see her struggle.

A pebble skittered past her face, dislodged by Bellius’s boot. She flinched but didn’t look down. Another followed, then another, raining down in a deliberate shower. She twisted to the side, her body pressing flat against the cliff as the stones clattered against the rock where she’d just been.

“Careful, Archeron,” Bellius called down, his voice dripping with false concern. “Wouldn’t want you to slip.”

She didn’t answer. She wouldn’t give him the satisfaction.

Below, the warriors watched, their expressions a mix of amusement and disdain. Kars stood among them, arms crossed, his face unreadable. And then there was Cassian.

His jaw was clenched so tight she could see the muscle in his cheek twitch from here. His wings were rigid at his back, the crimson Siphons on his knuckles glowing faintly, as if straining against some invisible force. He didn’t move, didn’t speak, but the tension radiating off him was palpable.

She forced her attention back to the climb. Bellius was pulling ahead, his movements efficient, ruthless. He didn’t care if she kept up—he wanted her to fail. Wanted her to fall.

Her fingers scraped against a loose outcropping, and for a heartbeat, she was weightless. The world tilted, the ground rushing up to meet her—

She caught herself.

Her breath came in ragged gasps, her heart hammering against her ribs. The rock beneath her fingers was slick with sweat, her grip tenuous. She didn’t dare look down.

Bellius’s laughter echoed off the cliffs, sharp and mocking. “You’re going to fall.”

She didn’t answer. She wouldn’t give him the satisfaction of a response.

Instead, she fixed her gaze on the next handhold, her fingers curling around the cold stone. She pulled herself up, one agonizing inch at a time.

She wouldn’t fall.

Not today.

---

The wind howled against the cliffs, biting at Nesta’s exposed skin as she hauled herself up the sheer rock face. Her muscles screamed, her lungs burned, but she didn’t stop. Couldn’t stop.

Below, the Illyrian warriors watched in silence, their usual jeers replaced by something like awe. Or unease. She didn’t care. She only cared about the summit—about reaching it before Bellius.

The bastard was ahead of her, his broad back just visible above the jagged outcrop. His wings were tucked tight, his movements fluid, effortless. He turned, his dark eyes locking onto hers, and a slow, cruel smile curled his lips.

Nesta’s fingers slipped on the damp stone.

A rock clattered past her, dislodged by Bellius’s boot. Then another. She twisted, pressing herself against the cliff as the debris rained down. But he wasn’t done.

A larger stone, jagged and deliberate, flew straight for her face.

She ducked, but not fast enough. Pain exploded across her temple, hot and bright. Blood trickled down her cheek, warm against the cold air.

A gasp rippled through the warriors below. Cassian’s voice cut through the wind, sharp with command. *"Enough!"*

Bellius didn’t even glance down. His smirk only deepened as Nesta wiped the blood from her face, her fingers coming away smeared red.

Rage burned through her, white-hot and searing. She didn’t look at him. Didn’t give him the satisfaction. Instead, she reached higher, her grip unyielding, her body moving faster now, driven by something deeper than pain.

The summit was close. So close.

Bellius snarled something under his breath, his own pace quickening. But Nesta was relentless. Her breath came in ragged gasps, her vision swimming, but she didn’t slow. Didn’t falter.

Then—her hand closed over the edge.

She hauled herself up, her knees scraping against the rough stone as she dragged herself onto the flat summit. The wind tore at her hair, her clothes, but she didn’t care. She was here.

First.

Bellius’s boots thudded onto the rock behind her. She didn’t turn. Didn’t acknowledge him. She only stood, her chest heaving, her body trembling with exhaustion—and triumph.

His breath was hot against her ear. *"Beginner’s luck,"* he snarled.

Nesta didn’t respond. She stared out at the horizon, at the endless expanse of sky and mountain, her blood still dripping onto the stone at her feet.

The wind howled, wild and free.

And for the first time in months, she felt something.

Alive.

---

The wind still howled, but it was no match for the silence that fell over the training grounds. Nesta’s breath came in ragged gasps, her lungs burning as if she’d inhaled smoke. The taste of copper lingered on her tongue, her knuckles split and bleeding where they’d met Bellius’s jaw. The stone beneath her boots was slick with it—her blood, his, she didn’t know. Didn’t care.

Devlon’s voice cut through the haze. “Winner: Nesta Archeron.”

A beat of stunned quiet. Then the murmurs started, low and disbelieving, like the rustle of dry leaves before a storm. Some of the warriors nodded, their grizzled faces unreadable. Others shifted, their wings twitching with restless energy, as if they wanted to take flight and leave this moment behind.

Bellius didn’t move. His broad chest heaved, his dark eyes locked onto hers, burning with something that wasn’t just anger. It was humiliation. And beneath that, something uglier.

Nesta bared her teeth. “Problem?”

His lips peeled back in a snarl. Then, without a word, he turned on his heel and stalked off, his boots kicking up dust as he disappeared into the maze of huts and training rings.

The warriors dispersed, their voices rising in a hum of conversation. Some cast her glances—curious, wary, a few even respectful. But most avoided her gaze entirely, as if looking at her too long might invite the same fate as Bellius.

Nesta flexed her fingers, the ache in her bones a welcome distraction from the storm inside her chest. She had won. And yet—

“You pushed too hard.”

The voice was quiet, but it might as well have been a shout. She didn’t need to turn to know who it was. The weight of his presence was unmistakable, like the press of a storm front before the first crack of thunder.

Cassian stood a few paces behind her, his arms crossed over his chest, his Siphons glinting crimson in the pale mountain light. His wings were half-spread, as if he’d been about to take off but had thought better of it. His face was unreadable, but his jaw was tight, the muscle there ticking.

Nesta whirled on him, her voice a whip-crack. “I didn’t ask for your concern.”

His dark eyes flashed. “No. But you *need* it.”

The words struck her like a physical blow. She recoiled, her pulse roaring in her ears. “I don’t need anything from you.”

“That’s where you’re wrong.” His voice was low, dangerous. “You think this is about winning? It’s not. It’s about surviving. And right now, you’re not just fighting them.” He jerked his chin toward the retreating warriors. “You’re fighting yourself.”

Nesta’s hands clenched into fists, her nails biting into her palms. “Spare me the lecture, Cassian. I didn’t ask you to come here. I didn’t ask for any of this.”

“No,” he said, stepping closer. The scent of leather and wind and something darker, something that was just *him*, wrapped around her. “You didn’t. But here we are.”

She wanted to scream. Wanted to shove him back, to make him feel even a fraction of the fury and helplessness that coiled inside her like a serpent. But she didn’t. Instead, she turned away, her boots crunching over the bloodied stone as she stalked toward her hut.

The ache in her body was nothing compared to the storm in her chest. Every step was agony, every breath a battle. But she didn’t let herself slow. Didn’t let herself look back.

Behind her, Cassian stood motionless, his hands clenched at his sides. Then, with a sharp exhale, he turned and walked in the opposite direction, his wings snapping open as he launched himself into the sky.

---

The hut smelled of damp wool and old smoke. Nesta didn’t bother to light the fire. The cold was a familiar weight against her skin, pressing into her bones as she collapsed onto the pallet. Every muscle screamed, her body a map of bruises and scrapes from the day’s training. She didn’t move to tend to them. What was the point?

The door creaked open, and she didn’t have to look to know it was him. Kars slipped inside, his broad shoulders nearly filling the doorway before he shut it behind him. He carried a bowl of stew in one hand, a jar of salve in the other. The scent of herbs and meat curled through the air, and her stomach twisted, hollow and aching.

He didn’t speak. Just knelt beside her, setting the bowl on the dirt floor before uncorking the jar. The salve was cool as he dipped his fingers into it, the scent of mint and something earthy rising between them.

Nesta watched him through half-lidded eyes. His hands were rough, calloused from years of gripping swords and ropes, but they moved with surprising gentleness as he reached for her arm. She flinched when his fingers brushed the raw skin of her wrist, but he didn’t pull away. Just held her steady, his touch firm but careful.

“Why do you keep helping me?” The words slipped out before she could stop them, quiet and brittle.

Kars didn’t look up. His dark eyes stayed fixed on the wound he was wrapping, the linen strip winding around her forearm. “Because you’re not the only one who’s tired of being told what to do.”

Her breath caught. She studied his face—the sharp line of his jaw, the scar that cut through his eyebrow, the way his mouth pressed into a thin line. He wasn’t looking at her, but she could feel the weight of his words anyway.

She wanted to ask what he meant. Wanted to know who had ordered him around, who had made him feel as trapped as she did. But the words stuck in her throat, tangled in the exhaustion that pressed down on her like a stone.

Kars finished wrapping her wrist and moved to her other arm, his fingers brushing over the bruises there. She didn’t flinch this time. Just let him work, the silence between them heavy but not uncomfortable.

When he was done, he recorked the jar of salve and stood, wiping his hands on his trousers. His gaze lingered on her for a moment, something unreadable in his dark eyes. Then he turned and slipped out of the hut, the door clicking shut behind him.

Nesta lay back on the pallet, the rough wool scratching against her skin. The stew sat untouched beside her, the scent of it making her stomach clench. She stared at the thatched roof, the shadows shifting in the dim light.

*Tired of being told what to do.*

The words echoed in her mind, settling over her like a second skin. She didn’t know what to do with them. Didn’t know what to do with any of it.

But for the first time in months, she felt something.

And it terrified her.

---

The night air was a blade against her skin, sharp and unrelenting. Nesta stepped out of the hut, the wooden door groaning softly behind her. The mountains loomed like silent sentinels, their jagged peaks swallowed by the dark. She didn’t bother with a cloak. The cold was nothing compared to the ice inside her chest.

She walked toward the cliffs, her boots crunching over frost-kissed rocks. The wind howled, tugging at the loose strands of her braided coronet, as if trying to unravel her piece by piece. She welcomed it. Let it tear at her. Let it remind her she was still alive, even if she didn’t want to be.

The edge of the cliff yawned before her, a drop into nothingness. She stopped just shy of it, the wind whipping her gray gown against her legs. The stars above were distant, cold. Indifferent.

A shadow detached itself from the darkness.

Cassian.

His wings were folded tight against his back, the crimson Siphons on his knuckles glinting faintly in the starlight. He didn’t speak. Didn’t move. Just stood beside her, close enough that she could feel the heat of him, the solid presence of him, like a wall she couldn’t scale.

Nesta didn’t look at him. “You don’t have to stay.”

His voice was quiet, rough. “I know.”

The wind screamed between them, filling the silence with its own fury. She clenched her jaw, her fingers curling into fists at her sides. She wanted to scream. Wanted to shove him away. Wanted to make him leave before he saw the cracks in her armor.

But he didn’t move. Didn’t flinch. Just stood there, as immovable as the mountains at her back.

“Why are you here?” The words tore from her, raw and jagged.

Cassian turned his head slightly, his dark eyes catching the light. “Because you’re here.”

Her breath hitched. She forced herself to meet his gaze, to hold it. The intensity in his eyes was a physical thing, a brand against her skin. “You don’t get to do that,” she said, her voice shaking. “You don’t get to act like this—like you care. Not after everything.”

His jaw tightened. “I *do* care, Nesta.”

The words hung between them, heavy and suffocating. She wanted to laugh. Wanted to tell him he was a fool. That caring was a weakness, that love was a lie. But the words died in her throat, choked by the truth she couldn’t bear to admit.

She looked away, her throat burning. “You should go.”

“I’m not leaving.”

“Why?” The question was a whisper, a plea. “Why won’t you just *leave*?”

Cassian turned fully toward her then, his wings shifting slightly, the only sign of his frustration. His gaze burned into her, unrelenting. “You don’t have to keep pushing everyone away.”

Her breath caught. The wind howled, as if trying to drown out the words she couldn’t say. She forced herself to meet his eyes, to let him see the storm inside her. “What if I don’t know how to stop?”

---

The wind howled through the Illyrian peaks, sharp as a blade against the cliffs. Nesta’s breath came in ragged bursts, her fingers curled into fists at her sides. The words she’d hurled at Cassian still hung between them, heavy and unyielding.

*What if I don’t know how to stop?*

The question had slipped out before she could stop it, raw and honest in a way she hadn’t allowed herself to be in months. And now, the silence stretched, thick with the weight of it.

Cassian stepped closer, his boots crunching on the frost-kissed earth. His wings were slightly ruffled from the wind, the crimson Siphons on his knuckles glinting in the dim light. “Then let someone help you.”

The words struck her like a physical blow. She wanted to snarl, to spit at his feet, to tell him she didn’t need anyone—least of all him. But the fury that usually rose so easily now tangled in her throat, choking her.

Her hands trembled.

She turned away before he could see it, before he could see the way her chest tightened, the way her breath hitched. The training grounds stretched before her, empty and desolate, the cliffs looming like silent sentinels. She walked, her steps uneven, her body aching with exhaustion. The weight of the day’s training pressed down on her, but it was nothing compared to the weight of his words.

*Let someone help you.*

She didn’t look back. She couldn’t. If she did, she might see the way he watched her—steady, patient, unyielding. The way he always watched her, even when she tried to push him away.

The door to her hut creaked as she shoved it open, the darkness inside swallowing her whole. She didn’t bother lighting a candle. The shadows suited her.

Her pallet was thin, the blankets rough against her skin as she collapsed onto it. The wind outside howled, rattling the shutters, but the silence in the hut was louder. It pressed in on her, suffocating.

She closed her eyes, but the storm inside her didn’t quiet. It raged, yes—it always raged—but now there was something else beneath it. Something she couldn’t name. Something that made her chest ache in a way that wasn’t just anger.

*Let someone help you.*

The words echoed in her mind, settling over her like a promise. Or a threat.

She didn’t know which.
