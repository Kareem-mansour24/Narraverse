CHAPTER 8
The First Spark

The world was a knife against her skin when Nesta woke.

She lay still for a moment, listening to the silence of the mountains—the wind hissing through the rocks, the distant cry of some nightbird. Her ribs ached, a dull, persistent throb where Bellius had struck her. She had not bothered to heal them. Let them ache. Let them remind her.

She dressed in the dark, fingers moving by memory alone. The gray gown was immaculate, as always, though the hem was damp from yesterday’s rain. She braided her hair into its coronet, the ritual familiar, soothing in its monotony. The hut was small, sparse. No comforts. No distractions. Just the bed, the washbasin, the narrow table where her few belongings sat.

She did not look at the books Elain had given her. Did not touch them.

Outside, the air was sharp with the scent of rain and pine. The training grounds stretched before her, slick with mud, the warriors already gathered in loose clusters. Their wings were folded tight, their breath misting in the cold dawn. She ignored the way their eyes flicked toward her, then away.

Devlon stood at the center of the field, his arms crossed over his chest. His gaze was heavy when it landed on her. “You’re late.”

Nesta said nothing. She had learned that silence was its own weapon here.

“Today’s challenge,” Devlon announced, voice carrying over the murmurs of the warriors, “is weighted weapons. Precision and strength. No magic. No tricks.” His mouth twisted. “Nesta will spar with Kars.”

A ripple of interest passed through the crowd. Kars stepped forward, his dark eyes gleaming. He was broad-shouldered, his arms corded with muscle, his wings a deep, mottled brown. He had not spoken to her since she arrived. Had not so much as looked at her.

Until now.

She met his stare, unflinching. Let him see the hollow place inside her. Let him wonder if it was a weakness or a blade.

Devlon tossed her a weighted staff. She caught it, the wood heavy in her hands, the iron bands biting into her palms. The warriors fell back, forming a loose circle around the training ground. Their murmurs faded into silence.

Kars lifted his own staff, testing its weight. He was not as large as Bellius, but he was faster. She had watched him train. Knew the way he moved—efficient, ruthless.

She rolled her shoulders, ignoring the protest of her ribs. The staff was an extension of her arms, the weight familiar now after weeks of drills. She had not asked for this. Had not wanted it.

But she would not break.

Kars stepped forward, his boots sinking into the mud. The air between them thickened, charged with anticipation.

Nesta tightened her grip on the staff.

And waited.

---

The mud sucked at Nesta’s boots as she shifted her weight, the staff heavy in her hands. Kars didn’t move—just watched her with those dark, assessing eyes. The other warriors had gone silent, their breath misting in the cold mountain air. Even Bellius had stopped smirking, his arms crossed as he leaned against a boulder, waiting.

Then Kars lunged.

His staff whipped toward her ribs, a controlled strike, not meant to wound but to test. Nesta twisted, blocking the blow with a sharp crack of wood against wood. The impact vibrated up her arms, but she held firm, pivoting to counter. Her staff lashed out, aiming for his knees, but Kars danced back, fluid and precise.

Again. And again.

Each strike was measured, deliberate. He wasn’t holding back, but he wasn’t trying to break her either. Just pushing. Probing for weaknesses.

Nesta gritted her teeth. Her muscles burned, her breath coming in sharp bursts, but she didn’t falter. She had spent weeks in this mud, in this cold, learning the weight of the staff, the rhythm of the fight. Kars was faster, stronger—he always would be—but she was faster than she’d been before.

A feint to the left. She saw it coming, sidestepped, and drove her staff toward his gut. Kars grunted as the blow landed, but he didn’t stumble. Instead, he spun, his staff swinging low. Nesta barely managed to leap over it, her boots skidding in the mud as she landed.

The warriors murmured. Bellius’s smirk returned, but the others—Devlon included—watched with something like interest.

Kars came at her again, faster this time. His staff blurred, a relentless storm of strikes. Nesta blocked, countered, but he was everywhere at once. A blow to her shoulder sent her reeling. Another to her thigh made her leg buckle. She barely kept her footing, her staff a whirlwind as she fought to keep him at bay.

Then—pain.

A sharp crack to her ribs. The breath left her lungs in a rush as she staggered back, her boots slipping in the mud. She hit the ground hard, the impact jarring through her bones.

Silence.

Nesta lay there for a heartbeat, the cold seeping through her clothes, the taste of iron in her mouth. Then—movement. A hand extended toward her.

Kars stood over her, his expression unreadable. “Get up.”

She ignored his hand. Pushed herself to her feet, her ribs screaming in protest. The mud clung to her clothes, her skin, but she didn’t care. She tightened her grip on the staff, her breath ragged, and met Kars’s gaze.

The warriors were no longer silent. Their murmurs rose like a tide, some impressed, others disbelieving. Bellius’s smirk had vanished, replaced by a scowl.

Nesta didn’t look at them. Didn’t look at Cassian, who stood apart, his hands clenched into fists at his sides, his wings slightly flared.

She only looked at Kars.

And waited.

---

The whistle pierced the air, sharp and final.

Devlon lowered his hand, his weathered face unreadable. "Enough. Draw."

Nesta's chest heaved, her lungs burning with each ragged breath. Sweat slicked her brow, her braid unraveling at the temples, but her eyes—her eyes were alight. Not with victory. Not with triumph. But with something wild and untamed, something that had been clawing its way to the surface for months.

Kars stepped back, his broad chest rising and falling in steady rhythm. He didn’t smile. Didn’t gloat. Just gave her a single nod, his dark eyes holding hers for a heartbeat too long. "You’re stronger than you look," he said, voice low enough that only she could hear.

Bellius scoffed, the sound dripping with venom. "A draw? She barely touched you." His lip curled as he looked Nesta up and down, as if she were something foul clinging to his boot. "Pathetic."

Kars didn’t turn. Didn’t acknowledge the other warrior at all. "She’s stronger than *you* think."

The words hung in the air, thick with implication. Nesta’s fingers twitched at her sides, her nails biting into her palms. She wanted to snarl, to snap back at Bellius, to prove him wrong in the most vicious way possible. But she didn’t. She just held Kars’ gaze, something unspoken passing between them. Acknowledgment. Respect.

Then Devlon clapped his hands, the sound like a crack of thunder. "Enough posturing. Back to drills."

The warriors dispersed, some grumbling, others casting glances over their shoulders at Nesta. She ignored them all. Ignored the way Bellius muttered something under his breath as he stalked off. Ignored the way Kars lingered for half a second longer than necessary before turning away.

And she *definitely* ignored Cassian.

He stood apart, as he always did, his wings folded tightly against his back. His hands were still clenched into fists, the crimson Siphons on his knuckles glinting in the pale morning light. His jaw was set, his dark eyes burning with something she didn’t want to name.

Pride. Frustration. Anger.

She didn’t care.

Nesta turned on her heel, her boots kicking up dust as she strode toward the cluster of huts at the edge of the training grounds. Her body ached, her muscles trembling with exertion, but she didn’t slow. Didn’t let herself limp. Didn’t let herself show even a flicker of weakness.

Behind her, she heard the soft rustle of wings. Knew without looking that Cassian had taken to the air, that he was following her from above, keeping his distance but never letting her out of his sight.

She gritted her teeth.

The storm may have passed, but the tension between them was far from over.

---

The hut was little more than a box of rough-hewn timber, its walls groaning under the weight of the wind that howled through the Illyrian peaks. Nesta didn’t bother to light the lantern. The dark suited her.

She collapsed onto the pallet, her body screaming in protest. Every muscle burned, every breath a knife between her ribs. The training had been brutal—Cassian had made sure of it. Not out of cruelty, but something worse: persistence. As if he believed, truly believed, that she could become something more than the brittle, broken thing she was.

The door creaked open.

Nesta didn’t turn. She knew the sound of his footsteps, the way the floorboards dipped beneath his weight. The scent of leather and iron and something wild, like the mountains themselves, filled the small space.

“You didn’t eat.” His voice was low, rough with exhaustion of his own.

“I’m not hungry.”

A lie. Her stomach had been growling for hours. But hunger was a distraction, and she had spent the day learning how to endure worse.

The scrape of wood against wood—he set something on the table. The rich, savory scent of stew curled through the air, thick with meat and herbs. Her mouth watered.

“I don’t need your pity,” she said, still not looking at him.

Silence. Then, “It’s not pity.”

She finally turned her head. Cassian stood near the table, his wings folded tight against his back, the crimson Siphons on his knuckles dull in the dim light. His dark hair was disheveled, strands falling into his face, and there was a bruise darkening his jaw—one she had put there.

“Then what is it?” she demanded.

“Respect.”

The word hung between them, heavy and unfamiliar. Nesta let out a sharp, disbelieving laugh. “Respect? For what? For nearly breaking my ribs today? For making me crawl through the mud like an animal?”

Cassian’s jaw tightened. “For not quitting.”

She opened her mouth to argue, but the words died on her tongue. Because he was right. She *hadn’t* quit. No matter how much she had wanted to. No matter how much she had *hated* him for pushing her.

He took a step closer, then another, until he was kneeling beside her pallet. His wings brushed against the low ceiling, stirring the dust. She could see the exhaustion in the lines of his face, the way his shoulders sagged with weariness. But his eyes—his eyes were steady, unyielding.

“You think I don’t know what it’s like?” he said quietly. “To feel like you’re drowning and there’s no one to pull you out? To hate the world and yourself and everyone in it?” He reached out, his fingers hovering just above the dark bruises blooming along her ribs. “I’ve been there, Nesta. And I’m still here.”

She flinched, not from pain, but from the way his words cut through the armor she had spent months forging. “Respect doesn’t change anything,” she said, her voice barely above a whisper.

“No,” he agreed. “But it’s a start.”

She wanted to argue. Wanted to shove him away, to tell him he didn’t know her, didn’t understand. But the fight had drained out of her, leaving only a hollow ache in its wake.

Cassian stood, his wings rustling against the ceiling. “Rest,” he said, his voice rough. “Tomorrow, we start again.”

He didn’t wait for her reply. The door clicked shut behind him, leaving her in the dark.

Nesta lay there, staring at the ceiling. Her fingers traced the bruises along her ribs, each one a testament to the day’s battle. And for the first time, she didn’t feel alone in the silence.

---

The night was a living thing, breathing cold and biting against her skin as Nesta stepped outside the hut. The wind howled through the mountains, carrying the scent of pine and frost, but she barely felt it. The bruises along her ribs ached, a dull throb beneath her fingers, but even that was distant, as if her body belonged to someone else.

She had lain on her pallet for hours, staring at the rough-hewn beams of the ceiling, her mind a storm of half-formed thoughts. Sleep was a stranger now. Rest was a myth. So she rose, pulling on her boots with mechanical precision, and stepped into the darkness.

The training grounds were silent, the usual clamor of drills and grunts swallowed by the night. Only the distant cry of some nocturnal creature broke the stillness. She walked toward the cliffs, her breath misting in the air, the moonlight casting long, jagged shadows across the rocks. The drop beyond the edge was a yawning abyss, but she didn’t flinch. She never did.

Footsteps crunched on the gravel behind her.

Nesta didn’t turn. She knew the sound of his gait, the way his boots settled into the earth. Kars. Of course it was Kars.

He stopped beside her, close enough that she could feel the warmth of him, but not so close that their shoulders brushed. The wind tugged at his dark hair, pulling strands loose from the knot at the nape of his neck. His face was all sharp angles in the moonlight, his jaw shadowed with stubble.

"You fought well today," he said quietly.

Nesta bared her teeth, though she didn’t look at him. "I don’t need your approval."

"I know." His voice was calm, unruffled. "But you have it anyway."

She wanted to snap at him, to tell him to leave her be, to go back to whatever hole he’d crawled out of. But the words died on her tongue. There was no malice in him, no pity. Just quiet certainty.

She exhaled sharply through her nose. "Why are you even here?"

Kars was silent for a long moment. Then, softly, "Because someone has to be."

The wind howled between them, sharp as a blade. Nesta finally turned her head, but he was already walking away, his broad shoulders disappearing into the darkness. The words he’d left behind hung in the air, heavy and unshakable.

*Because someone has to be.*

She stood there long after he was gone, the cold seeping into her bones, the weight of his words pressing down on her like the mountains themselves.
