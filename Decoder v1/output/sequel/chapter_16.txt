CHAPTER 16
The Unspoken Truth

The cold bit into Nesta’s bones before she even opened her eyes. She lay still for a moment, listening to the wind howl through the cracks in the stone walls of the barracks, the faint snores of the other warriors a distant hum beneath it. Then the ache set in—her ribs, her arms, the back of her thighs. She didn’t need to look to know the bruises would be livid, a map of last night’s lesson.

Bellius had been thorough.

She exhaled through her nose, slow and controlled, and pushed herself up. The movement sent a sharp pain lancing through her side, but she ignored it. She had long since learned to ignore pain.

The barracks were dim, the first gray light of dawn barely creeping through the narrow windows. Nesta swung her legs over the edge of the cot, her bare feet touching the cold stone floor. She dressed quickly, the familiar motions of pulling on her leathers, lacing her boots, braiding her hair into its tight coronet—all done without thought, without feeling. The gown she’d worn from Velaris was gone, left behind in some chest or another. She didn’t miss it.

She didn’t miss anything.

The training yard was empty when she stepped outside, the air crisp and thin at this altitude. The mountains loomed around her, jagged and unforgiving, their peaks lost in the low-hanging clouds. She took a deep breath, filling her lungs with the sharp, clean air, and began her warm-up. Stretches, then drills—punches, kicks, blocks. Her body moved on instinct, each motion precise, efficient. The bruises protested, but she pushed through, her jaw clenched tight.

She was halfway through a set of lunges when Devlon’s voice cut through the quiet.

“Archeron.”

She didn’t stop. Didn’t look at him.

“Today’s challenge,” he said, striding toward her, his boots crunching on the frost-covered ground. “Solo survival test. You’ll be given a head start, then the warriors will take to the skies. You’ll have until sundown to reach the eastern ridge. No food. No weapons. No help.”

Nesta straightened, her breath steady despite the exertion. “And if I don’t make it?”

Devlon’s lips curled into something that wasn’t quite a smile. “Then you fail. And we’ll see if you’re still standing tomorrow.”

She said nothing. There was nothing to say.

The warriors began to gather, their wings rustling as they stretched them in the cold morning air. Some watched her with open disdain, others with something closer to curiosity. None of them spoke to her. None of them ever did.

Devlon jerked his chin toward the starting line, a crude mark scraped into the dirt. “Get moving.”

Nesta walked to the line, her boots sinking slightly into the damp earth. The wind tugged at her braid, pulling strands loose to whip against her face. She didn’t bother tucking them back.

She could feel their eyes on her—the warriors, Devlon, the mountains themselves, watching, waiting. She had no illusions about what this was. It wasn’t a test. It was a hunt.

She stepped over the line.

The moment her foot touched the other side, the warriors took to the skies. Their wings beat against the air, a thunderous rhythm, and then they were rising, their massive forms blotting out the pale morning light. She didn’t look up. Didn’t give them the satisfaction.

The wind howled around her as she started forward, her boots finding purchase on the rocky path. The mountains stretched before her, a maze of cliffs and ravines, of hidden drops and sudden, deadly winds. She had no map. No supplies. No idea where the eastern ridge even was.

But she had her instincts.

And for the first time in months, that was enough.

---

The wind howled through the jagged teeth of the Illyrian Mountains, biting at Nesta’s cheeks, her ears. She didn’t flinch. The cold was nothing—less than nothing. It was a distraction, and she had long since learned to ignore those.

Her boots crunched over frost-crusted rock, the sound swallowed by the vast, empty sky. The warriors had set their traps well. Pitfalls disguised as shadows, tripwires strung between boulders, their edges glinting in the weak winter light. But she saw them. Not with her eyes—no, her vision had always been sharp, but this was something else. Something deeper, quieter. A whisper in her bones, a pull in her gut. *There. And there.*

She sidestepped a patch of loose scree, her fingers brushing the rough stone of the cliff face as she steadied herself. The warriors’ taunts still echoed in the distance, their voices rough with laughter, with challenge. *Human. Weak. Useless.* She bared her teeth, though no one was there to see it. Let them shout. Let them think she couldn’t hear them over the wind.

A shadow blotted out the sun.

Nesta didn’t look up. She *felt* him—Bellius, his wings casting a sudden, suffocating darkness over the path. The downdraft hit her like a fist, sending pebbles skittering, dust stinging her eyes. She dropped into a crouch, her braid whipping over her shoulder as she rolled to the side. The warrior’s talons scraped against stone where she’d just been standing, the sound like nails on glass.

She was on her feet before he could turn, already sprinting. The path narrowed ahead, a razor’s edge between two cliffs. She didn’t hesitate. The warriors had expected her to falter, to freeze, to beg. They didn’t know her anymore.

The wind screamed in her ears, but she didn’t slow. Behind her, Bellius roared, the sound raw with frustration. She didn’t look back. She didn’t need to. She could *taste* his fury, metallic and sharp on her tongue.

The path widened again, and she risked a glance over her shoulder. The warriors were small now, dark shapes against the pale sky, their voices fading into the howl of the wind. They weren’t following. Not yet.

Nesta exhaled, her breath curling in the air. Her chest burned, her thighs ached, but she didn’t stop. She wouldn’t give them the satisfaction.

The pass ahead was narrow, a jagged slit between two towering cliffs. The wind funneled through it, a relentless, keening wail. She slowed, her fingers flexing at her sides. The air here was different. Thicker. Charged.

She stepped forward—and froze.

The warriors weren’t watching her.

But *someone* was.

---

The wind howled through the jagged peaks, biting at Nesta’s cheeks as she stood alone on the cliff’s edge. The training camp sprawled below, a sea of tents and flickering torchlight, the distant clatter of weapons and drunken laughter drifting up to her. She hadn’t meant to wander this far, but the weight of their stares—of *his* stares—had driven her into the rocks, where the air was thinner and the silence louder.

She exhaled sharply, her breath curling in the cold. The gray gown she wore, immaculate despite the grime of the camp, clung to her frame. She had lost weight in the weeks since arriving here. Not that it mattered. Not that anything did.

A scuff of boot against stone.

Nesta stiffened, her fingers curling into fists at her sides. She didn’t turn. Didn’t need to. She knew that sound—the quiet, deliberate tread of someone who didn’t want to be heard.

“You’re going to freeze out here.”

The voice was low, rough. Kars. She didn’t look at him as he stepped from the shadows, his broad frame blocking the worst of the wind. He held out a waterskin, then a small bundle wrapped in cloth.

Nesta stared at them. “I’m not hungry.”

“Doesn’t matter.” His dark eyes met hers, unreadable. “Take them.”

She hesitated, then snatched the offerings from his hands. The waterskin was cool, the weight of it familiar. The bundle held hard bread and dried meat—nothing special, but enough. She unwrapped it with stiff fingers.

“Why?” The word slipped out before she could stop it.

Kars didn’t answer at first. The wind tugged at his dark hair, at the edges of his worn leathers. He was younger than most of the warriors here, but the scars on his knuckles and the set of his jaw made him seem older. “Because you’re not the only one who’s tired of being told what to do.”

Nesta’s throat tightened. She wanted to press him—wanted to demand what he meant, if he’d heard the whispers about her, if he *agreed* with them. But the words stuck in her chest, heavy and useless.

Kars jerked his chin toward the camp. “They’re coming.”

She didn’t need to look to know he was right. The laughter had shifted, grown louder. Closer. The warriors, drawn by the defiance in her silence, in her refusal to play their games.

Kars stepped back into the shadows, melting into the rocks as if he’d never been there at all. Nesta didn’t call after him. Didn’t ask him to stay.

The wind carried the sound of their approach, the crunch of boots on gravel, the low murmur of voices. She stood there, the waterskin cold in her grip, the food untouched in her other hand.

And for the first time in months, she didn’t feel hollow.

She felt the weight of their stares like a brand. And she didn’t look away.

---

The wind howled across the cliffs, biting at Nesta’s face, but she didn’t flinch. The cold was nothing compared to the fire in her veins, the heat of their gazes burning into her back. She quickened her pace, boots kicking up dust as she moved toward the edge of the drop.

The warriors’ shadows blotted out the sun, their wings casting jagged shapes over the rocky ground. Their voices were a snarling chorus, sharp with mockery.

*“Look at her run.”*
*“Coward.”*
*“Afraid of a little fall, girl?”*

Nesta didn’t slow. The cliff’s edge loomed ahead, a sheer drop into nothingness. She could hear Bellius’s wings slicing through the air as he dove low, his breath hot against her neck.

*“You’re not getting away that easily,”* he growled.

She turned just enough to meet his gaze, her lips curling back from her teeth. *“Is this the best you’ve got?”*

Bellius’s face twisted, his wings snapping wide as he pulled up short. The other warriors laughed, but the sound was brittle, uncertain. Nesta didn’t wait for his reply. She stepped off the edge.

The wind rushed up to meet her, but she didn’t fall.

Her foot found the hidden ledge below, a narrow shelf of rock jutting from the cliff face. She crouched, fingers splayed against the cold stone, her breath steady. Above her, the warriors’ voices rose in confusion, then anger.

*“Where is she?”*
*“She couldn’t have just—”*
*“Find her!”*

But their shouts faded as they wheeled away, searching the skies, the ground below. Nesta stayed still, listening as their voices grew distant, swallowed by the wind.

She had won.

For now.

The question was—what would she do with this victory?

---

The wind howled through the jagged peaks of the Illyrian Mountains, biting at Nesta’s exposed skin like a thousand tiny knives. She didn’t flinch. Didn’t acknowledge the sting. Her body moved on instinct, each step a battle against the trembling in her legs, the exhaustion that clawed at her bones. The training camp sprawled before her, a cluster of rough-hewn tents and fire pits, the scent of smoke and sweat thick in the air.

The warriors fell silent as she passed.

She could feel their eyes on her—some wary, some admiring, some outright hostile. A few nodded in grudging respect. Others sneered, their lips curling as if her very presence offended them. Nesta kept her chin high, her spine straight. She wouldn’t give them the satisfaction of seeing her falter.

Then she saw him.

Cassian stood near the edge of the camp, his wings half-furled, his crimson Siphons glowing faintly in the gathering dusk. His dark hair was windswept, his jaw set in that familiar stubborn line. He watched her approach, his expression unreadable.

Nesta’s pulse spiked, but she didn’t slow. Didn’t look away.

“You survived,” he said, his voice low, rough.

She stopped a few paces from him, her breath coming in shallow gasps. “I did.”

Cassian’s gaze raked over her, lingering on the bruises blooming along her arms, the dirt smeared across her cheek. His hands flexed at his sides, as if he were fighting the urge to reach for her. “But at what cost?”

The words struck like a blade between her ribs. Nesta’s chest tightened, her throat burning. She wanted to snap at him, to tell him it was none of his business. But the words wouldn’t come. Instead, she stood there, silent, her fingers curling into fists at her sides.

Cassian stepped closer, the heat of his body cutting through the cold mountain air. “You think this is what you need?” he demanded, his voice raw. “To punish yourself until there’s nothing left?”

Nesta’s breath hitched. She could feel the weight of his gaze, the weight of his words, pressing down on her. She wanted to scream. Wanted to shove him away. But she didn’t. She just stood there, trembling, her heart pounding so hard she was sure he could hear it.

Then his hand brushed against hers.

She flinched, but she didn’t pull away.

The air between them crackled, charged with something neither of them dared to name. Cassian’s fingers hovered near hers, close enough that she could feel the warmth of his skin, the rough calluses from years of wielding a sword.

“Nesta,” he whispered, his voice barely audible over the wind. “I—”

---

The wind howled through the Illyrian peaks, carrying with it the scent of pine and frost. Nesta stood beside Cassian on the training grounds, the firelight casting long, dancing shadows across the rocky earth. Her fingers twitched at her sides, still humming with the memory of his touch—warm, rough, real. She had almost let herself lean into it. Almost.

Then the shout came.

A sharp, jagged sound that cut through the night like a blade. Nesta turned, her braided coronet glinting in the firelight as she followed the direction of the noise. A cluster of warriors had gathered around one of the larger fires, their voices rising in a crescendo of anger. Bellius stood at the center, his broad frame rigid, his face twisted in a snarl.

*"She cheated."* The words slithered through the air, venomous and deliberate. *"She didn’t follow the rules."*

The warriors murmured, their gazes flickering toward Nesta like embers in the dark. She felt the weight of their stares, heavy and accusing. Her jaw tightened.

Cassian’s voice was a whip-crack. *"She survived. That’s all that matters."*

Bellius didn’t flinch. He stepped forward, his boots crunching over the frozen ground, his wings half-spread in a display of dominance. *"This isn’t over."*

Nesta didn’t wait for him to finish. She strode forward, her gray gown—immaculate despite the dirt and grime of the camp—whispering against her legs. The warriors parted for her, their expressions a mix of hostility and reluctant curiosity. Bellius’s lips curled back, baring his teeth.

*"You think because you’re some high-born lady, you can waltz in here and break our laws?"* His voice was a growl, low and dangerous. *"You think you’re above us?"*

Nesta stopped just out of reach, her own teeth bared in a mirror of his snarl. *"I think I did what I had to do to stay alive. If that offends your delicate Illyrian sensibilities, then perhaps you should have made the rules clearer before you tried to kill me."*

A few of the warriors shifted, their wings rustling. Bellius’s eyes darkened. *"You’re not one of us. You never will be."*

*"Good,"* Nesta snapped. *"Because I have no interest in being part of a brotherhood that would rather see a woman dead than adapt."*

The words hung between them, sharp and unyielding. Bellius’s hands clenched into fists, his Siphons flaring crimson in the firelight. For a heartbeat, Nesta thought he might strike her. She braced herself, her fingers curling into invisible claws, her body coiled and ready.

But then Cassian was there, his presence a solid wall at her back. *"Enough."* His voice was quiet, but it carried the weight of centuries of command. *"Unless you’d like to challenge me for leadership, Bellius, I suggest you walk away."*

Bellius’s gaze flicked to Cassian, then back to Nesta. His lips peeled back in a final, wordless snarl before he turned on his heel and stormed into the night, his wings snapping out as he launched himself into the sky. The other warriors dispersed slowly, their murmurs fading into the wind.

Silence settled over the camp, thick and suffocating. Nesta didn’t move. Didn’t breathe.

Cassian’s voice was low, barely audible. *"What are you willing to fight for?"*

She turned to face him, her chest tight, her heart pounding. His eyes burned in the firelight, not with anger, but with something far more dangerous—hope. A question. A challenge.

Nesta lifted her chin. She didn’t answer. But for the first time in months, she let herself wonder.

---

The hut was a tomb of silence, the kind that settled into her bones like frost. Nesta didn’t bother lighting the fire. The cold was a familiar companion now, one that didn’t demand anything of her except endurance.

She collapsed onto the narrow bed, her body screaming in protest. Every muscle ached, every breath a labor. The training yard had been a battlefield today, and she had lost. Again. Not that it mattered. Not that anything did.

The door creaked open.

She didn’t need to look to know who it was. The scent of wind and steel preceded him, wrapping around her like a chain.

“You can’t keep doing this.”

Cassian’s voice was low, rough. She kept her eyes on the ceiling, counting the cracks in the wood. One. Two. Three.

“I don’t need your permission.”

A lie. She knew it the moment the words left her mouth. But lies were easier than the truth.

The bed dipped as he sat beside her, close enough that she could feel the heat of him through the thin blanket. “No,” he said. “But you *need* someone.”

Her breath hitched. The words struck something deep inside her, something she had buried beneath layers of ice. She turned her head, meeting his gaze.

His dark eyes were stormy, his jaw tight. The crimson Siphons on his knuckles glinted in the dim light, a stark contrast to the worn leather of his gloves. He looked like a warrior who had seen too many battles, fought too many wars.

And yet, here he was. Still fighting for her.

“Why?” she demanded, her voice sharper than she intended. “Why do you even care?”

His throat worked, as if the words were caught there. “Because I know what it’s like to feel like you’re drowning. And I know what it’s like to have someone pull you back.”

Her chest tightened. She wanted to lash out, to tell him she didn’t need saving. But the truth was a fire in her veins, burning hotter with every passing second.

Cassian reached out, his calloused fingers brushing against her cheek. She didn’t pull away.

The air between them was charged, thick with something neither of them dared to name. His thumb traced the line of her jaw, slow, deliberate. “Let me in.”

Her heart pounded, a wild, frantic thing. She wanted to say yes. Wanted to lean into his touch, to let him carry the weight she had been shouldering for months.

But the words wouldn’t come.

Instead, she turned away, her voice barely a whisper. “I can’t.”
