CHAPTER 7
The Storm Before the Calm

The storm came for the mountains before dawn.

Nesta woke to the howl of wind against the wooden slats of her hut, the rain a relentless drumming on the thatched roof. She lay still for a moment, listening to the way the gale rattled the shutters, the way it whispered through the cracks in the walls like a living thing. The air was thick with the scent of wet earth and pine, the cold seeping through the thin blanket draped over her.

She had not slept well. The dreams had come again—fragments of fire and screaming, of hands reaching for her in the dark. She had woken with her heart pounding, her skin slick with sweat, and the storm had already been waiting.

Nesta sat up, pushing the blanket aside. The floor was cold beneath her bare feet, the rough wood groaning as she stood. She dressed quickly, pulling on the gray training gown she had come to despise, the fabric stiff with salt and sweat from yesterday’s sparring. Her fingers fumbled with the laces, her body still heavy with exhaustion. The bruises from Bellius’s blows throbbed along her ribs, a dull ache that flared with every movement.

Outside, the world was a blur of gray and white. The rain fell in sheets, the wind whipping it sideways so that it stung her face like needles when she stepped onto the muddy path. The training grounds were deserted, the usual shouts and clangs of sparring blades replaced by the eerie quiet of the storm. The warriors were huddled in their barracks, no doubt laughing at the thought of her braving this weather alone.

She found Devlon near the armory, his broad frame hunched against the rain as he surveyed the empty field. He turned when he heard her approach, his scarred face unreadable.

“Training’s canceled,” he said, raising his voice over the wind. “Storm’s too bad.”

Nesta said nothing. She knew what this was. Another test. Another way to break her.

Devlon’s mouth twisted. “You can go back to your hut if you want.”

She met his gaze, unflinching. “No.”

He studied her for a long moment, then gave a sharp nod. “Suit yourself.”

Nesta turned away before he could see the way her hands trembled. She didn’t need his permission. She didn’t need anyone’s.

The path along the cliffside was slick with mud, the rocks treacherous beneath her boots. The wind howled in her ears, the rain lashing at her skin like a thousand tiny whips. She kept her head down, her breath coming in sharp, controlled bursts as she pushed forward. The cold seeped into her bones, her muscles burning with every step. The bruises along her ribs screamed in protest, but she ignored them.

She would not break.

She would not give them the satisfaction.

The storm raged around her, the world reduced to the sound of her own breathing, the slap of her boots against the wet earth. She focused on the rhythm of her steps, on the way her lungs burned, on the way the rain mixed with the sweat on her skin. She had spent months feeling nothing at all. Now, at least, she could feel this.

The path curved sharply, the drop to the valley below a sheer, deadly plunge. Nesta’s foot slipped on a patch of ice, her arms windmilling as she fought for balance. For a heartbeat, she thought she would fall, that the wind would take her over the edge. But she caught herself, her fingers digging into the wet rock as she steadied her footing.

She exhaled sharply, her heart pounding. Then she kept running.

The storm was a living thing, a beast that snarled and snapped at her heels. She bared her teeth at it, her fingers curling into fists. She would not yield.

She rounded a bend in the path, her breath ragged, her body trembling with exhaustion. And then she saw him.

Bellius stood ahead of her, his massive frame blocking the way, his arms crossed over his chest. His smirk was visible even through the rain, his dark eyes gleaming with amusement.

“Lost, little mouse?” he called over the wind.

---

The rain had turned the training grounds into a slick, sucking mire, each step a battle against the mud that clung to her boots like a living thing. Nesta kept her chin high, her spine straight, though her fingers itched to wipe the cold droplets from her face. She wouldn’t give Bellius the satisfaction.

He stood in her path, arms crossed over his broad chest, his smirk twisting into something uglier. The rain plastered his dark hair to his forehead, but his eyes—oh, his eyes burned with something far worse than the storm.

“Lost, little mouse?” he called again, his voice carrying over the wind as if the elements themselves bent to his amusement.

Nesta didn’t slow. “Move.”

His laughter was a sharp, cutting thing. “Or what? You’ll ask nicely?” He stepped closer, his boots sinking into the mud with a wet squelch. “You think you belong here, Archeron? You think a pretty face and a sharp tongue will keep you alive when the real training begins?”

She stopped just short of him, close enough to see the flecks of gold in his irises, close enough to smell the sour tang of ale on his breath. “I don’t think,” she said, her voice low, steady. “I *know*.”

His smirk faltered for half a breath before he recovered, leaning in until his nose nearly brushed hers. “You know nothing. You’re soft. Weak. A human playing at being a warrior.” His gaze flicked over her, lingering on the mud splattered across the hem of her once-immaculate gown. “Look at you. You can’t even walk through the rain without getting dirty.”

Nesta bared her teeth. “And you can’t stand the thought of a woman who won’t cower.”

Something dark flashed in his eyes. His hand shot out, shoving her hard in the chest.

She didn’t see it coming.

The world tilted, her boots skidding in the mud as she crashed onto her back, the impact knocking the breath from her lungs. Cold, wet earth seeped through the fabric of her gown, soaking into her skin. For a heartbeat, she lay there, staring up at the storm-choked sky, the rain needling her face.

Then the rage came.

It surged through her like a wildfire, burning away the hollow numbness she’d carried for months. Her fingers curled into the mud, nails digging in as she pushed herself up, slow, deliberate. The warriors had gathered at the edges of the training grounds, their expressions a mix of amusement and something else—something like curiosity.

Bellius loomed over her, his grin returning. “Get up, little mouse. Let’s see if you can—”

She was on her feet before he finished, her body moving on instinct, on the memory of every insult, every dismissal, every time someone had looked at her and seen only weakness. Her fist flew before she could think, connecting with his ribs in a sharp, satisfying crunch.

Bellius staggered back, his breath leaving him in a pained grunt. The warriors’ murmurs died instantly, the silence so thick she could hear the rain hissing against the mud.

For a heartbeat, no one moved.

Then Bellius’s face twisted, his hand flying to his side as he sucked in a ragged breath. His eyes locked onto hers, wide with shock—and something darker. Something hungry.

Nesta didn’t wait for him to recover.

She lunged.

---

The rain lashed down in sheets, turning the training ground to mud beneath Nesta’s boots. Her lungs burned, her muscles screamed, but she didn’t stop. Not when Bellius’s sneer twisted into something uglier. Not when his fist came at her again.

She dodged, barely, the wind of his swing ruffling the damp strands of hair that had escaped her braid. The warriors around them had gone silent, their usual jeers replaced by something sharper—anticipation. Or maybe it was pity.

Nesta bared her teeth. She didn’t want their pity.

Bellius’s next strike was slower, sloppier. She saw the opening, the way his weight shifted just a fraction too far to the left. She feinted right, then drove her knee into his gut.

The impact sent him stumbling back, his breath wheezing out of him in a wet gasp. The warriors murmured, a few of them chuckling. Bellius’s face flushed dark with rage.

“Enough.”

The word cut through the rain like a blade.

Cassian landed beside them, his wings flaring wide, the crimson Siphons on his knuckles glowing faintly in the storm-light. The warriors scattered like leaves in a gale, clearing a path for him. Bellius straightened, his chest heaving, but his eyes flicked to Cassian with something like fear.

Nesta didn’t look at him. She didn’t need to. She could feel the weight of Cassian’s gaze on her, heavy and assessing.

“Stand down, Bellius,” Cassian said, his voice low, controlled. But there was no mistaking the command in it.

Bellius’s jaw worked, his fingers flexing at his sides. For a heartbeat, Nesta thought he might argue. Then his shoulders slumped, just slightly, and he spat at her feet. The glob of saliva landed in the mud between them, and Nesta’s stomach twisted.

She didn’t flinch. Didn’t react. But her hands curled into fists at her sides.

Bellius stalked off, the warriors parting before him like a sea. None of them met her eyes as they turned away, retreating to the edges of the training ground.

The rain drummed against the earth, filling the silence between her and Cassian. She could feel his gaze on her still, could feel the weight of it like a physical touch.

“You’re pushing too hard,” he said at last.

Nesta turned to him then, her chin lifting. “I don’t need your concern.”

Something flickered in his eyes—irritation, maybe, or frustration. His jaw tightened, the muscle there jumping. “You’re going to get yourself killed.”

“Then let me.”

The words hung between them, sharp and final. Cassian’s wings twitched, as if he wanted to reach for her, but he didn’t. Instead, his voice dropped, rough with something she didn’t want to name. “Go back to your hut. Rest.”

Nesta laughed, a short, bitter sound. “Or what? You’ll make me?”

His eyes darkened, his Siphons flaring brighter. “Don’t test me, Nesta.”

She held his gaze, unflinching. “I don’t need your permission.”

For a moment, she thought he might argue. Thought he might grab her, haul her over his shoulder, and carry her back to the hut himself. But then his wings flexed, the feathers ruffling in the wind, and he stepped back. “Fine,” he said, his voice clipped. “But don’t come crying to me when you’re too exhausted to lift a sword tomorrow.”

Nesta didn’t answer. She didn’t trust herself to speak.

Cassian didn’t wait for a response. He turned, his wings snapping out, and launched himself into the storm. The wind from his ascent whipped at her face, cold and damp, but she didn’t look away. She watched as he climbed higher, his silhouette growing smaller against the gray sky, until he was nothing more than a shadow cutting through the rain.

The training ground was empty now, the warriors dispersed, the mud churned beneath her boots. Nesta stood alone, the rain soaking through her clothes, her skin, her bones.

But she wasn’t cold.

The fire inside her burned too hot for that.

---

The rain had followed her from the training grounds, a relentless, icy companion. It slithered down her neck, soaked through the wool of her tunic, turned her braids into heavy ropes that clung to her shoulders. Nesta didn’t bother brushing the water from her face as she ducked inside the hut, the door groaning shut behind her.

The fire in the hearth had long since died, leaving only embers that glowed weakly in the dim light. She stood there, dripping onto the rough wooden floor, her breath coming in shallow gasps. The storm outside howled, but it was nothing compared to the silence within.

She peeled off her wet clothes with numb fingers, the fabric clinging stubbornly to her skin. The cold had seeped into her bones, but it wasn’t the kind of cold that could be chased away by a fire. It was deeper than that. A hollow ache that had taken root months ago and refused to leave.

A thin blanket lay draped over the back of a chair. She wrapped it around herself, the wool scratchy against her bare skin, and sank onto the edge of the bed. The hut was small, barely more than a box with a bed, a table, and a single chair. It smelled of damp wood and old smoke. Home, for now.

Her fingers curled into the blanket, knuckles white. She hadn’t asked for this. Hadn’t asked to be sent away, to be forced into this place where every breath was a battle. Where every glance from the warriors was a blade, every word a taunt. She was Nesta Archeron. She had survived the Court of Nightmares, the King of Hybern, the weight of a power she didn’t understand. And yet here, in this gods-forsaken mountain, she felt more powerless than ever.

The door creaked.

Nesta stiffened, her head snapping toward the sound. Kars stood in the doorway, his broad frame silhouetted against the storm outside. He didn’t speak, didn’t meet her eyes. In his hands was a bowl, steam curling from its surface, the scent of herbs and meat filling the small space.

He set it on the table without a word, his movements deliberate, careful. As if he were handling something fragile. Then he turned, his boots heavy on the floor as he made for the door.

“Why?”

The word slipped out before she could stop it, quiet, rough. Kars paused, his back still to her. The rain drummed against the roof, a steady rhythm that filled the silence between them.

For a long moment, she thought he wouldn’t answer. Then, without turning, he said, “Because you’re not what they think you are.”

The door clicked shut behind him.

Nesta stared at the bowl. The steam had begun to fade, but the warmth of it still radiated into the cold air. Her throat tightened.

She stood, the blanket slipping from her shoulders as she crossed the room. The stew was thick, chunks of meat and vegetables swimming in a rich broth. She lifted the spoon, the metal cool against her lips. The first taste was a shock—warm, savory, the flavors bursting across her tongue.

It was good. Too good.

She swallowed, the warmth spreading through her chest, down into her stomach. But the gesture felt heavier than the storm outside, heavier than the weight of the mountains pressing down on her. Because kindness, here, was a foreign thing. And she didn’t know what to do with it.

---

The storm howled outside, a living thing clawing at the wooden walls of the hut. Rain lashed against the roof, the wind shrieking like a banshee through the cracks in the door. Nesta lay on her pallet, her body aching in ways she hadn’t thought possible. Every muscle screamed, every breath a reminder of the blows she’d taken—of the way Bellius had smiled as he’d driven his fist into her ribs.

She should have been asleep. Exhaustion dragged at her bones, pulled at her eyelids. But her mind wouldn’t quiet. It replayed the day’s failures over and over, each one a fresh wound. The way she’d stumbled. The way she’d fallen. The way Cassian had watched it all with those dark, unreadable eyes.

The door creaked open.

Nesta didn’t need to look to know who it was. She could smell the rain on him, the leather of his gear, the faint metallic tang of his Siphons. The air shifted, charged with his presence, and she sat up slowly, her body protesting the movement.

Cassian stepped inside, his wings dripping water onto the packed earth floor. He didn’t speak, didn’t even look at her at first. He just stood there, his broad shoulders blocking out the faint light from the storm-lit sky outside, his crimson Siphons glowing faintly in the dim.

“What do you want?” Her voice was sharp, edged with something brittle. She didn’t bother to soften it.

Cassian turned then, his dark eyes meeting hers. For a heartbeat, neither of them moved. Then, without a word, he knelt beside her pallet.

Nesta stiffened as his hands came toward her, her instincts flaring. But he didn’t touch her face, didn’t try to soothe her with empty words. His fingers brushed the hem of her tunic, lifting it just enough to expose the bruises blooming along her ribs.

“You’re bruised.” His voice was quiet, rough.

She jerked away, yanking the fabric from his grasp. “I can take care of myself.”

His jaw tightened, but he didn’t pull back. “I know.”

The words hung between them, heavy with something she didn’t want to name. She wanted to snap at him, to tell him to leave, to stop pretending he cared. But the way he was looking at her—like she was something fragile, something breakable—made her throat tighten.

Cassian exhaled slowly, his breath warm against her skin. “But you don’t have to.”

Nesta’s pulse jumped. She opened her mouth to argue, to tell him she didn’t need his pity, his concern. But the words died on her tongue as his hand settled back against her side, his thumb brushing the edge of a bruise with a gentleness that made her chest ache.

For a moment, the storm outside faded. There was only the two of them, the heat of his touch, the way his breath hitched as if he, too, felt the weight of this—whatever this was.

Then Cassian stood abruptly, his wings flaring slightly as if to shake off the rain. His voice was gruff, almost angry. “Rest. Tomorrow, we start again.”

He didn’t wait for her reply. He didn’t even look back as he strode to the door, his boots thudding against the floor. The door slammed shut behind him, the sound final, echoing in the silence he left behind.
