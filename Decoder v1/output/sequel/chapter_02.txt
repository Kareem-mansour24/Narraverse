CHAPTER 2
The First Cut

The cold was a living thing inside the hut, slithering through the cracks in the stone walls, coiling around Nesta’s bones. She woke before dawn, as she always did now, her body a map of aches and bruises from the day before. The humiliation of it burned worse than any physical pain—Cassian’s laughter, the way the other warriors had watched her stumble, the way her own limbs had betrayed her.

She refused to acknowledge the pain. Instead, she focused on the cold. The way it bit into her skin, the way her breath fogged in the frigid air. It was better than feeling anything else.

A knock at the door shattered the silence.

Nesta didn’t move. Didn’t speak.

The door swung open anyway, and Cassian filled the frame, his wings brushing the sides of the doorway. His dark hair was windswept, his crimson Siphons glinting in the dim light. He didn’t wait for an invitation before stepping inside, his boots thudding against the packed earth floor.

“Get up,” he said, his voice rough.

She didn’t look at him. Didn’t acknowledge the way his presence made the air in the hut feel even thinner.

He tossed something at her feet—a bundle of leather. “Put these on.”

Nesta stared at the training leathers, her fingers curling into the thin blanket covering her. She didn’t move.

Cassian exhaled sharply through his nose. “Five minutes, Nesta.”

She didn’t answer. Didn’t so much as blink.

He turned on his heel and left, the door slamming shut behind him.

The silence that followed was heavier than before. Nesta sat up slowly, her muscles protesting every movement. The leathers were stiff and unfamiliar, the laces rough against her fingers as she pulled them on. Her hands trembled, but she didn’t let herself think about why.

She was dressed in minutes, her breath coming in shallow gasps as she forced herself to stand. The leathers were too tight, too constricting, but she didn’t care. She wouldn’t give him the satisfaction of complaining.

Cassian was waiting outside, his arms crossed over his chest. He didn’t look at her as she stepped out of the hut, his gaze fixed on the horizon where the first hints of dawn were beginning to lighten the sky.

“You’re late,” he muttered.

She said nothing.

He turned to face her, his expression unreadable. For once, there was no smirk, no teasing glint in his eyes. Just a quiet intensity that made her stomach twist.

“Let’s go,” he said, his voice low.

She followed him in silence, her boots crunching over the frost-covered ground. The wind howled through the mountains, biting at her exposed skin, but she didn’t flinch. Didn’t react at all.

Cassian stopped abruptly, turning to face her. His jaw was tight, his eyes searching hers. “You’re not going to say anything?”

She met his gaze, her own eyes empty. Hollow.

His throat worked, but he didn’t push. Instead, he turned away, muttering, “We leave in five.”

---

The morning air in the Illyrian Mountains was thin and sharp, biting at Nesta’s lungs as she followed Cassian toward the training grounds. The stone beneath her boots was slick with frost, the jagged peaks around them wreathed in mist. She had slept little, her mind a storm of nothingness, and the cold had seeped into her bones, numbing her fingers despite the gloves she wore.

The training grounds sprawled before them, a vast expanse of packed earth and scattered boulders, ringed by warriors already locked in sparring matches. The moment she stepped onto the field, the clatter of wooden swords and the grunts of exertion died. Heads turned. Eyes narrowed.

She didn’t look at them. Didn’t give them the satisfaction of seeing her flinch.

Devlon stood at the center of the ring, his arms crossed over his broad chest, a smirk already curling his lips. “Back so soon, Archeron?” His voice carried, deliberate. “Thought you’d have run home to your pretty dresses by now.”

Nesta said nothing. She didn’t need to. The silence was answer enough.

Cassian stepped forward, his wings half-furled, the crimson Siphons on his knuckles glinting in the pale light. “She’s here to train,” he said, his voice low but carrying the weight of command. “Not to listen to your mouth.”

Devlon’s smirk didn’t waver, but his eyes flickered with irritation. “Fine. Bellius!” he barked, jerking his chin toward a hulking warrior with a scar running down his cheek. “Take her on.”

Nesta’s fingers twitched. She knew Bellius. Knew the way he fought—brute strength over finesse, every swing designed to humiliate.

Cassian’s jaw tightened. “No.”

Devlon’s brows shot up. “No?”

“She’s not ready for him.” Cassian’s gaze swept the crowd, landing on a younger warrior lingering at the edge of the ring. “Kars.”

The boy—no, the man, though he barely looked older than a boy—startled, his dark eyes widening. “Me?”

Devlon’s laugh was a bark. “Kars? You want her to spar with *Kars*?”

Cassian ignored him. “Kars,” he repeated, softer this time. “You’ll go easy on her.”

Kars swallowed hard, his Adam’s apple bobbing. He wasn’t much taller than Nesta, his frame lean rather than muscled, his wings still the dull gray of youth. He looked like he’d rather be anywhere else.

Devlon’s smirk returned. “Afraid she’ll break if she faces someone with a spine, Cassian?”

Nesta didn’t wait for Cassian to respond. She stepped into the ring, her boots kicking up dust. Kars hesitated, then followed, his movements stiff.

The warriors around them fell silent again, watching.

Kars raised his wooden sword, his grip uncertain. Nesta didn’t bother with a weapon. She didn’t need one.

Devlon’s voice cut through the quiet. “Begin.”

Kars lunged.

It was slow. Too slow.

Nesta sidestepped, her body moving before her mind could catch up. Her fist connected with his ribs, a sharp, clean hit. The air left his lungs in a whoosh, and he crumpled, his sword clattering to the ground.

The crowd erupted—not in laughter, but in stunned silence.

Kars gasped, his hands clutching his side, his face twisted in shock. Nesta stood over him, her chest heaving, her knuckles stinging. She hadn’t meant to hit him that hard. But the moment his sword had swung toward her, something inside her had *snapped*.

Devlon’s smirk was gone. His face darkened, his eyes narrowing as he looked from Kars to Nesta.

She didn’t smile. Didn’t gloat.

She just stood there, waiting.

---

The training grounds were a cacophony of grunts and steel, the scent of sweat and iron thick in the air. Nesta wiped her brow with the back of her hand, her fingers still trembling from the force of the last blow she’d blocked. The wooden practice sword lay discarded at her feet, its weight suddenly too much to bear.

Cassian stepped into her path, his wings half-spread in that infuriating way that made him look like he owned the sky—and her. His dark eyes burned with something she refused to name. “You’re improving,” he said, voice low. “But you’re still dropping your guard when you strike.”

Nesta bared her teeth. “I don’t need your critiques.”

“You do if you don’t want to get your throat slit.” His jaw tightened. “You think Bellius won’t use that opening?”

She scoffed. “Afraid I’ll embarrass you?”

His Siphons flared crimson, the only warning before he leaned in, his breath hot against her ear. “I’m afraid you’ll get yourself killed.”

A shiver ran down her spine, but she shoved it away. “I don’t need your protection.”

“No?” His laugh was bitter. “Then why are you shaking?”

She didn’t have time to answer.

A shadow fell over them, and the jeers of the surrounding warriors cut through the tension like a blade. Nesta turned to see Bellius and his cronies sauntering toward them, their grins sharp as knives.

“Look at the little city mouse,” Bellius sneered, his gaze raking over her. “Still too pretty to get her hands dirty.”

Nesta’s fingers curled into fists. “Walk away.”

Cassian stepped between them, his wings flaring. “Enough.”

But Nesta shoved him aside. “I said I don’t need your protection.”

Bellius laughed, the sound grating. “Oh, we’re going to have fun breaking you.”

She didn’t think. Didn’t breathe.

Her fist connected with his jaw before she even registered the movement. Bone cracked against bone, and Bellius staggered back, his eyes wide with shock. The crowd roared, but Nesta barely heard them. Her hand was on fire, her knuckles splitting open as blood welled to the surface.

Cassian’s grip closed around her arm like a vise. “Move,” he snarled, dragging her away from the ring before the other warriors could react.

She wrenched free, but he didn’t let go. His wings beat the air as he hauled her toward her hut, his breath ragged with fury. The door slammed shut behind them, the sound echoing through the stillness.

Nesta stood in the center of the room, her chest heaving. The first drops of blood from her split knuckles splattered onto the stone floor, dark as rubies in the dim light.

---

The blood had dried to a rusted crust along her knuckles by the time she reached her hut. Nesta flexed her fingers, watching the flakes crack and fall like brittle leaves. The pain was good. It was something.

She had left the training grounds without a word, ignoring the stares of the Illyrian warriors as she stalked past. Let them whisper. Let them wonder. She didn’t care.

The hut was cold, the fire long dead. She didn’t bother to light it. Instead, she sank onto the edge of the narrow bed, her gray gown pooling around her like storm clouds. The fabric was still immaculate, untouched by the dirt and sweat of the day. She had scrubbed her hands raw before leaving Velaris, as if she could wash away the past months as easily as grime.

Her reflection in the small, cracked mirror above the washbasin was a stranger’s. Sharp cheekbones, hollowed cheeks, eyes that had once been warm and now held nothing but frost. She bared her teeth at the glass, watching the way her lips peeled back, feral.

A knock at the door.

Nesta didn’t move. She knew that knock—hesitant, almost apologetic. Cassian. Of course it was Cassian. She had told him to leave her alone, had thrown his concern back in his face like a weapon, and still, he persisted.

The knock came again, softer this time.

She exhaled through her nose, a sound like a snarl. “Go away.”

Silence. Then, the creak of the door opening anyway.

But it wasn’t Cassian who stood in the doorway.

A young Illyrian warrior hovered there, his broad shoulders nearly filling the frame. His dark hair was cropped short, his face still boyish despite the scars crisscrossing his arms. He held a small jar in his hands, turning it over and over like it might save him from whatever fate awaited him in this hut.

Nesta’s fingers curled into the edge of the bed. “What do you want?”

The warrior—Kars, she remembered his name from the training grounds—flinched. “I—I saw your hands.” He lifted the jar slightly, as if that explained everything. “I brought salve.”

She said nothing.

Kars swallowed hard. “I’m sorry. For earlier. I didn’t mean to—” He cut himself off, his throat bobbing. “I just wanted to help.”

Nesta stared at him. The silence stretched, taut as a bowstring.

Finally, she jerked her chin toward the small table near the door. “Leave it there.”

Kars hesitated, then stepped inside, placing the jar on the table with exaggerated care. He lingered for a moment, his gaze flicking over the sparse room—her neatly made bed, the single chair, the absence of any personal touches. As if he were trying to memorize her through the things she didn’t have.

“You’re not like them,” he said suddenly.

Nesta’s head snapped up. “Like who?”

“The other females.” His voice was quiet, but steady. “The ones who come here. They’re… different.”

“Different how?”

Kars shrugged, his shoulders lifting and falling like he was carrying a weight he couldn’t name. “Softer. They don’t fight like you do.”

Nesta let out a sharp, humorless laugh. “Is that a compliment?”

“No.” He met her eyes, unflinching. “It’s just the truth. You’re not like anyone.”

Something in her chest tightened. She looked away, focusing on the jar of salve. The glass was warm, as if it had been clutched in his hands for a long time.

Before she could respond, Kars stiffened. His head turned toward the window, listening. Then, without a word, he bolted for the back door, slipping out into the gathering dusk as silently as a shadow.

Nesta didn’t move. Didn’t breathe.

Footsteps approached the front of the hut, heavy and familiar. Cassian’s voice cut through the stillness. “Nesta.”

She closed her fingers around the jar of salve, the glass smooth and solid against her palm.

For the first time since arriving, the hollowness inside her flickered—not with rage, but with something dangerously close to curiosity.
