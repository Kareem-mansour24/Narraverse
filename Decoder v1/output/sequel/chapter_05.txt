CHAPTER 5
The Weight of the Sky

The hut was too quiet.

Nesta lay on the thin pallet, staring at the rough-hewn beams of the ceiling, counting the knots in the wood like stars in a sky she no longer recognized. Her ribs throbbed with every breath, a dull, insistent ache that had kept her awake for hours. The bruise along her jaw pulsed in time with her heartbeat, a reminder of yesterday’s mock battle—and the warriors’ delight in landing that final blow.

She didn’t move. Not yet.

Outside, the first hints of dawn crept through the cracks in the shutters, painting the dirt floor in pale gray light. The training grounds would already be stirring. The warriors would be stretching, sharpening their blades, laughing in that low, rough way of theirs. She could picture Devlon among them, his scarred face twisted in that smug grin he wore whenever he spoke to her.

*Nesta.*

The name slithered through her mind, a whisper she couldn’t silence. She clenched her teeth, the sound of her own name grating like gravel underfoot.

She sat up slowly, her muscles protesting. The gown she’d worn yesterday—gray, immaculate, a mockery of the filth around her—was draped over the single chair in the corner. She didn’t remember taking it off. Didn’t remember much of anything after the fight, after the way they’d circled her like wolves scenting blood.

Her fingers trembled as she reached for the dress, but she forced them still. She would not let them see her shake.

The water in the basin was cold, the cloth rough against her skin as she scrubbed away the sweat and grime of the previous day. The bruises bloomed like dark flowers across her ribs, her arms, her thighs. She traced them with her fingertips, not out of vanity, but out of something colder. A tally. A reminder.

*This is what they think of you.*

She braided her hair with practiced efficiency, twisting the dark strands into a coronet that sat like a crown of thorns against her scalp. The mirror above the basin was small, cracked, but it showed her enough. The hollows beneath her cheekbones were sharper now, her collarbones jutting like blades beneath her skin. She looked like a ghost of herself.

Or perhaps this was who she’d always been.

The hut’s door creaked as she pushed it open, the morning air biting against her skin. The training grounds were alive with movement, warriors sparring in pairs, their grunts and the clash of steel filling the air. Devlon stood at the center, his arms crossed over his chest, his voice carrying over the din.

“Today,” he announced, “we test your endurance. The cliffs. Weighted packs. First to the top earns the right to sit out tomorrow’s drills.”

A murmur rippled through the crowd. Nesta’s stomach twisted.

She knew what this was. Another game. Another way to break her.

She didn’t move as the warriors began strapping on their packs, the leather creaking under the weight of stones and iron. Devlon’s gaze flicked to her, his lips curling.

“Problem, Archeron?”

She said nothing. She wouldn’t give him the satisfaction.

One of the warriors—a hulking male with a broken nose—tossed a pack at her feet. It landed with a thud, sending up a cloud of dust. “Don’t slow us down,” he sneered.

She bent to pick it up, her muscles screaming in protest. The weight settled onto her shoulders like an anchor, pressing her down, down, down. The straps dug into her collarbones, the stones inside shifting with every breath.

Devlon clapped his hands. “Move.”

The warriors surged forward, their boots pounding against the earth as they made for the cliffs. Nesta followed, her steps slow, deliberate. She would not run. She would not give them the satisfaction of seeing her struggle.

The base of the cliffs loomed ahead, jagged and unforgiving. The warriors were already scaling the rock face, their fingers finding purchase in the cracks, their muscles straining against the weight of their packs. Nesta tilted her head back, her breath curling in the cold air.

This wasn’t just a test of strength.

It was a test of will.

And she would not break.

---

The wind howled through the Illyrian peaks, biting at Nesta’s exposed skin like a thousand tiny knives. She ignored it, her fingers digging into the frozen rock as she hauled herself up another inch. The pack on her back was a leaden weight, dragging her down with every movement, but she refused to acknowledge the burn in her arms, the scream of her thighs. She had spent months feeling nothing—this was nothing new.

Above her, Bellius and his friends scaled the cliff face with infuriating ease, their laughter ringing off the stone. “Lost your footing, princess?” one of them called, his voice dripping with mock concern. “Maybe you should’ve stayed in your pretty gowns.”

Nesta didn’t answer. She didn’t have the breath to waste. Her fingers slipped on a patch of ice, and for a heartbeat, she was weightless, her stomach lurching as the world tilted beneath her. Then her grip found purchase again, her nails scraping against the rock as she hauled herself back onto the ledge. Her pulse roared in her ears, but she didn’t look down.

She wouldn’t give them the satisfaction.

Then she felt it—the weight of a gaze. Cassian stood below, his wings folded tight against his back, his arms crossed over his chest. His face was unreadable, but his presence was a challenge in itself. *Prove them wrong.*

Nesta bared her teeth. She didn’t need his silent judgment. She didn’t need his help.

Bellius’ voice cut through the wind again. “What’s the matter, Archeron? Afraid of heights?” A chorus of laughter followed.

Nesta’s fingers curled into the rock, her knuckles white. She forced herself upward, her muscles trembling with the effort. The pack shifted, throwing her off balance, and her foot slipped. For a single, terrifying moment, she was dangling by one hand, the world spinning beneath her.

Then she found a foothold. She hauled herself up, her breath ragged, her body screaming in protest.

Below her, Cassian’s wings twitched, as if he were fighting the urge to launch himself into the air and catch her. She met his gaze, her own defiant, and mouthed a single word.

*No.*

---

The wind howled across the cliffs, biting at Nesta’s cheeks like a thousand tiny teeth. She ignored it, ignored the way her lungs burned, the way her fingers trembled against the cold, jagged rock. The halfway point. She had made it this far.

She pressed her forehead against the stone, the rough surface grounding her in a way nothing else had in months. For a heartbeat, she allowed herself to feel it—the exhaustion, the fear, the weight of every decision that had led her here. To this mountain, to this moment, to this hollow shell of herself.

Below, the Illyrian warriors watched. Some with disdain, others with something like curiosity. And then there was Kars, his dark eyes fixed on her, his expression unreadable. She could feel his gaze like a brand.

“You’re doing well,” he called up, his voice carrying on the wind. “Just a little farther.”

She didn’t answer. Didn’t even look at him. She didn’t need his encouragement, his pity. She didn’t need anyone’s.

Nesta opened her eyes, her vision swimming for a moment before she blinked it clear. The sky above was a vast, endless gray, heavy with the promise of snow. She exhaled sharply, her breath curling in the air before dissipating. Then she reached up, her fingers searching for the next handhold.

The rock was slick with frost, the wind tugging at her braided coronet, trying to pull her loose. She gritted her teeth and pulled herself higher, her muscles screaming in protest. She had lost weight since coming here, her body leaner, harder. The gray gown she wore—immaculate despite the filth of the training grounds—clung to her like a second skin, the fabric damp with sweat.

She didn’t know why she bothered to keep it clean. Maybe it was the last shred of defiance, the last thing she could control. Or maybe it was just habit, a remnant of the life she had left behind.

Another gust of wind tore at her, and her foot slipped on a loose stone. Her heart lurched as she scrambled for purchase, her fingers digging into the rock until they ached. She caught herself just in time, her breath coming in ragged gasps.

Below, Kars took a step forward, his hand outstretched as if he could somehow catch her from this distance. “Nesta—”

She turned her head, her eyes locking onto his. Her voice was sharp, cutting through the wind like a blade. “I don’t need your help.”

Kars stilled, his expression unreadable. Then he nodded, once, and stepped back.

Nesta turned away, her jaw set. She didn’t look down again. Didn’t acknowledge the way her hands shook, the way her legs burned. She just climbed.

One handhold at a time. One breath after another.

She would make it to the top. Not for them. Not for anyone.

For herself.

---

The wind howled across the summit, biting into Nesta’s skin like a thousand needles. Her fingers trembled—not from the cold, but from the sheer, brutal effort of hauling herself up the last stretch of rock. The weighted pack had felt like an anchor around her shoulders, dragging her down with every step, every lunge. But she had done it.

She had *won*.

The moment her boots hit solid ground, the world seemed to still. The warriors who had already reached the top turned, their murmurs dying in their throats. Bellius stood among them, his arms crossed over his chest, his usual sneer twisting his lips. But there was something different in his eyes today—something almost like respect, though he’d sooner die than admit it.

“Lucky,” he muttered, but the word lacked its usual venom.

Nesta didn’t look at him. She didn’t need to. Instead, she reached for the straps of her pack, her fingers fumbling with the buckles. The moment they gave way, she let the weight drop to the ground with a heavy *thud*, the sound echoing across the cliffs like a challenge.

Then she heard it—the rush of wind, the beat of wings.

Cassian landed beside her, his boots kicking up dust as he touched down. His wings flared wide, catching the sunlight, the crimson Siphons on his knuckles glinting like fresh blood. He didn’t speak. He didn’t need to.

His eyes were on her, dark and unreadable, and for once, Nesta didn’t look away.

She met his gaze, her chin lifting just a fraction. “I did it.”

His lips twitched, the barest hint of a smile. “You did.”

The words were simple, but they settled into her bones like a promise. She had expected mockery, or at least some smug remark about how she’d finally proven herself. But there was none of that. Just quiet pride, as if he had known all along that she would make it.

Nesta turned away before he could see the way her breath hitched. The mountains stretched out before her, vast and jagged, their peaks piercing the sky. The wind whipped through her hair, tugging strands loose from her braided coronet, but she didn’t care. For the first time in months, she didn’t feel hollow.

She felt *alive*.

---

The wind howled through the Illyrian peaks, biting at Nesta’s cheeks as she descended the cliffside. Below, the training grounds sprawled like a scar upon the earth, the warriors’ huts little more than dark smudges against the snow. She moved carefully, her muscles screaming in protest with every step. The descent was slower now, the others ahead of her dragging their feet, their exhaustion as palpable as the cold.

She didn’t look at them. Didn’t acknowledge their labored breaths or the way their wings sagged. She was no better, her own body heavy with fatigue, but she refused to let it show.

A patch of ice glinted beneath her boot.

Her foot slipped.

For one heart-stopping moment, she was falling, the world tilling sideways—

A hand shot out, gripping her arm with bruising force. She steadied, her pulse roaring in her ears, and looked up.

Kars.

His dark eyes were unreadable, his grip firm but not unkind. *Careful,* he said quietly.

Nesta yanked her arm free, her voice a blade. *I don’t need your help.*

Kars didn’t flinch. *I know.*

She expected him to sneer, to mock her. But he only stepped back, his gaze lingering on her, thoughtful. As if she were a puzzle he couldn’t quite solve.

Nesta turned away, her jaw clenched, and continued her descent. The snow crunched beneath her boots, the sound too loud in the silence between them.

Why had he helped her?

The question gnawed at her, unwanted. She didn’t want his kindness. Didn’t want to wonder if it was pity or something else. She didn’t want to think about the way his fingers had felt against her skin, warm despite the cold.

She reached the bottom of the cliff, her legs trembling. The others were already dispersing, their wings rustling as they trudged toward their huts. She didn’t follow. Instead, she looked back up.

Kars was still watching her.

She turned away without a word, but the weight of his gaze lingered, heavy as the sky above.

---

The hut was a tomb of silence, the only sound the ragged drag of her breath as she stumbled through the door. Nesta’s body screamed—every muscle, every bone, every inch of skin that had been scraped raw against the unforgiving earth. She didn’t bother to light the lantern. The dark was better. The dark didn’t demand anything of her.

She collapsed onto the pallet, her limbs trembling like a newborn fawn’s. The coarse wool of the blanket scratched against her cheek, but she didn’t care. She just needed to lie there. To not move. To not think.

The door creaked open.

Nesta didn’t lift her head. She knew who it was by the way the air shifted—by the way her traitorous pulse quickened at the scent of leather and wind and something faintly smoky, like embers left to cool.

Cassian’s boots thudded against the dirt floor. A bowl clinked onto the wooden table. “Eat.”

She didn’t look at him. “I’m not hungry.”

A lie. Her stomach twisted, gnawing at itself. But she’d be damned if she let him see it.

The bowl scraped against the table again, closer this time. “Eat anyway.”

Nesta bared her teeth. “Go away.”

A pause. Then the rustle of wings as he crouched beside her, the heat of him seeping into her side. “You’re shaking.”

“I’m fine.”

“You’re not.” His voice was low, rough. “You pushed yourself too hard today.”

She finally turned her head, glaring at him through the dim light. “What do you care?”

Cassian didn’t flinch. His dark eyes held hers, unyielding. “Because someone has to.”

Her stomach growled then, loud and obnoxious, betraying her. Cassian’s mouth twitched—just for a second—before he schooled his expression into something neutral. “Thought so.”

Nesta snatched the bowl from the table, her fingers curling around the warm ceramic. The stew was thick, rich with chunks of meat and vegetables, the scent of thyme and garlic rising in a cloud that made her mouth water. She took a bite, then another, barely chewing before swallowing.

Cassian watched her, his wings brushing against her leg as he settled onto the edge of the pallet. The contact sent a jolt through her, sharp and unwelcome.

“You did well today,” he said.

Nesta’s spoon paused halfway to her mouth. “Don’t patronize me.”

“I’m not.” His voice was quiet, but there was steel beneath it. “I mean it.”

She looked at him then, really looked at him. His face was shadowed, the sharp angles of his cheekbones and jaw more pronounced in the dim light. But his eyes—his eyes were different. There was no pity there. No frustration. Just… something else. Something that made her chest tighten.

Respect.

The word settled between them, heavy and undeniable.

Nesta set the spoon down, her appetite suddenly gone. “Why are you doing this?”

Cassian didn’t answer right away. He just stared at her, his gaze searching her face as if he were memorizing it. Then he stood, his wings rustling like dry leaves in the wind. “Because someone has to.”

The door clicked shut behind him, leaving her alone in the dark.

Nesta lay back on the pallet, her mind racing. The stew sat heavy in her stomach, but it was nothing compared to the weight of his words.

For the first time, she wondered if Cassian was more than just her jailer.

If he might be her ally.
