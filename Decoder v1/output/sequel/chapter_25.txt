CHAPTER 25
The Storm Before Dawn

The world was still dark when Nesta woke, the kind of darkness that clung like damp wool, thick and suffocating. She lay there for a heartbeat, listening to the distant murmur of warriors—low voices, the scrape of metal, the occasional cough. The camp never slept, not truly. Even in the dead of night, there was always someone awake, always someone watching.

Her body ached. Not just from the training, but from the night before—the fight, the blood, the way her muscles had screamed in protest as she’d driven her blade into flesh. She sat up slowly, her bones stiff, her skin prickling with the cold. The tent flap rustled in the wind, and she could see the faintest hint of gray light creeping over the mountains.

She dressed quickly, her fingers numb as she laced up her boots. The gray gown she wore was immaculate, as always, though the hem was now stained with dirt and something darker. She didn’t bother to brush it off. What was the point?

Outside, the air was sharp, biting. The training grounds were nearly empty, just a handful of warriors moving through their forms, their breaths misting in the cold. But Kars was already there, his broad back hunched over a whetstone, his blades laid out before him like offerings.

He didn’t look up as she approached. “You’re awake early.”

“Couldn’t sleep.” She crossed her arms, her gaze flicking to the northern cliffs. “Any news?”

Kars exhaled through his nose, the sound almost a growl. “Bellius’s lot was spotted near the ridge. More of them than yesterday.”

Nesta’s fingers curled into fists. “And?”

“And nothing.” He finally looked at her, his dark eyes grim. “Devlon’s waiting.”

“For what?”

“For Cassian to make the first move.” Kars spat onto the ground. “He won’t act until the general gives the order.”

Nesta bared her teeth. “So we’re just supposed to sit here and wait for them to slit our throats?”

Kars didn’t answer. He didn’t have to.

She turned away, her pulse hammering in her ears. The wind howled through the peaks, carrying with it the scent of snow and pine. She could see the northern cliffs from here, jagged and dark against the pale sky. Somewhere beyond them, Bellius was gathering his forces. Waiting.

Waiting for them to make a mistake.

Her fingers twitched at her sides. She could feel the weight of the blade at her hip, the familiar press of the hilt against her palm.

Kars watched her, his expression unreadable. “What are you going to do?”

Nesta didn’t answer. She didn’t need to.

Her fingers curled around the hilt of her blade.

“Then we don’t wait either.”

---

The door to Cassian’s quarters was ajar when Nesta reached it, the dim glow of a single lantern spilling into the narrow hallway. She didn’t bother knocking. The wood groaned as she shoved it open, the sound sharp in the quiet of the mountain night.

Cassian stood by the small table, his wings half-furled, the crimson of his Siphons pulsing faintly in the lamplight. He didn’t turn at the intrusion, his broad shoulders tense beneath the worn leather of his flying gear. “If you’re here to lecture me about Devlon, save your breath.”

Nesta stepped inside, the door clicking shut behind her. The room smelled of pine and steel, of the oil he used to care for his weapons. “You’re letting them call the shots.” Her voice was low, controlled, but the edge in it could have cut glass. “You’re letting Devlon dictate the terms.”

Cassian finally turned, his dark eyes flashing. “This isn’t about what I want, Nesta.” His wings rustled, the sound like the whisper of a blade being drawn. “This is about keeping this camp from tearing itself apart.”

“Then stop treating me like I’m the problem.” The words snapped out of her, quick and vicious. Her fingers curled into fists at her sides, nails biting into her palms. “I’m not some fragile thing you need to protect. I’m not some obstacle you have to work around.”

Cassian’s jaw tightened. “You want to be part of this?” He took a step toward her, the floorboards creaking beneath his weight. “Fine. But you don’t get to question my choices. Not when you won’t even follow the simplest orders.”

Nesta bared her teeth. “Orders?” The word tasted like ash. “You think I’m here to take orders from you?”

“No.” His voice dropped, rough and low. “I think you’re here because you don’t know what else to do with yourself. And that’s fine. But if you’re going to stand in this camp, you’re going to do it on my terms.”

The air between them crackled, charged with something raw and unspoken. Nesta could feel the heat of him, the barely leashed fury rolling off his body in waves. She didn’t back down. “Or what?”

Cassian’s Siphons flared crimson, the light casting shadows across the sharp planes of his face. “Or you can walk away.” He turned toward the window, his wings shifting with the movement. “Be ready at dawn. Or don’t.” His voice was a growl, final. “But if you’re not there, I won’t come looking for you.”

---

The wind howled across the northern cliffs, biting through the thin wool of Nesta’s gown as if it were nothing. She barely felt it. The cold had long since settled into her bones, a numbness that matched the hollow space inside her chest. Below, the Illyrian camp sprawled like a wounded beast, its fires guttering in the predawn dark. She and Kars had been crouched in the shadows for what felt like hours, watching, waiting.

Kars shifted beside her, his wings rustling against the rocky outcrop. His breath came in shallow puffs, visible in the frigid air. “There,” he murmured, nodding toward a cluster of figures gathered near the base of the cliff. Bellius stood at their center, his broad frame casting a long shadow in the flickering torchlight.

Nesta’s fingers curled into the damp earth, her nails biting into her palms. She should have felt something—rage, fear, anything. But there was only the same emptiness, the same weightless detachment that had followed her from Velaris. Even the sight of Bellius, the male who had made her life in the camp a living hell, failed to stir more than a flicker of recognition.

Then she heard the words.

“—burn it to the ground,” Bellius was saying, his voice low and rough. “Every tent, every supply cache. Let them wake to nothing but ash.”

Nesta’s breath hitched. Beside her, Kars stiffened, his hand flying to the dagger at his belt.

“Not just an attack,” Bellius continued, his followers murmuring in agreement. “A purge. By the time Cassian realizes what’s happening, it’ll be too late.”

Kars’s voice was a whisper, urgent. “They’re not just planning an attack. They’re planning to burn the camp to the ground.”

Nesta’s gaze snapped to him, her pulse roaring in her ears. For the first time in months, something hot and fierce uncoiled in her chest. Not fear. Not emptiness.

Determination.

Her voice was steady, cold as the wind. “Then we stop them.”

Kars’s dark eyes locked onto hers, his jaw tightening. “We’ll need more than just us.”

Nesta didn’t hesitate. She turned her gaze toward the shadows beyond the cliffs, where the first hints of dawn were beginning to bleed into the sky. “Then we find them.”

Kars’s hand tightened on his blade, his wings shifting with restless energy. The wind carried the scent of smoke and snow, the promise of violence.

And for the first time in a long time, Nesta Archeron did not look away.

---

The camp was a graveyard of embers and silence when Nesta returned, the warriors who had watched the night’s battle now huddled in small, wary clusters. They fell quiet as she approached, their eyes tracking her like prey assessing a predator. She didn’t slow her stride. Didn’t soften her expression.

The gray gown she wore was immaculate, as if the blood and dirt of the world couldn’t touch her. It only made her more alien among them—this female who moved like a storm given form, her braided coronet catching the faint light of the dying fires.

She stopped before the nearest group, four males, their wings tucked tight. One of them, Kars, she recognized. His jaw was set, his dark eyes flickering with something like calculation.

Nesta didn’t waste breath on pleasantries. “Bellius is planning to destroy this camp.” Her voice was low, rough with disuse. “He’ll burn it to the ground before the week is out. And if you don’t want to be in the ashes, you’ll listen to me.”

Silence. The wind howled between them, carrying the scent of snow and iron.

Then Kars exhaled, sharp and humorless. “And why should we believe you?”

Nesta bared her teeth. “Because I was there last night. Because I saw the way he looked at you—like you were already dead.” Her fingers curled into invisible claws. “Because I don’t give a damn if you live or die, but if you want to survive, you’ll fight with me.”

The warriors exchanged glances. One of them, younger than the others, shifted on his feet. “Bellius is our commander. He’s—”

“He’s a coward,” Nesta snapped. “And he’s weak. He’ll throw you into the fire before he risks his own skin.” She stepped closer, close enough that they could see the rage simmering in her eyes. “I’m not asking for your loyalty. I’m telling you how to live.”

Kars studied her for a long moment. Then, slowly, he nodded. “We’re with you.”

The others followed, murmurs of assent rippling through them. Nesta didn’t smile. Didn’t relax.

“Be ready at dawn,” she said, her voice a command. “And don’t let them see you coming.”

---

The wind howled through the jagged peaks of the Illyrian Mountains, biting at Nesta’s exposed skin like a thousand tiny knives. She ignored it, her fingers working deftly as she secured the last of the tripwires between the boulders. The gray gown she wore—immaculate despite the grime of the camp—fluttered around her legs, the fabric whispering against the rock.

Kars crouched beside her, his broad shoulders hunched against the cold. "That’s the last of them," he muttered, wiping sweat from his brow despite the chill. His dark eyes scanned the terrain, the narrow pass below where the enemy would soon march. "They won’t know what hit them."

Nesta didn’t answer. Her gaze was fixed on the horizon, where the first hints of dawn painted the sky in streaks of violet and gold. The light caught the edges of the cliffs, turning the snow into glittering shards. She exhaled, her breath curling in the air.

A shadow shifted behind them.

She didn’t turn. Didn’t need to. She knew the weight of his presence, the way the air itself seemed to still when he was near. Cassian stepped forward, his boots crunching on the frost-laced stone. His wings were half-spread, the crimson Siphons on his knuckles glowing faintly in the dim light.

"You’re really doing this."

His voice was low, rough. She could hear the weight of it, the years of war and bloodshed tangled in those few words.

Nesta kept her eyes on the pass. "Someone has to."

A muscle feathered in his jaw. She could feel the heat of his stare, the way it burned into the side of her face. But she didn’t look at him. Couldn’t.

Then Devlon’s voice cut through the tension like a blade.

"This is treason, girl."

Nesta turned then, slow and deliberate. Devlon stood a few paces away, his arms crossed over his chest, his face twisted in disgust. The other warriors behind him shifted, their gazes flickering between her and their commander.

She met Devlon’s stare without flinching. "No." Her voice was steady, cold. "This is survival."

Devlon’s lip curled. "You don’t give the orders here."

Nesta tilted her head, her braided coronet glinting in the dawn light. "Don’t I?"

The words hung between them, sharp and unyielding. The warriors around Devlon stilled, their eyes widening slightly. Even Kars went rigid beside her.

Cassian moved then, stepping forward until he stood at her side. His wings spread wide, the Siphons on his knuckles flaring to life, casting crimson light across the snow. "Then we do this together."

Nesta finally looked at him.

His dark eyes were fierce, unrelenting. But there was something else there, too—something raw and aching. She swallowed, her throat suddenly tight.

"I was never going to let you do it alone."

---

The wind screamed through the Illyrian peaks, a living thing, hungry and wild. It tugged at the hem of Nesta’s gray gown, whipped strands of hair free from her braided coronet, but she didn’t move. Didn’t flinch. The blade in her hand was a cold, familiar weight, its edge catching the faintest glimmer of starlight.

Behind her, the camp was silent. No fires burned. No voices murmured. Only the soft rustle of leather, the whisper of steel against scabbard, as the warriors who had sworn themselves to her shifted in the shadows. Their breath was steady, their faces set. They had followed her here, not because they believed in her cause, but because they believed in *her*. Or perhaps because they had nothing left to lose.

Kars stepped beside her, his broad shoulders cutting a dark silhouette against the moonlit cliffs. His voice was low, barely audible over the wind. “We’re ready.”

Nesta didn’t look at him. Her gaze remained fixed on the northern cliffs, where the sky bled into the jagged teeth of the mountains. Somewhere beyond them, Bellius and his faction were moving. She could feel it in her bones, in the way the air hummed with tension, like the moment before a storm broke.

“Then we wait,” she said.

The words were simple. Flat. But there was something beneath them—something sharp and unyielding. Kars exhaled, a quiet sound of approval, and melted back into the darkness.

She had spent months feeling nothing. Hollow. A ghost haunting the edges of her own life. But now, as she stood on the precipice of something vast and terrible, she felt the first flicker of something else. Not fear. Not regret.

Anticipation.

The wind howled again, and this time, it carried with it the sound of wings. Distant. Faint. But unmistakable.

They were coming.

Nesta tightened her grip on the blade. The warriors around her stilled, their bodies coiled like springs. She could see their faces now, illuminated by the cold light of the stars—determined, resolute. They had chosen her. And she would not fail them.

The first shadows shifted on the cliffs above. Not the wind. Not the night.

Something else.

Nesta’s lips curled back from her teeth. “Dawn is coming.”

The words were a whisper, a promise. A warning.

And then the shadows moved again.

Someone was watching.

The storm was about to break.
