CHAPTER 30
The Things We Fight For

The world was a symphony of pain.

Nesta woke to the groan of wounded men, the crackle of dying fires, the distant wail of some warrior’s lament. Her body throbbed in time with the rhythm—each breath a knife, each movement a betrayal. She had not expected to wake at all.

She lay on the hard ground, her cheek pressed against the cold earth, her braided coronet half-undone. The gray gown she wore was no longer immaculate. Dirt streaked the fabric, and blood—some hers, some not—darkened the hem. She flexed her fingers, wincing as the cuts along her knuckles split open anew. The battle had been a blur of snarling faces and steel, of Cassian’s voice shouting her name, of the sickening crunch of bone beneath her fists.

She pushed herself up, her muscles screaming in protest. The camp was in chaos. Warriors limped between tents, their faces drawn with exhaustion. Some carried the dead, their bodies wrapped in tattered cloaks, their weapons still clutched in stiffened hands. Others sat in stunned silence, staring at nothing. A few tended to their wounds with grim efficiency, their movements practiced, as if they had done this a hundred times before.

Nesta’s stomach twisted. She had done this.

No—*he* had done this. Devlon. The coward.

She spat onto the ground, the taste of copper still thick on her tongue. Her blade lay beside her, its edge notched from use. She picked it up, wiping the blood onto her thigh before sheathing it with more force than necessary. The weight of it was familiar now, a comfort in the chaos.

“You’re alive.”

Nesta turned. Kars stood a few paces away, his face streaked with soot and blood, his dark eyes weary. He had been one of the few who had not flinched from her these past weeks. One of the few who had not treated her like a fragile thing.

“So are you,” she said, her voice rough.

Kars exhaled sharply, running a hand through his tangled hair. “Barely.” He jerked his chin toward the center of the camp, where the largest tent had once stood. Now, it was little more than a collapsed heap of canvas and broken poles. “Devlon’s gone.”

Nesta stilled. “Gone?”

“Fled in the night. Took a handful of his lackeys with him.” Kars’s mouth twisted. “Left the rest of us to clean up his mess.”

A cold fury settled in her chest. Of course he had. Cowardice ran in his veins like poison. She had known that the moment she had first laid eyes on him, had seen the way he flinched from her, the way he had sneered at her presence here.

“And the others?” she asked.

Kars’s gaze flicked over her shoulder, toward the cliffs where the fighting had been fiercest. “We lost twenty-three. Another thirty wounded, some of them bad.” He swallowed. “The healers are doing what they can, but…”

But they were Illyrians. They did not beg for mercy, even in death.

Nesta’s fingers curled into fists. Twenty-three. Twenty-three lives snuffed out because Devlon had been too proud, too stubborn, too *weak* to listen. Because he had seen her as a threat rather than an ally.

Because she had let him.

She had not wanted this. Had not asked for it. She had only wanted to survive, to carve out some semblance of control in a world that had taken everything from her. But the camp was leaderless now. And the warriors—these men who had fought beside her, who had bled beside her—they would look to someone.

They would look to *her*.

The realization settled over her like a shroud. She had spent months pushing everyone away, building walls so high and so thick that no one could reach her. And now, when she wanted nothing more than to disappear, they would demand that she stand at the center of it all.

Kars was watching her, his expression unreadable. “What do we do now?”

Nesta’s throat tightened. She had no answer. She had never been a leader. She had never wanted to be.

But the alternative was worse.

She turned away from him, her gaze sweeping over the camp—the wounded, the grieving, the lost. The Illyrians had fought for her, had followed her into battle. And now they were broken, scattered.

She exhaled sharply, her breath fogging in the cold morning air. “We bury our dead,” she said at last. “And then we decide who will lead us.”

Kars said nothing, but she felt the weight of his stare, the unspoken question hanging between them.

Nesta wiped her blade clean on her thigh, the blood smearing across the fabric. Her hands trembled.

The weight of leadership settled onto her shoulders like an unseen burden.

---

The war tent smelled of damp wool and iron. Nesta stood just inside the flap, the cold mountain wind biting at her cheeks, her fingers still trembling—not from the chill, but from the weight of the dagger now strapped to her thigh. The blood had dried, but she could still feel its sticky residue beneath her nails.

Cassian and Azriel were already there, the former leaning against the rough-hewn table at the center of the tent, his arms crossed over his chest. His crimson Siphons glinted in the dim light, and his dark eyes tracked her as she stepped forward. Azriel stood slightly apart, his shadows whispering against his neck like living things, his scarred fingers resting on the hilt of his dagger.

The senior warriors—Kars, Torin, and the others—turned toward her as one. Their expressions were unreadable, but their silence was heavy with expectation.

Nesta swallowed. She had no right to stand here. No right to speak. But the memory of Devlon’s betrayal still burned in her veins, and the image of the camp in chaos—vulnerable, exposed—gnawed at her.

She lifted her chin. “We’re sitting ducks here.”

The words hung in the air. Cassian’s gaze didn’t waver, but his jaw tightened.

Kars, a grizzled warrior with a scar bisecting his brow, crossed his arms. “And what would you have us do, girl?”

Girl. The word grated, but she didn’t flinch. “We fortify the perimeter. Double the watch. Send a message to Rhysand—we need reinforcements, and we need them now.”

Torin, his dark eyes sharp, nodded slowly. “And if the enemy moves before help arrives?”

Nesta’s fingers curled into fists. “Then we hold them off. We use the terrain—these cliffs are a natural barrier. We funnel them into the narrow passes where their numbers won’t matter.”

A murmur rippled through the warriors. Cassian pushed off the table, his wings shifting slightly, as if testing the air. “You’ve fought in battles before,” he said quietly. “But not like this.”

Nesta met his gaze. “No. But I’ve seen enough to know when a position is indefensible.”

Azriel’s voice cut through the tension, low and measured. “She’s right. We’re spread too thin.”

Kars exhaled sharply, then looked at the others. “She’s got a point.”

The warriors exchanged glances, then turned back to her. There was no mockery in their eyes now—only calculation, and something like respect.

Nesta’s pulse pounded in her ears. She hadn’t asked for this. Hadn’t wanted it. But the words kept coming, her voice steady despite the tremor in her hands. “We move the supplies into the caves. Set up lookouts at the highest points. And we prepare for an attack—because they *will* come.”

Silence. Then, one by one, the warriors nodded.

Cassian’s voice cut through the murmurs of agreement. “You’re not just leading them.” His eyes burned into hers. “You’re leading us.”

---

The wind howled across the cliffs, biting through the thin fabric of Nesta’s gown. She welcomed the sting of it, the way it numbed her skin, her bones, her thoughts. Anything to drown out the weight of Cassian’s words still ringing in her ears.

*You’re leading us.*

As if she could lead anyone. As if she hadn’t spent the last months—no, the last years—dragging herself through the motions of living, a ghost haunting her own existence. The war camp sprawled below, a hive of activity, but up here, on the jagged edge of the world, there was only the wind and the vast, empty sky.

She didn’t hear him approach. Not until his wings rustled, the sound too familiar, too intimate. She stiffened but didn’t turn.

“You shouldn’t be up here alone,” Cassian said, his voice rough. The wind carried it to her, frayed at the edges.

Nesta didn’t answer. She didn’t owe him anything—not explanations, not apologies, not even the courtesy of acknowledgment. The horizon stretched before her, endless and indifferent, and she let it swallow her whole.

Silence settled between them, thick and suffocating. She could feel his gaze on her, heavy as a touch. It took everything in her not to flinch.

“You could have left,” he said finally, the words raw. “After the battle. After everything. You could have walked away.”

Her fingers curled into the fabric of her gown. She knew what he wasn’t saying—that she had walked away. From him, from Feyre, from the life they’d tried to build for her. She had let the hollow inside her swallow every attempt to reach her.

“So could you,” she said, her voice steady despite the storm inside her.

The words hung between them, fragile as glass. She didn’t turn to see his reaction. She didn’t need to. She could feel the weight of them, the truth of them, pressing down on her chest like a stone.

Cassian exhaled sharply, as if she’d struck him. “I didn’t want to.”

The admission was a blade to her ribs. She swallowed hard, her throat tight. “Neither did I.”

The wind howled again, whipping strands of hair loose from her braid. She didn’t bother to tuck them back. Let them tangle. Let them tear free. What did it matter?

Cassian shifted beside her, his boots crunching on the loose rock. She could sense the tension in him, the way his body coiled like a spring. “You’ve been pushing me away for months,” he said, quieter now. “And I let you. Because I thought—” His voice cracked. “I thought if I gave you space, you’d come back to me.”

Nesta closed her eyes. The wind stung her face, but it was nothing compared to the ache in her chest. “I don’t know how to come back,” she whispered.

The words were out before she could stop them. She hadn’t meant to say them. Hadn’t meant to let him see even that much. But there they were, hanging between them, raw and bleeding.

Cassian was silent for so long she thought he might not answer. Then, softly, “Neither do I.”

Her breath hitched. She turned her head just enough to see him from the corner of her eye. His jaw was clenched, his Siphons glowing faintly in the dim light. His wings were slightly spread, as if he were bracing himself against the wind—or against her.

“I don’t know how to do this without you,” he said, his voice barely audible over the howl of the wind. His hand hovered near hers, his fingers brushing the air between them, close enough that she could feel the heat of him, but not close enough to touch.

Nesta’s chest tightened. She wanted to reach for him. Wanted to close the distance between them and let his strength anchor her. But the hollow inside her yawned wider, a chasm she couldn’t cross.

Not yet. Maybe not ever.

---

The wind screamed through the Illyrian peaks, sharp as the blades Nesta had once wielded in the war. It tore at her braided coronet, tugging strands loose to whip across her face. She didn’t bother to brush them away. The cold had long since numbed her fingers, her skin, the hollow space behind her ribs where something vital had once lived.

Below, the war camp sprawled like a festering wound across the valley floor. Tents sagged under the weight of snow, fires guttered in the gale, and the distant shouts of warriors carried on the wind. She had stood here for hours, watching them train, watching Cassian move among them like a storm given form. Even from this height, she could pick him out—the broad shoulders, the crimson glow of his Siphons, the way his wings cut through the air as he demonstrated some maneuver.

She should have been down there with them. Should have been learning, fighting, doing something other than standing here like a statue carved from ice. But the thought of facing them—of facing him—made her stomach twist.

Footsteps crunched in the snow behind her. She didn’t need to turn to know who it was.

“You’re going to freeze to death up here,” Cassian said, his voice rough with the cold. His wings rustled as he came to stand beside her, close enough that she could feel the heat radiating off him, but not close enough to touch.

Nesta said nothing. The wind howled between them, filling the silence.

“Feyre’s worried about you,” he tried again. “She asked me to check on you.”

Nesta’s fingers curled into the fabric of her gown. “I don’t need checking on.”

“No,” he agreed quietly. “But maybe you need someone to stand with you while you figure out what you *do* need.”

The words struck too close to the truth. She turned her head sharply, baring her teeth. “I don’t need anything from you.”

Cassian didn’t flinch. His dark eyes held hers, steady and unyielding. “That’s not what your face says.”

Her breath hitched. “What’s that supposed to mean?”

“It means,” he said, stepping closer, “that you look like you’re one wrong word away from shattering. And I’m not going to let that happen.”

The wind tore between them, but neither moved. Nesta’s pulse pounded in her throat. She wanted to lash out, to push him away, to retreat back into the hollow safety of her anger. But the words died on her tongue.

Because he was right.

She was tired. So gods-damned tired of pretending she didn’t care, that nothing touched her, that she was fine. The mask had cracked the moment she’d stepped foot in these mountains, the moment she’d seen the way the warriors looked at her—not with pity, but with something worse. Respect. Expectation.

And Cassian—stubborn, infuriating Cassian—hadn’t treated her like she was broken. He’d treated her like she was still Nesta Archeron. Like she was still worth fighting for.

The realization stole her breath.

“I don’t know how to do this at all,” she whispered.

The admission tore from her like a blade, raw and bleeding. Her voice trembled, and she hated herself for it, hated the way her hands shook at her sides.

Cassian didn’t hesitate. He closed the distance between them in a single step, his wings flaring slightly as if to shield her from the wind. “Then we’ll figure it out together.”

The words settled over her like a promise. His gloved hand hovered near hers, not touching, but close enough that she could feel the heat of him through the leather.

Nesta’s breath caught.

She should pull away. Should retreat. Should do anything but stand here, trembling like a leaf in the storm.

But for the first time in months, she didn’t want to.

Slowly, so slowly she barely felt it, Cassian’s fingers brushed against hers. His grip was warm, steady, anchoring her to the earth when she felt like she might blow away.

The wind howled around them, but for the first time, neither pulled away.

---

The wind still clawed at the cliffs, but the bite of it had dulled. Or perhaps it was the heat of Cassian’s body beside hers, the way his arm had brushed against hers as they stood atop the rocky outcrop, that made the cold seem less sharp. Nesta didn’t move away.

Then the sky split open with the sound of wings.

Not the heavy, labored beats of the Illyrian warriors she’d come to recognize, but the swift, sure strokes of something far more powerful. Cassian’s spine straightened, his Siphons flaring crimson as he scanned the horizon.

Rhysand and Feyre landed in a gust of wind that sent pebbles skittering across the stone. The High Lord’s face was unreadable, but his eyes burned with something dark and urgent. Feyre’s braid had come loose in places, strands of auburn whipping around her face as she took in the scene—the smoldering remains of the camp below, the warriors still tending to the wounded, the way Nesta stood beside Cassian, not quite touching, but not apart.

Rhysand didn’t waste time. “We came as soon as we heard.” His voice was low, carrying the weight of command. “The rogue clan isn’t just a threat to the camp. They’re a threat to the entire Night Court.”

Nesta’s fingers curled into the fabric of her gown. Beside her, Cassian exhaled sharply, his wings shifting as if readying for flight.

Feyre stepped forward, her boots crunching on the frost-laced stone. She wore a heavy charcoal sweater, the sleeves pushed up to her elbows, and the silver-and-sapphire band on her left hand glinted in the pale light. Her gaze found Nesta’s, and for a heartbeat, neither of them looked away.

“You did this,” Feyre said, her voice quiet but steady. “You saved them.”

Nesta’s throat tightened. She had expected anger, or at the very least, the cold distance that had grown between them over the past months. But this—this was something else. Something like pride.

Cassian shifted beside her, but Nesta didn’t look at him. She held Feyre’s gaze, letting the words settle into her bones.

Then she spoke, her voice steadier than she felt. “We did this.” A pause. “And we’re not done yet.”

---

The war tent smelled of damp wool and old blood. Nesta stood near the back, arms crossed over her chest, the gray gown she wore immaculate despite the mud and filth of the camp. She had scrubbed it clean in the icy stream that morning, as if the act of washing away the grime could somehow scrub away the hollowness inside her. It hadn’t.

Rhysand leaned over the battered wooden table at the center of the tent, his fingers tracing the crude map of the mountains spread before him. Feyre stood at his side, her silver-and-sapphire band glinting in the dim light, her braid loose over one shoulder. Cassian and Azriel flanked them, their wings half-furled, shadows clinging to Azriel’s scarred fingers like living things.

“We can’t afford to wait,” Rhysand said, his voice low and measured. “If we give them time to regroup, they’ll only come back stronger. And next time, they won’t be content with raiding villages.”

Nesta’s stomach twisted. She had seen the aftermath of those raids—the burned homes, the bodies left in the snow. She had felt nothing then, just as she had felt nothing for months. But now, standing in this tent, listening to Rhysand outline the risks of inaction, something flickered in her chest. Not anger. Not fear. Purpose.

Cassian’s gaze flicked to her, as if he could sense the shift. His crimson Siphons glowed faintly against his dark leathers, the only warmth in the cold tent. She ignored him.

“What’s the plan, then?” Feyre asked, her voice steady. Nesta envied her sister’s composure, the way she stood so effortlessly at Rhysand’s side, as if she belonged there. As if she had always belonged there.

Rhysand exhaled, his breath curling in the frigid air. “We hit them before dawn. Catch them off guard. But we’ll need to move fast—split our forces, take the high ground.”

Nesta’s fingers curled into the fabric of her sleeves. The high ground. The fractured loyalty of the camp. The way the warriors had looked at her when she’d walked through the training grounds earlier, some with respect, others with open hostility. She had seen the cracks in their ranks, the way some of them had hesitated when the rogue clan’s leader had called for blood.

“Or,” she said, her voice cutting through the silence, “we let them think they’ve already won.”

Four pairs of eyes snapped to her.

Rhysand’s brows lifted. “Explain.”

Nesta stepped forward, her boots silent against the packed earth. “The camp is divided. Half of them want to follow the rogue clan, the other half are too afraid to speak up. If we attack now, we risk pushing the undecided into their arms.” She met Rhysand’s gaze, unflinching. “But if we make it seem like the rogue clan is turning on their own, we can fracture them from the inside.”

Cassian’s wings shifted, a slow, considering movement. “You’re suggesting we plant false information.”

“Not just information,” Nesta said. “Doubt. Fear. If we make them question whether their leader is actually looking out for them, they’ll turn on each other before we even lift a sword.”

Feyre’s lips parted, but no words came. Rhysand studied Nesta, his dark eyes unreadable. Then, slowly, he nodded. “It’s risky. If they see through the ruse—”

“They won’t,” Nesta said. “Because they’re already looking for a reason to doubt. We just give them one.”

Silence stretched between them, thick and heavy. Then Rhysand smiled, sharp and approving. “Then it’s settled.”

Cassian’s gaze burned into the side of her face, but she didn’t look at him. She didn’t need to. She could feel the weight of his pride, the way it settled over her like a mantle.

Azriel was the first to move, his shadows whispering as he turned toward the tent flap. “I’ll gather the spies.”

Feyre touched Rhysand’s arm, her fingers brushing the sleeve of his coat. “We should move quickly. Before word spreads.”

Rhysand nodded, then looked at Nesta. “You’ll lead the first wave.”

Nesta’s pulse jumped, but she didn’t let it show. “Fine.”

Cassian stepped forward, his wings rustling. “I’ll go with her.”

Nesta finally turned to him, her chin lifting. “I don’t need a babysitter.”

His mouth quirked, just slightly. “I know.”

She wanted to argue. Wanted to tell him she didn’t need him at all. But the words died on her tongue as she looked at the warriors gathering outside the tent, their faces set in grim determination. She wasn’t ready. She would never be ready.

But she would go anyway.

Cassian’s hand found hers, his grip firm, unyielding. “Ready?”

Nesta looked at him, then at the sea of Illyrians waiting beyond the tent. “No.” She exhaled, sharp and steady. “But let’s go anyway.”

---

The first light of dawn bled across the jagged peaks of the Illyrian Mountains, painting the war camp in hues of gold and crimson. Nesta stood at the edge of the cliff, the wind tugging at the braided coronet of her hair, her breath steady despite the storm raging inside her. Below, the rogue clan stirred, their jeers and laughter carrying on the frigid air. They thought themselves untouchable. They would learn otherwise.

Cassian landed beside her, his wings flaring wide before folding neatly against his back. His crimson Siphons glinted in the pale light, the only color in the muted world around them. "Last chance to back out," he murmured, though his voice held no expectation that she would.

Nesta didn’t look at him. "I don’t back out of fights."

A corner of his mouth lifted, but his eyes remained serious. "This isn’t just another fight, Nesta. These aren’t mindless soldiers. They’re desperate, and desperate men are dangerous."

She finally turned to him, her gaze unflinching. "So am I."

Cassian studied her for a long moment, then nodded once. "Then let’s remind them what that means."

The signal came—a sharp whistle from Azriel, who stood atop a nearby outcropping, his shadows coiling like serpents around his arms. Rhysand and Feyre were already in the air, their forms little more than dark smudges against the sky. Nesta didn’t wait for further instruction. She stepped off the cliff.

The wind screamed in her ears as she plummeted, her body instinctively shifting into the form Cassian had drilled into her over the past weeks. Her wings snapped open, catching the air, and she banked hard, arrowing toward the heart of the enemy camp. The rogue clan barely had time to register her before she was among them, her blade flashing.

Steel met steel in a cacophony of shrieks and grunts. Nesta moved like a storm, her body a weapon honed to lethal precision. She didn’t think. She didn’t hesitate. She simply *was*—a force of nature, unstoppable and unyielding. The rogue warriors fell back, their confidence wavering as she carved through their ranks. Beside her, Cassian fought with the same brutal efficiency, his own blade a blur of motion. They moved in sync, as if they had been fighting side by side for centuries rather than weeks.

A hulking warrior, his face twisted into a sneer, stepped into her path. His armor was dented and scarred, his greatsword notched from countless battles. "You think you’re one of us?" he spat, his voice a guttural growl. "You’re nothing."

Nesta didn’t flinch. She didn’t smile. She simply adjusted her grip on her sword, the weight of it familiar and comforting in her hands. "I don’t need to be one of you," she said, her voice colder than the mountain wind. "I just need to be better than you."

The warrior roared and lunged.

The fight was brutal. He was stronger, his reach longer, his blows heavy enough to rattle her bones. But Nesta was faster. She had spent weeks pushing her body to its limits, learning to endure pain, to move through it, to *use* it. She ducked beneath his swing, her blade slicing across his thigh. He snarled and retaliated, his fist connecting with her jaw. She tasted blood, but she didn’t stop. She couldn’t.

She feinted left, then drove her sword upward, burying it in his gut. His eyes widened, his breath coming in ragged gasps as he staggered back. Nesta didn’t give him time to recover. She twisted the blade, yanking it free in a spray of crimson, and kicked him to his knees. He collapsed onto his back, his hands clutching at the wound, his breath gurgling in his throat.

Nesta stood over him, her chest heaving, her body trembling not from fear but from the sheer *force* of the fight. The world around her had narrowed to this single moment—the rise and fall of her breath, the metallic tang of blood in the air, the weight of the sword in her hand.

The warrior’s lips curled into a bloody smile. "You’re still nothing," he rasped.

Nesta didn’t answer. She didn’t need to. The proof of her victory was written in the blood pooling beneath him, in the silence of his comrades as they watched their leader fall. She turned away, her gaze sweeping over the battlefield. The rogue clan was broken, their resistance crumbling. Rhysand and Feyre had landed, their weapons still drawn but their expressions grimly satisfied. Azriel stood at the edge of the camp, his shadows retreating like smoke on the wind.

And then there was Cassian.

He stood a few paces away, his sword lowered, his wings slightly drooped from exertion. His dark eyes met hers, and in them, she saw no triumph. No pride. Only quiet understanding.

"You were always enough," he said, his voice barely audible over the wind.

Nesta’s breath hitched. The words settled into her like a balm, soothing the raw, jagged edges of her soul. She wanted to argue. To tell him he was wrong. But the fight had stripped her of the energy for lies.

So she simply nodded, her fingers tightening around the hilt of her sword. And for the first time in months, she let herself believe it.

---

The camp was a graveyard of shattered tents and smoldering embers. The scent of blood and smoke clung to the air, thick enough to taste. Nesta stood amidst the wreckage, her body trembling—not from fear, but from the sheer weight of exhaustion pressing down on her bones. Her breath came in ragged gasps, her muscles screaming in protest with every movement. The sword at her side felt heavier than it had moments ago, as if the metal itself had absorbed the weight of the battle.

Around her, the warriors moved like ghosts. Some tended to the wounded, their hands steady despite the horror in their eyes. Others gathered the dead, their faces grim. A few glanced her way, their expressions unreadable. But there was no scorn in their gazes now. Only something quieter. Something like respect.

Kars stepped forward, his dark eyes scanning the destruction. His voice was low, rough from shouting orders. "What now?"

Nesta didn’t answer right away. Her gaze drifted past him, past the ruins of the camp, to the horizon where the first hints of dawn painted the sky in streaks of gold and pink. The wind howled through the cliffs, carrying with it the distant cry of wings. But this time, it wasn’t the sound of an approaching enemy. It was the sound of something else. Something like hope.

She exhaled slowly, her fingers flexing at her sides. "Now," she said, her voice steadier than she felt, "we rebuild."

Kars blinked, as if he hadn’t expected the answer. Then he nodded, once, sharply. "The supplies—"

"Will come," Nesta cut in. "We’ll send word to Velaris. To the Night Court." She turned slightly, her eyes finding Cassian where he stood near the edge of the camp, his wings slightly spread as if bracing against the wind. His Siphons glowed faintly, the crimson light catching the early morning sun. He was watching her, his expression unreadable, but there was something in the set of his jaw that made her chest tighten.

Torin, ever the pragmatist, stepped forward. "And the dead?"

Nesta swallowed hard. "We honor them. Properly." She didn’t elaborate. She didn’t need to. The warriors around her understood. They had all lost someone today. They had all bled.

A murmur of agreement rippled through the group. Kars clapped Torin on the shoulder, then turned back to Nesta. "We’ll start with the wounded. Then the dead."

Nesta nodded. "And the rest of us will clear the debris. Salvage what we can." She gestured to the ruins around them. "We’ll need wood. Stone. Anything that can be used again."

The warriors dispersed, their movements purposeful now. The weight of the task ahead settled over them, but there was no despair in it. Only determination.

Nesta turned away from the camp, walking toward the cliffs. The wind tugged at her braided coronet, pulling strands loose to whip around her face. She didn’t bother to fix them. She just stood there, staring out at the horizon, letting the cold air bite at her skin.

She didn’t hear Cassian approach, but she felt him. Felt the warmth of him before his hand found hers, his fingers intertwining with hers. She didn’t pull away.

The wind carried the sound of distant wings, but this time, it wasn’t a threat. It was a promise. Of what came next. Of the future they would build from the ashes.

Nesta squeezed his hand, just once. And for the first time in a long time, she let herself believe in it.
